# Node: Anthropology (The Doctrine of Humanity)

**Weight:** Theological anthropology determines everything downstream about sin, salvation, and ethics. The image of God (*imago Dei*) is the foundation of human dignity; its distortion by sin is the foundation of hamartiology; its restoration is the goal of redemption. Anthropology is also where theology intersects most directly with contemporary cultural debates: human identity, gender, the body, and what it means to be a person are all fought over in the present moment using categories that have deep theological roots.

**Connects to:** Sin, Christology, Creation, Sanctification, Eschatology, Resurrection, Sovereignty & Free Will, Vocation & Work, Social Ethics

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- Human beings are created in the image of God (*imago Dei*) — this is the defining characteristic of humanity that no other creature shares
- The *imago Dei* is retained after the fall — it is distorted and corrupted but not destroyed; this is the ground of human dignity even in fallen humanity
- Human beings are psychosomatic unities — body and soul/spirit belong together; the body is not a prison or a vehicle but constitutive of what it means to be human
- Human beings are created, not divine — the Creator/creature distinction is absolute; humans are not fragments of the divine, nor capable of becoming divine in the pantheistic sense
- Human beings are inherently relational — made for relationship with God, with each other, and with creation; autonomy is not the primary human good
- Human beings are morally responsible — they are genuine agents who act and are accountable; this is not diminished by the fall's corruption of the will
- Human beings have a destiny — redemption is not mere return to the pre-fall state but eschatological completion; humanity is moving toward something greater than Eden

## Key Tensions

### 1. The *Imago Dei* — What Is the Image of God?

Three primary interpretations have dominated the tradition, and each captures something genuine.

**Substantive/structural:** The image consists in some structural feature of human beings — most commonly rationality, moral consciousness, or the capacity for relationship. Augustine and Aquinas emphasized rationality; Karl Barth and more recently relational theologians emphasize the capacity for I-Thou relationship. The image is a property or set of properties that humans have and animals lack.

**Functional:** The image is not a property but a role — humans are God's representatives and stewards in creation, exercising dominion on his behalf (Gen 1:26–28). This reading connects *imago Dei* to the ancient Near Eastern practice of kings placing images (statues) of themselves in provinces they ruled — the image represents the authority of the ruler. Humanity is God's royal representative in creation. This reading is supported by the immediate context of Gen 1:26–28 where "image" and "dominion" are directly connected.

**Relational:** Karl Barth's reading — the image is found in the differentiation and unity of male and female (Gen 1:27 — "in the image of God he created them; male and female he created them"). The correspondence is to the inner-Trinitarian relations: as God exists in differentiated unity (three persons, one God), so humanity exists in differentiated unity (male and female, one humanity). The image is not something an individual has but something that is expressed in relationship.

**The fall's effect on each:** If the image is structural (rationality), the fall distorts it without destroying it. If the image is functional (dominion), the fall corrupts the way dominion is exercised (toward exploitation rather than stewardship). If the image is relational, the fall fractures the relationships that express the image (shame, blame-shifting, domination replacing partnership). Redemption restores what the fall damaged in each case.

### 2. Dichotomy vs. Trichotomy — Body and Soul, or Body, Soul, and Spirit?

**Dichotomy:** Human beings consist of two components — body (material) and soul/spirit (immaterial). "Soul" and "spirit" in Scripture refer to the same immaterial reality, used somewhat interchangeably. The fall affects both components; resurrection restores both. This is the dominant view in Reformed and most evangelical theology.

**Trichotomy:** Human beings consist of three components — body (material), soul (*psyche* — the psychological/emotional life), and spirit (*pneuma* — the faculty for communion with God). The spirit is the highest faculty, through which humans relate to God; it was most severely affected by the fall. 1 Thess 5:23 ("may your whole spirit, soul and body be preserved blameless") and Heb 4:12 ("dividing soul and spirit") are the key texts. Common in the Holiness and Charismatic traditions.

**The practical difference:** Trichotomy tends to produce a spirituality that elevates the spirit over soul and body — the spiritual is real religion; the psychological and physical are secondary. Dichotomy integrates the two-component view with a more holistic approach in which the body and its experiences are fully part of what it means to be human before God. The Hebrew anthropology underlying the OT is strongly holistic — *nephesh* (often translated "soul") refers to the whole living creature, not a separable spiritual component.

### 3. Human Sexuality and Gender — Creation Order and Its Meaning

Genesis 1 and 2 establish the pattern: humanity is created male and female, united in marriage, as the climax of the sixth day. The theological anthropology of gender has become the most culturally contested area of the discipline.

**The complementarian position:** Male and female are created with differentiated natures and complementary roles — equal in dignity and *imago Dei*, but ordered in their relationship. The husband's headship in marriage (Eph 5:22–33; 1 Cor 11:3) and the male-only eldership (1 Tim 2:12–3:7; Titus 1:5–9) reflect creation order, not cultural accommodation. This is not a result of the fall but the design prior to it.

**The egalitarian position:** The apparent hierarchical passages reflect the cultural situation of the early church and should be read in their redemptive-historical context — moving toward the Gal 3:28 vision ("neither male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus") in which the distinctions remain but the hierarchy does not. The headship language in Eph 5 is mutual submission (v. 21 — "submit to one another") applied to the specific situation; Paul's explicit argument for male leadership in 1 Tim 2 is bounded by its specific cultural context.

**The contemporary debate beyond complementarianism/egalitarianism:** Questions of transgender identity, gender dysphoria, and same-sex relationships press beyond the traditional egalitarian/complementarian frame into the more fundamental question of what the body's sex means for personal identity. The theological anthropological question: is the body constitutive of identity (sex is not separable from self), or is identity primarily a matter of inner experience that the body may or may not express? This question has deep implications for how the *imago Dei* relates to the sexed body.

### 4. The Intermediate State — What Happens Between Death and Resurrection?

**Soul sleep:** At death, the soul enters an unconscious state until the resurrection (Martin Luther held a version of this). The intermediate state is genuine rest — not active conscious experience.

**Conscious presence with Christ:** At death, the believer's soul passes immediately into conscious fellowship with Christ (Phil 1:23 — "to depart and be with Christ, which is better by far"; 2 Cor 5:8 — "to be away from the body is to be at home with the Lord"; Luke 23:43 — "today you will be with me in paradise"). This is the dominant evangelical position.

**The body-soul tension in the intermediate state:** Paul's language in 2 Cor 5 reveals the tension: he expects to be "at home with the Lord" between death and resurrection (v. 8), but he does not want to be "unclothed" (without a body, v. 4) — he wants to be "clothed" (resurrected). The intermediate state is real but incomplete; the resurrection body is the goal. Christian hope is not the survival of the soul but the resurrection of the body.

## Scripture Anchors

- **Gen 1:26–27** — "let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness, so that they may rule"; the *imago Dei* and dominion in direct connection; "male and female he created them"
- **Gen 2:7** — "the Lord God formed a man from the dust of the ground and breathed into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living being" — psychosomatic unity; body and breath-of-life together constitute the human person
- **Gen 2:18** — "it is not good for the man to be alone" — human sociality as constitutive, not supplementary; relational nature from creation
- **Ps 8:5–6** — "you have made them a little lower than the angels and crowned them with glory and honor" — dignity and royal status; *imago Dei* in poetic form
- **Col 3:10** — "the new self, which is being renewed in knowledge in the image of its Creator" — the *imago Dei* as the goal of sanctification; the image being restored
- **Phil 1:23** — "to depart and be with Christ, which is better by far" — conscious intermediate state
- **2 Cor 5:4** — "we do not wish to be unclothed but clothed instead, so that what is mortal may be swallowed up by life" — resurrection body as the goal, not soul survival alone
- **Gal 3:28** — "there is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus" — the unity in Christ; the egalitarian key text
- **Eph 5:22–33** — the household code; headship and submission in marriage as structured on the Christ/church relationship; the complementarian key text

## Theologian Map

**Augustine of Hippo (354–430)**
The Western tradition's fundamental anthropology. Augustine's reading of the *imago Dei* as residing primarily in the rational soul shapes the entire medieval tradition. His *De Trinitate* traces Trinitarian analogies in the structure of the human mind: memory, understanding, and will as a creaturely image of Father, Son, and Spirit. The fall, for Augustine, corrupts the will's orientation — it turns from God (the highest good) toward self and created goods — without destroying the rational structure that constitutes the image. Redemption is the reordering of love: *ordo amoris* — loving things in their proper order, with God as the supreme object of love.

**Karl Barth (1886–1968)**
His relational reading of the *imago Dei* is one of the most influential 20th century anthropological contributions. The image is not a property of the individual but the structure of I-Thou relationship that corresponds to the intra-Trinitarian relations. "Male and female" (Gen 1:27) is the creaturely analogy to the divine differentiation. This relational anthropology grounds human dignity in relationship rather than in individual properties — which has implications for how we think about human development (embryos), disability, and dementia.

**Phyllis Trible (20th century, OT scholar)**
Her *God and the Rhetoric of Sexuality* (1978) is the most careful literary reading of Genesis 1–2 from a feminist perspective within evangelical-adjacent scholarship. Her analysis of Gen 2 argues that the text does not establish male priority — *ha-adam* (the human/earth creature) is not "male" until after the creation of the woman; the naming of the woman (Gen 2:23) is an act of recognition rather than authority. Her work has been both adopted by egalitarians and contested by complementarians, but her close textual reading set a new standard for the exegetical debate.

**John Cooper (20th–21st century)**
*Body, Soul, and Life Everlasting* is the most careful evangelical treatment of the intermediate state and the body-soul question. Cooper defends holistic dualism — humans are genuinely one (against Platonic body-soul dualism) yet genuinely two components (against physicalism/materialism). The soul survives physical death in a conscious but incomplete state; the resurrection of the body is the completion of salvation. This is the most precise statement of the dominant evangelical position on the nature of the human person.

## Entry Points

- **"What makes humans different from animals?"** — the *imago Dei* entry; rationality, morality, relationship, and vocation all in play
- **"Does my body matter spiritually?"** — the psychosomatic unity question; against the gnostic instinct that the spiritual is real and the physical is secondary
- **"What happens when I die?"** — the intermediate state entry; the conscious presence with Christ; the resurrection as the fuller answer
- **"Is gender just a social construct?"** — the creation order entry; the theological anthropology of the sexed body
- **"Does God care about my mental health / emotional life?"** — the soul's full participation in the *imago Dei*; the whole person matters
- **Scholars:** The three *imago Dei* interpretations; Barth's relational reading; dichotomy vs. trichotomy; the intermediate state; theological anthropology and gender

## Failure Modes

- **Gnosticism** — treating the body as secondary or inferior to the soul; spirituality that ignores or devalues physical existence, embodiment, and materiality
- **Reductionism** — treating humans as merely biological animals; the secular reduction that misses the *imago Dei* dimension and its moral implications
- **Individualism** — treating the human person as essentially atomistic; missing the relational and communal nature that is constitutive of the *imago Dei*
- **Romanticization of Eden** — treating creation/pre-fall humanity as the goal of redemption; missing the eschatological direction; redemption is not mere restoration but completion
- **Angelism** — treating human spirituality as if the body were an obstacle to overcome rather than a constitutive dimension of what it means to be human; affects both ascetic traditions and disembodied spirituality
- **Identity-collapse** — reducing the human person to any single dimension: intellect alone (classical Augustinian tendency), experience alone (therapeutic culture), or biology alone (physicalism)
