# Node: Christology

**Weight:** The load-bearing node of the entire system. Everything else is downstream — the atonement's value depends on who is dying; the nature of union with Christ depends on what kind of being he is; the resurrection's power depends on whether the risen one is truly divine. Christology is also the place where the ancient heresies prove most durable: Arianism, Docetism, and Apollinarianism resurface constantly in popular theology, worship songs, and casual Christian thinking. Users in conservative contexts often hold a Christology that has functionally absorbed the humanity; users shaped by progressive or academic theology often hold one that has functionally dissolved the divinity.

**Connects to:** Trinity, Atonement, Resurrection, Anthropology, Grace, Justification, Ecclesiology, Eschatology, Heresy & False Teachers & the Great Apostasy

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- Jesus Christ is fully God — of the same substance (*homoousios*) as the Father; not a lesser divine being, not a created being, not an elevated human
- Jesus Christ is fully human — a real human being with a human body, human soul, human emotions, human growth, and genuine human limitation in his human nature
- The two natures are united in one person — the hypostatic union; the eternal Son of God subsisting in two natures simultaneously
- The union is without confusion, without change, without division, without separation (Chalcedon, 451) — the four negatives that bound the mystery without resolving it
- The subject of the incarnation is the eternal Son — the human nature does not pre-exist; the one who becomes human is divine from eternity
- The incarnation does not end at the resurrection — Christ remains permanently incarnate; the risen Son of God is embodied in a glorified human nature forever
- What is not assumed is not healed — the full humanity must be genuine for salvation to reach the full human person (Gregory of Nazianzus)

## Key Tensions

*These have occupied serious theologians for centuries.*

### 1. The Hypostatic Union — One Person, Two Natures

The Council of Chalcedon (451) defined the central Christian confession against a century of Christological controversy. One and the same Christ: fully divine, fully human, one person.

**The four Chalcedonian negatives are the essential content:**
- *Without confusion:* the two natures do not mix into a third thing (against Eutychianism); Christ is not a hybrid of divine and human but genuinely both
- *Without change:* neither nature is transformed into the other; the divine does not become human, the human does not become divine
- *Without division:* the person is not split into two subjects, two Christs
- *Without separation:* the natures are not two independent entities loosely associated; they belong to one person

**What Chalcedon does not do:** It does not explain *how* one person can have two complete natures without the attributes of one overwhelming the other. It draws boundaries around the mystery without resolving it. This is deliberate: the council's task was to rule out error, not to provide a metaphysical account of what it defined. The mechanics of the hypostatic union remain genuinely mysterious — affirmed, bounded, unexplained.

**The pastoral significance:** The Chalcedonian definition is not a philosophical curiosity; it guards the gospel at two points simultaneously. Against a collapse toward pure divinity: if Jesus is not genuinely human, he cannot represent humanity or die as a human being. Against a collapse toward pure humanity: if he is not genuinely divine, his death and resurrection have no salvific power beyond that of any other martyr.

### 2. The Kenosis Question — What Did the Son "Empty"?

Philippians 2:5–11: Christ "emptied himself" (*ekenosen*), "taking the form of a servant, being born in the likeness of men." The question is what the emptying involved. It is one of the most debated texts in Christology.

**The classical answer:** The Son emptied himself of the independent use or exercise of divine prerogatives — not of the attributes themselves. During the incarnation, the Son voluntarily operated within human limitations, but the divine nature was not diminished. The divine nature cannot lose omniscience or omnipotence without ceasing to be divine. What the Son laid aside was not divine attributes but the status and external glory that belongs to the divine existence — the "equality with God" he did not hold onto (v. 6).

**Kenotic theology (19th century, Gottfried Thomasius):** The Son actually divested himself of the relative (communicable) divine attributes — omniscience, omnipotence, omnipresence — while retaining the essential (incommunicable) attributes — holiness, love, and will. This is meant to take seriously Jesus's limited knowledge (Mark 13:32 — "not even the Son" knows the day or hour) without the awkwardness of one person who simultaneously knows and does not know.

**The problems kenotic theology creates:** A Christ who has genuinely relinquished omniscience is not fully divine during the incarnation — which reopens the Arian question. And the notion of "giving up" an attribute raises the deeper problem: divine attributes are not properties a person has and can set down; they are what it means to be God. The classical tradition answers: Jesus's limited knowledge was a genuine limitation of his *human* nature, which is a real and complete nature; the divine nature was not limited, but the person chose to operate through human limitation rather than continuously overriding it.

**The mystery that remains:** The classical answer requires that one person simultaneously know all things (divine nature) and not know the day or hour (human nature). This is not incoherent — the person's knowledge is expressed through whichever nature is engaged in a given act — but it presses the limits of what can be stated clearly. Chalcedon was right to draw boundaries rather than resolve it.

### 3. The Heresies and Why They Still Matter

The Christological heresies were not settled by the councils and then disappear from history. They resurface continuously — in popular theology, in bad worship lyrics, in progressive and conservative distortions alike. Naming them is not antiquarianism; it is diagnostic.

**Arianism:** Christ is the highest creature — the first and greatest of all created beings, through whom God made everything else, but himself created rather than eternal. The eternal Son is subordinate in being, not merely in role. This is the position of Jehovah's Witnesses and, functionally, of any theology in which Christ is "divine" in a qualitative but not ontological sense. The Nicene response (325): *homoousios* — the same substance as the Father, not merely similar. The pastoral consequence: if Christ is a created being, his death cannot bear infinite guilt; a finite sacrifice can address only finite debt.

**Docetism:** Christ only *appeared* to be human — the divine person took on the *appearance* (*dokein* = to seem) of flesh without genuinely entering it. This is the Gnostic instinct: matter is corrupt, and the divine cannot genuinely unite with it. 1 John's polemical edge — "every spirit that does not confess that Jesus Christ has come in the flesh is not from God" (4:2) — targets exactly this. The pastoral consequence: if Christ was not truly human, he cannot be humanity's representative; a phantom cannot die for real people.

**Nestorianism:** The divine and human natures in Christ are so distinct that they amount to two separate persons — the eternal Son loosely associated with the human Jesus. The Nestorian concern is legitimate: it seems to preserve the integrity of each nature. But the cost is that there is no genuine incarnation — God did not really become human, he allied himself with a human. The denial of *Theotokos* (God-bearer) for Mary is the diagnostic marker: if the one she bore is not the eternal Son but merely a human associated with the Son, she is only *Christotokos*. Cyril of Alexandria's response: the *one* subject of everything the incarnate Christ experiences is the eternal Logos.

**Eutychianism / Monophysitism:** The opposite error from Nestorianism — the two natures merge at the incarnation into a single nature, neither fully divine nor fully human. A "Christly nature" that is a mixture of both. This preserves the unity of the person at the cost of the integrity of each nature. The pastoral consequence: a Christ who is not fully human cannot represent us; a Christ who is not fully divine cannot save us.

**Apollinarianism:** Christ had a human body and perhaps soul but not a human rational spirit or will — the divine Logos replaced the human rational faculty. This was a well-intentioned attempt to solve the problem of how Christ could have two wills without being two persons. Gregory of Nazianzus's response is the permanent answer: "what is not assumed is not healed." The scope of salvation exactly tracks the scope of the incarnation. If Christ did not take on a human will, the human will is not redeemed. The complete human person — body, soul, will, rational mind — must be genuinely united to the Son for complete salvation to be possible.

**Where these heresies live today:** Docetism appears whenever Jesus is portrayed as God walking around in a human costume — when his hunger, weariness, limited knowledge, and emotional distress are treated as performances rather than genuine experiences. Arianism appears whenever Jesus is described as a "divine being" who is less than the Father, or in worship that treats the Spirit as a force rather than a person. Apollinarianism appears whenever the humanity of Jesus is acknowledged at the level of body but his emotional life and limited knowledge are quietly attributed to "the divine nature taking over."

### 4. Christology from Above vs. Christology from Below

A methodological question with significant theological and pastoral consequences.

**Christology from above** (classical): Begin with the Nicene/Chalcedonian definition — Jesus Christ is the eternal Son of God incarnate — and read the gospels through this established lens. The risk: the genuine humanity can be functionally absorbed. If you know in advance that Jesus is divine, every human moment gets quietly explained away — his anguish in Gethsemane is performance; his not-knowing is diplomatic concealment; his genuine development in wisdom (Luke 2:52) becomes theater. The result is a Christ who is God dressed in a human costume rather than genuinely human.

**Christology from below** (modern, post-Enlightenment): Begin with the historical figure — the human being who lived, taught, died, and was experienced as risen. Work up from the historical evidence toward claims about his identity. The risk: the divinity gets reduced to a quality of experience rather than an ontological claim. Jesus was "so surrendered to God" or "so fully human" that he was functionally divine; the resurrection becomes a transformative experience of his followers rather than a bodily event; "truly God" becomes a metaphor for deep spiritual reality. The full Nicene claim is quietly evacuated.

**The canonical answer:** Both movements are necessary, and the gospels model both. John's prologue begins from above: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was God... and the Word became flesh." Mark begins from below: the baptism, the temptation in the wilderness, the human ministry. Luke 2:52 — "Jesus grew in wisdom and stature" — is a from-below verse that a from-above Christology has difficulty with. John 1:1 is a from-above verse that a from-below Christology tends to soften into metaphor. A fully biblical Christology must account for both registers without privileging one.

## Scripture Anchors

*Key passages with a note on what they carry.*

- **John 1:1–18** — the Logos prologue; "in the beginning was the Word, and the Word was God"; "the Word became flesh and made his dwelling among us"; full divinity and full incarnation in one passage; the foundational Christological text
- **Phil 2:5–11** — the kenosis passage; pre-existence assumed ("being in very nature God"); self-emptying; genuine incarnation; exaltation; the full narrative arc of the Son in compressed form
- **Col 1:15–20** — "the image of the invisible God, the firstborn over all creation"; "by him all things were created"; "he is before all things, and in him all things hold together"; cosmic Christology at its widest scope
- **Heb 1:1–4** — "the radiance of God's glory and the exact representation of his being, sustaining all things by his powerful word"; full divinity; the Son as the definitive speech of God
- **Heb 2:14–18** — "since the children have flesh and blood, he too shared in their humanity so that by his death he might break the power of him who holds the power of death"; full humanity required for the saving work; "he had to be made like them, fully, in every way"
- **Heb 4:15** — "we do not have a high priest who is unable to empathize with our weaknesses, but we have one who has been tempted in every way, just as we are — yet he did not sin" — genuine human temptation without sin; the pastoral significance of the full humanity
- **John 8:58** — "before Abraham was born, I am" — the divine "I AM" of Exodus 3:14 applied to Jesus; pre-existence and divine identity in a single statement; the reaction of the crowd confirms they heard the claim
- **Mark 13:32** — "not even the Son" knows the day or hour — the genuine limitation of human knowledge in the incarnate one; the kenosis passage's practical instance
- **Luke 2:52** — "Jesus grew in wisdom and stature, and in favor with God and man" — genuine human development; not performance but real growth in the incarnate state
- **John 20:28** — Thomas: "My Lord and my God" — the strongest direct confession of divinity in the gospels; Jesus accepts it without correction

## Theologian Map

*Substantive positions — enough for serious engagement from the first exchange.*

**Athanasius of Alexandria (c. 296–373)**
The defender of Nicene orthodoxy against Arianism — *contra mundum* (against the world) during the years when Arianism was the position of emperors and most bishops. His treatise *On the Incarnation* is one of the most important Christological texts in Christian history. His soteriological argument for full divinity: only God can save; if Christ is a created being, however exalted, his death and resurrection accomplish no more than any other creature's — an infinite problem cannot be solved by a finite solution. His soteriological argument for full humanity: humanity had corrupted the image of God in which it was made; only the Image himself (the divine Logos) could restore it by uniting himself to human nature. His permanent formulation: "he became what we are so that we might become what he is" — the *admirabile commercium* (wonderful exchange), the logic of incarnation for salvation. This exchange principle grounds the Eastern Orthodox doctrine of theosis (deification): salvation is participation in the divine nature (2 Pet 1:4), made possible by the Son's participation in human nature.

**Gregory of Nazianzus (329–390)**
One of the Cappadocian fathers; the most important Christological thinker after Athanasius and before Chalcedon. His decisive contribution: "what is not assumed is not healed; what is united to God is also saved." Against Apollinarius (who gave Christ a divine rational soul in place of a human one): the scope of salvation exactly tracks the scope of the incarnation. If the Son did not take on a complete human nature — including will, rational mind, human emotions, all that belongs to being human — then that part of human nature is not redeemed. Every element of the human person that is genuinely united to the Son in the incarnation is capable of being saved; whatever is left out is left unredeemed. This has immediate pastoral implications: the doctrine of the full humanity of Christ is not a speculative philosophical claim — it is a claim about the scope of salvation.

**Cyril of Alexandria (376–444)**
The theologian of the one subject of the incarnation — *mia physis* ("one nature") language that was clarified and preserved at Chalcedon. Against Nestorius, who seemed to describe two subjects (the divine Son and the human Jesus), Cyril insisted that the eternal Logos is the *one* subject of everything the incarnate Christ experiences — his hunger, his weariness, his suffering, his death. This is why Mary is properly called *Theotokos* (God-bearer): the one she bore is the eternal Son, so the birth is the birth of the Son of God in the flesh. Cyril's enduring contribution is the *communicatio idiomatum* (communication of attributes/properties): what belongs to either nature can properly be predicated of the one person. "God suffered" and "God was born" and "God died" are proper theological statements — not because the divine nature suffers but because the person who suffered is the eternal Son. The pastoral significance: the cross is not the death of a human being who was associated with God; it is God himself, in his own person, dying.

**Karl Barth (1886–1968)**
The most important Christological theologian of the 20th century. Barth places Christology at the center of *all* theology: the knowledge of God, the doctrine of election, the nature of humanity, the shape of ethics — everything is determined by who Jesus Christ is and what he has done. His key move in the doctrine of election (*Church Dogmatics* II/2): election is not an abstract divine decree about individual destinies but a decision God makes *in* Jesus Christ. Christ is simultaneously the electing God and the elected human — God's "yes" to humanity is spoken in him, and God's "no" to sin is borne by him. This means that Christology is not a chapter of systematic theology; it is the organizing center around which every other chapter takes its shape. His account of the humanity of Jesus is unusually strong: the human nature of Christ is not a temporary vehicle for divine action but the definitive and permanent form of God's self-revelation. What God is, is seen in this human being.

**C.S. Lewis (1898–1963)**
*Mere Christianity*, Book II, Chapter 3 ("The Shocking Alternative"): the Lord/Liar/Lunatic trilemma. Jesus made claims that placed him in the position of God — forgiving sins on his own authority, claiming pre-existence before Abraham, applying the divine "I AM" to himself. Lewis argues these claims allow only three coherent responses: he was *lying* (deliberately deceiving his followers), *lunatic* (sincerely believing something false about his own identity), or *Lord* (telling the truth). The popular fourth option — that Jesus was simply a great moral teacher — is logically incoherent: a person who makes the claims Jesus made and is wrong about them cannot be a great moral teacher; the claim itself disqualifies the category. Lewis: "I am trying here to prevent anyone saying the really foolish thing that people often say about Him: 'I'm ready to accept Jesus as a great moral teacher, but I don't accept his claim to be God.' That is the one thing we must not say." Contemporary scholars have added a fourth option (*legend* — the divine claims were later inventions of the tradition, not the historical Jesus) and debate the extent of explicit divine claims in the Synoptics. As an entry-level forcing of the Christological question, however, the trilemma remains the most effective popular move in the apologetic tradition — it names what is actually at stake and prevents comfortable evasion.

## Entry Points

*How users typically arrive at this node.*

- **"Was Jesus really God?"** — the identity question; the Nicene answer and why it matters for salvation; the logical consequence of Arianism for soteriology
- **"If Jesus was God, how could he not know things, or be tempted, or die?"** — the kenosis and two-natures question; the human nature framework; genuine limitation without loss of divinity
- **"How can one person be both fully God and fully human — doesn't one cancel the other?"** — the hypostatic union directly; Chalcedon's four negatives; holding the mystery without resolving it
- **"Wasn't Jesus just a great teacher / enlightened human / prophet?"** — the low Christology entry; the internal logic of the gospels (the "I AM" claims, Thomas's confession); Lewis's *Lord, Liar, or Lunatic* as an entry-level framework, but the Nicene exegetical case is the more substantive one
- **"What does it mean that God became human?"** — the incarnation entry; Athanasius's exchange principle; wonder rather than problem; what the permanent incarnation implies for the material world
- **Scholars:** Chalcedon's limits and what it doesn't explain; kenotic theology and its problems; Barth's christocentric structure; the historical Jesus debates; Cyril vs. Nestorius and the *communicatio idiomatum*

## Failure Modes

*Where thinking about Christology characteristically goes wrong.*

- **Functional Arianism** — affirming full divinity doctrinally while treating Jesus as a secondary divine being in practice; worship and prayer directed to the Father as "real God" with Jesus as intermediary
- **Functional Docetism** — affirming the humanity doctrinally while treating every human moment of Jesus as performance; the hunger, anguish, and limited knowledge become diplomatic theater rather than genuine experience
- **Divine costume Christology** — God dressed in human clothing; the humanity is real enough to make the cross possible but not real enough to make the temptations real, the growth real, or the grief real
- **Therapeutic reduction** — "Jesus was fully human, which means he struggled just like we do" taken to mean he was *only* human; the full humanity used to evacuate the divinity
- **The two-Christs error** — holding the divine and human in such tension that there is functionally a divine self (omniscient, omnipotent, sovereign) and a human self (limited, suffering, dying) as two separate persons wearing the same name
- **Speculative resolution** — attempting to explain the mechanics of the hypostatic union rather than inhabiting the mystery Chalcedon defined; the union is bounded, not resolved
