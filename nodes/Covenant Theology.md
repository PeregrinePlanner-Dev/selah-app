# Node: Covenant Theology

**Weight:** The organizing framework through which Reformed Christianity reads the whole Bible as a unified story. Covenant theology is not a peripheral theory — it is the hermeneutical lens through which the relationship between the Old and New Testaments is understood, the basis for paedobaptism, the answer to why the law still matters after Christ, and the framework that defines the church as the continuation of the people of God across the Testaments. Its alternative — dispensationalism — is the other dominant evangelical hermeneutical framework, producing a different ecclesiology, a different eschatology, and a different account of Israel and the church. The choice between these frameworks shapes almost every area of doctrine.

**Connects to:** Scripture & Revelation, Anthropology, Sacraments & Ordinances, Ecclesiology, Eschatology, Sovereignty & Free Will, Law & Gospel, Creation

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- God has related to humanity through covenants — structured, binding relationships with specified parties, conditions, signs, and promises; this is Scripture's primary category for describing the God-humanity relationship
- The covenants are progressive — each new covenant advances, fulfills, and often expands the preceding one; there is genuine development without contradiction
- The covenant of grace is the one covenant of salvation — from the protoevangelium (Gen 3:15) through Noah, Abraham, Moses, David, and the new covenant in Christ; the content is always the same (God as their God, they as his people) though the form changes
- Christ is the mediator and fulfillment of all covenants — all the OT covenants find their fulfillment in him; he is the seed of Abraham, the son of David, the new Moses, the true Israel
- The church is the continuation of the covenant people — the church is not a parenthesis in God's plan for Israel but the fulfillment of Israel's calling; Gentiles are grafted into Israel (Rom 11:17–24), not given a separate covenant
- Baptism corresponds to circumcision as the covenant sign — if the new covenant community is the continuation of the covenant community, the covenant sign should similarly be administered to covenant members and their children

## Key Tensions

### 1. The Covenant of Works and the Covenant of Grace — The Classic Dual Framework

**The Covenant of Works (pre-fall):** Reformed federal theology (from the 16th century) construes the original relationship between God and Adam as a formal covenant: Adam stood as the federal representative of humanity; the condition was perfect obedience; the reward was confirmed eschatological life; the penalty for disobedience was death. This covenant is found not explicitly in Genesis (the word "covenant" is not used) but by theological inference from the parallel Paul draws in Rom 5:12–21 between Adam and Christ — if Christ's obedience is the basis of the covenant of grace, Adam's disobedience must have been a failure of something parallel. The Westminster Standards and London Baptist Confession both include the covenant of works.

**The objection:** The covenant of works is a theological inference, not a biblical category. Some Reformed theologians (notably Barth and some contemporary theologians) find it foreign to the biblical narrative and question whether Adam's pre-fall relationship with God was genuinely works-based. Without the covenant of works, the parallel structure between Adam and Christ is maintained through different categories.

**The Covenant of Grace:** The one covenant of salvation that runs from Gen 3:15 (the promise that the seed of the woman would crush the serpent's head) through all the subsequent covenants to Christ. The historical covenants (Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, new) are administrations of the one covenant of grace — they differ in form but not in substance. The elect in every age are saved by grace through faith in the promised Christ, whether looking forward (OT) or looking back (NT).

**The *pactum salutis* (covenant of redemption):** Some Reformed theologians (17th century) added a third covenant: an intra-Trinitarian agreement between the Father and the Son before creation, in which the Son agrees to serve as mediator and the Father agrees to give the elect to the Son. This grounds the covenant of grace in eternal Trinitarian purpose. The exegetical basis: John 6:37–40 — "all that the Father gives me will come to me" — implies a pre-temporal giving. This is highly speculative but expresses a genuine insight: salvation is grounded in God's own eternal purposes, not in temporal response to human need.

### 2. Covenant Theology vs. Dispensationalism — The Great Hermeneutical Divide

**Covenant theology's claim:** The Bible tells one story with one people of God (the elect) across the Old and New Testaments. Israel and the church are not two different peoples but one covenant people whose form changes at the coming of Christ. The NT fulfills and transforms the OT promises; the land promises to Israel are fulfilled in the new creation; the national promises are fulfilled in the church as the universal covenant community. There is no future ethnic/national program for Israel distinct from the church.

**Dispensationalism's claim:** The Bible tells the story of two distinct peoples of God — Israel (the earthly people, with earthly land and national promises) and the church (the heavenly people, formed at Pentecost and removed at the rapture). God's promises to Israel have not been fulfilled in the church; they await a literal fulfillment in the millennial kingdom. The Mosaic covenant defines Israel's national program; the new covenant defines the church's spiritual program; these do not merge.

**What is at stake:**
- *Hermeneutics* — does OT prophecy have a literal fulfillment (dispensational), or is it transformed and transcended in Christ (covenant)?
- *Eschatology* — will there be a literal millennial kingdom in the land of Israel (dispensational), or is the kingdom realized in Christ and consummated in the new creation without a distinct Israeli phase (covenant)?
- *The church* — is the church a new organism without OT antecedent (dispensational), or is it the continuation of the covenant community (covenant)?
- *Baptism* — paedobaptism is only coherent on covenant theology; credo-baptism is coherent on both frameworks (the baptists within covenant theology say the covenant sign now requires personal faith because the new covenant is constituted by regeneration)

### 3. The New Covenant — Continuity and Discontinuity

**Jeremiah 31:31–34 — the new covenant promise:** "Behold, the days are coming, declares the Lord, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and the house of Judah, not like the covenant that I made with their fathers on the day when I took them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt, my covenant that they broke... For I will put my law within them, and I will write it on their hearts."

**The key question: what is new about the new covenant?**

*The continuity view (Baptist covenant theology):* The new covenant is genuinely new — not an administration of the previous covenant but a qualitatively different arrangement in which the covenant people are constituted by regeneration (they will "all know me, from the least to the greatest" — Jer 31:34). This means the new covenant community is entirely composed of regenerate believers, which is why the covenant sign (baptism) should be administered only to believers.

*The Reformed paedobaptist view:* The new covenant is the fullest administration of the one covenant of grace — it is "new" in form (the Spirit's more direct and universal operation) but not in substance (grace through faith). Covenant children are still covenant members as in the OT; the covenant sign (baptism) is administered to them as circumcision was. Jer 31:34's "all know me" describes the covenant community's character, not the exclusion of unbelievers from its visible membership.

*The dispensational view:* The new covenant in Jer 31 is made with Israel and Judah — not the church. The church participates in the spiritual blessings of the new covenant (the Spirit's work of transformation) but the new covenant's national/land promises await fulfillment in the millennial kingdom with restored Israel.

### 4. The Mosaic Covenant — Conditional or Unconditional?

The Mosaic covenant (Sinai) presents the most complex hermeneutical questions in covenant theology. It is:
- *Conditional* — the blessings are tied to Israel's obedience (Deut 28); curses for disobedience; the exile is the covenant curse enacted
- *National* — it governs the specific nation of Israel as a theocratic community; its civil and ceremonial laws are particular to that context
- *Typological* — the tabernacle, priesthood, sacrifices, and purity laws all anticipate and are fulfilled in Christ (Hebrews passim)
- *Moral at its core* — the Decalogue stands at the heart of the Mosaic covenant and is not revoked in the new covenant; it expresses the moral law that is written on the heart in the new covenant

**How does the Mosaic covenant relate to the Christian?**

*Three-way division of the law (classic Reformed):* The law is divided into moral (Decalogue — binding on all people in all times), ceremonial (temple, sacrifice, purity — fulfilled in Christ and no longer operative in their original form), and civil (Israel's national law — abrogated with the theocratic state, though its principles remain instructive). The moral law continues in force; the ceremonial and civil laws do not, though they are fulfilled in Christ and illuminate his work.

*The whole law as one unity:* The NT nowhere explicitly divides the law into three categories. Paul's treatment of the law is complex: he treats it as a unit in some texts (Gal 3:10 — "cursed is everyone who does not abide by all things written in the Book of the Law") and makes distinctions in others. Some theologians reject the threefold division as artificially neat.

*New covenant context:* All OT laws are "pre-fulfilled" — they are received through the filter of their fulfillment in Christ. We do not ask "does this still apply?" but "how does this law look now that it is fulfilled in Christ?" This produces neither simple continuity (it says it in the OT, so it applies today) nor simple discontinuity (that was the OT, so it doesn't apply) but typological reading.

## Scripture Anchors

- **Gen 3:15** — "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel" — the *protoevangelium*; the first covenantal promise of Christ; the beginning of the covenant of grace
- **Gen 9:8–17** — the Noahic covenant; the rainbow sign; the promise of world stability within which redemption will unfold; the covenant with "all flesh"
- **Gen 15; 17** — the Abrahamic covenant; the promise of land, seed, and blessing; the covenant cutting (Gen 15) and the covenant sign (circumcision, Gen 17); the unconditional structure that underlies everything
- **Exod 19:5–6; 20** — the Mosaic covenant; "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation"; the Decalogue; the conditional structure and the blessing/curse framework
- **2 Sam 7:12–16** — the Davidic covenant; the promise of an eternal throne and a son who will reign; the typological trajectory toward Christ
- **Jer 31:31–34** — the new covenant promise; the law written on the heart; universal knowledge of God; forgiveness of sin — the most important OT text for understanding the new covenant's character
- **Rom 4:9–17** — Abraham's justification by faith before circumcision, making him "the father of all who believe"; the covenant of Abraham as the ground of Gentile inclusion
- **Rom 11:17–24** — the olive tree; Gentiles grafted into Israel; the one covenant people; the continuity of the people of God
- **Gal 3:15–29** — the priority of the Abrahamic covenant over the Mosaic; "the law came 430 years later"; the law did not annul the promise; Christ as the seed of Abraham
- **Heb 8:6–13** — the new covenant as "better" than the Mosaic; Jeremiah 31 cited; the Mosaic covenant becoming "obsolete"

## Theologian Map

**Johannes Cocceius (1603–1669)**
The father of federal theology (*foedus* = covenant) as a systematic framework. Cocceius's *Summa doctrinae de foedere et testamento Dei* (1648) organized Reformed theology around the covenants of works and grace as the organizing framework of salvation history. Before Cocceius, covenant theology existed as a tendency in Zwingli and Bullinger and was systematized in the Westminster Standards, but Cocceius gave it the most complete theoretical expression. His contribution: covenant theology as an alternative to the medieval decree-and-dispensation framework, rooted in the biblical narrative.

**Geerhardus Vos (1862–1949)**
*Biblical Theology* — the founder of Reformed biblical theology as a discipline distinct from systematic theology. Vos traced the progressive development of God's revelation through the covenants, showing how each covenantal stage advances the promise while maintaining continuity. His key insight: the covenant is not primarily a legal mechanism but a relational reality — God's self-disclosure through history. Vos distinguished the covenants from each other (the "old" ways of divine speech — prophets, visions, dreams) and from the final word in Christ (Heb 1:1–2). His work is foundational for all subsequent Reformed biblical theology and shapes how covenant theologians read the OT christologically.

**O. Palmer Robertson (1937–)**
*The Christ of the Covenants* (1980) — the most widely used evangelical textbook on covenant theology. Robertson defines covenant as "a bond in blood sovereignly administered" and traces the Noahic, Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, and new covenants with attention to their structure (grant vs. suzerain-vassal types) and their unifying trajectory toward Christ. Robertson argues against the sharp covenant-of-works/covenant-of-grace distinction in favor of seeing all God's covenants as expressions of his one sovereign grace, with the "works" element present throughout but in different modes.

**Charles Ryrie (1925–2016)**
*Dispensationalism* — the most systematic defense of the dispensational alternative. Ryrie's three sine qua non of dispensationalism: (1) consistent literal hermeneutics, (2) distinction between Israel and the church as two distinct peoples of God, (3) the glory of God (not human salvation) as the unified purpose of history. Ryrie's formulation is included here because covenant theology is only understood in contrast to its primary alternative. His argument: covenant theology's reading of OT prophecy as spiritually fulfilled in the church requires allegorization that violates the authorial intent of the OT authors.

## Entry Points

- **"Why do some churches baptize babies?"** — the most common popular entry; the covenant sign argument; covenant theology's account of the covenant community
- **"How does the Old Testament relate to the New?"** — the hermeneutical entry; one covenant of grace vs. two peoples; the fulfillment of OT promises in Christ
- **"Does God have a separate plan for Israel?"** — the covenant theology vs. dispensationalism entry; the church as grafted into Israel; eschatological implications
- **"Why don't we sacrifice animals anymore?"** — the Mosaic covenant entry; ceremonial/moral distinction; Christ as the fulfillment of the sacrificial system
- **"What does the Bible's story add up to?"** — the metanarrative entry; covenant as the organizing thread; from creation to new creation through the covenantal structure
- **Scholars:** Federal theology (Cocceius); Vos on biblical theology; Robertson on the covenants; Ryrie's dispensational challenge; covenant and baptism

## Failure Modes

- **Flat-Bible reading** — treating the entire Bible as equally applicable to the Christian today without attention to covenantal development; applying Mosaic civil law to contemporary states without the typological framework
- **Dispensational atomism** — reading the Bible as a series of disconnected programs with no organic unity; losing the single story of the one covenant of grace across the Testaments
- **Over-systematization** — forcing the biblical covenants into a neat theological schema (works/grace; grant/suzerain-vassal) that is tidier than the biblical data warrants; the covenants are not identical in structure
- **Losing new covenant newness** — reading the new covenant as simply more of the same without attention to what is genuinely new (the Spirit's universal operation; the knowledge of God as universal in the covenant community; the fulfillment of typology)
- **Anti-Semitism by accident** — supersessionist readings that treat the church as having simply replaced Israel, without attention to Paul's warnings against Gentile pride in Romans 11; covenant theology must account for the ongoing role of the Jewish people in God's purposes
- **Baptismal covenantalism as magic** — treating the covenant sign as automatically conveying what it signifies; the medieval error of baptismal regeneration returns when covenant membership is treated as guaranteeing salvation
