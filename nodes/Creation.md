# Node: Creation

**Weight:** The doctrine that establishes the fundamental relationship between God and everything that is not God. Creation *ex nihilo* — out of nothing — is not a scientific claim competing with cosmology but a theological claim about the kind of dependence all things have on God. At the same time, this node is where systematic theology intersects most acutely with natural science, and where evangelical Christianity has been most internally divided in the last century and a half. The origins debates (young earth, old earth, evolutionary creationism) have consumed disproportionate evangelical energy, sometimes at the cost of the richer theological substance: the world is good, God sustains it, and the new creation is its redemption, not its replacement.

**Connects to:** Theology Proper, Anthropology, Eschatology, Problem of Evil, Scripture & Revelation, Sovereignty & Free Will, Vocation & Work, Social Ethics

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- God created all things freely and from nothing (*ex nihilo*) — he was not compelled, did not work from pre-existing matter, and did not create out of himself (against pantheism); creation depends on God for its existence in every moment
- Creation is distinct from God — the world is not an emanation of the divine, not an aspect of God, not God's body; Creator/creature distinction is fundamental
- Creation is good — Genesis 1's sevenfold declaration; the material world is not a problem to be escaped but a gift to be received, stewarded, and ultimately renewed
- Humanity is the pinnacle of creation — made in the *imago Dei*, given dominion, accountable to God; distinctive within creation while being fully creaturely
- The creation is fallen — sin has introduced disorder, death, and futility into the created order; creation groans (Rom 8:20–22); the world is not as God made it
- God sustains creation continuously (*creatio continua*) — the world does not persist by its own power but is upheld moment by moment by God's sustaining will (Col 1:17 — "in him all things hold together")
- Creation will be redeemed — the end is not the destruction of creation but its liberation and renewal; the new creation is the telos of the original creation

## Key Tensions

### 1. Creation *Ex Nihilo* — The Fundamental Claim

*Ex nihilo* — "out of nothing" — is not a phrase from Genesis 1 (which begins with creation already in process) but a theological inference that developed in the early church in response to Greek philosophy and Gnostic alternatives.

**What *ex nihilo* denies:**
- *Dualism* — the idea that God shaped pre-existing, uncreated matter (Plato's Demiurge); this would make matter an independent principle alongside God
- *Emanationism* — the idea that creation flows necessarily from the divine nature (Plotinus's Neoplatonism); this collapses the Creator/creature distinction
- *Pantheism* — the idea that the world is identical with or part of God

**What *ex nihilo* affirms:**
- God's absolute freedom — creation was not necessary; God chose to create; this means creation is pure gift
- God's absolute priority — nothing existed before or independently of God's creative act
- Creation's complete dependence — the world has no existence apart from God's continuous willing of it

**The *ex nihilo* text:** 2 Maccabees 7:28 is the clearest biblical statement; John 1:3 — "through him all things were made; without him nothing was made that has been made"; Heb 11:3 — "by faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible." Genesis 1:1 — "in the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth" — has been read as affirming *ex nihilo* from Augustine onward.

**The pastoral significance:** *Ex nihilo* means that nothing in creation is ultimate; matter, time, space, energy — all are contingent. This positions Christian theology against both materialism (matter is ultimate) and idealism (mind/idea is ultimate). It also means that the created world can be trusted — it is the good gift of a good God, not an alien realm to be escaped.

### 2. The Origins Debate — Young Earth, Old Earth, Evolutionary Creationism

The single most internally contested empirical question in evangelical Christianity. Three positions dominate:

**Young Earth Creationism (YEC):**
- The earth and universe are approximately 6,000–10,000 years old
- Genesis 1–2 is a literal historical narrative; the six days are ordinary 24-hour days
- The global flood of Noah accounts for the geological and fossil record
- Death entered the world only after Adam's fall (no animal death before sin)
- Primary advocates: Ken Ham (Answers in Genesis), Henry Morris, the Creation Research Society

**Old Earth Creationism (OEC):**
- The earth and universe are approximately 4.5 billion and 13.8 billion years old respectively (consistent with cosmological and geological evidence)
- Genesis 1's "days" may be long ages (day-age theory), or Genesis 1 may be a literary framework describing what was created, not the sequence of creation (framework hypothesis); or there are gaps in the text (gap theory)
- Special creation of species, no common descent with modification
- Death may have existed in the animal kingdom before the fall; Adam's sin introduced human death and cosmic spiritual consequences
- Primary advocates: Hugh Ross (Reasons to Believe), Gleason Archer, C. John Collins, Walter Kaiser

**Evolutionary Creationism (EC) / Theistic Evolution:**
- The earth and universe are as old as mainstream science holds; evolutionary biology is the correct account of how biological diversity developed
- God created through the process of evolution; this is not a "religion of chance" but purposeful creation through natural processes God sustains
- Genesis 1–2 is not a scientific account but a theological narrative; the "days" are a literary structure; the text answers "who" and "why," not "how" and "when"
- The nature of Adam and Eve is contested: some hold a historical pair at the base of the human family tree; others treat them as representative figures or as literary archetypes; still others locate them within an ancestral human population
- Primary advocates: Francis Collins (BioLogos), Tremper Longman III, Tim Keller (in part), John Walton (*The Lost World of Genesis One*)

**What is the actual theological core beneath the debate?**

All three positions agree on: God created; creation is good; humans are made in the *imago Dei*; the fall is real and consequential; natural history is subject to God's governance. The debate is about: the genre and interpretation of Genesis 1–2; the age of the earth; the nature of Adam and Eve; the mechanism of biological diversity. The Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy leaves the question open (inerrancy is compatible with multiple Genesis interpretations). Evangelicals of the highest scholarly caliber hold each position.

### 3. Genesis 1–2 — What Kind of Text Is It?

John Walton's work (*The Lost World of Genesis One*, 2009) has been the most influential recent contribution: ancient Near Eastern cosmogony texts are not material origin accounts but functional accounts — they describe creation in terms of assigning function and purpose, not material manufacture. On this reading, Genesis 1 describes God assigning functions to a cosmos he is making into his temple/dwelling place, not a scientific sequence of material origins. This reading is compatible with any position on the earth's age and biological diversity.

**The literary framework hypothesis (Avi Hurvitz, Henri Blocher, Bruce Waltke):** Genesis 1 is structured in two triads of days (days 1–3 create realms; days 4–6 fill them with inhabitants) that reflect theological and aesthetic design rather than strict historical sequence. The "days" are a literary scaffold, not a chronological report.

**The historical-grammatical reading (standard YEC):** The Hebrew *yom* (day) with a numerical qualifier (first day, second day) always refers to an ordinary day in the rest of the OT. The literary features of Genesis 1 do not override its historical character. Jesus and Paul treat Adam and Eve as historical individuals (Matt 19:4; Rom 5:12–21; 1 Cor 15:22, 45).

**The hermeneutical question:** This is not a question that can be settled by scientific evidence alone — it is a question of what kind of text Genesis 1–2 is, which is a literary-hermeneutical question. Both the YEC and the EC position appeal to Genesis 1; they disagree about how to read it.

### 4. The New Creation as Telos — Creation's Purpose and End

The doctrine of creation is incomplete without eschatology. The world was created for a purpose that transcends itself — God's glory revealed in a community of redeemed image-bearers dwelling with him in a renewed creation. The new creation (Rev 21–22) is not the abandonment of the original creation but its liberation and fulfillment (Rom 8:21 — "the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay and brought into the freedom and glory of the children of God").

**The implications for now:**
- Creation care is a theological imperative, not merely an environmental preference — the world we are called to steward is the world God will redeem
- Human cultural activity (art, science, commerce, politics) has eschatological significance if the new creation is renovation rather than replacement — what is done in the Lord is "not in vain" (1 Cor 15:58; Wright's argument)
- The creation's present groaning is not a sign of its worthlessness but of its eager anticipation — it is waiting for the revelation of the sons of God, not waiting for its incineration

**The two-age framework:** Jesus and Paul operate with a two-age framework — "this age" and "the age to come." The present creation is not ultimate; the age to come is already breaking in through the resurrection of Christ. The new creation has already begun — in the resurrection body of Jesus, in the communities formed by the Spirit, in the genuine human flourishing that anticipates the kingdom. The theology of creation properly includes the theology of new creation as its horizon.

## Scripture Anchors

- **Gen 1:1** — "in the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth" — the foundational cosmological claim; the basis for *ex nihilo*
- **Gen 1:31** — "God saw all that he had made, and it was very good" — the goodness of the material creation; the anti-Gnostic text
- **John 1:1–3** — "through him all things were made; without him nothing was made that has been made" — Christ as agent of creation; *ex nihilo* implied
- **Col 1:15–17** — Christ as "firstborn over all creation... in him all things were created... he is before all things, and in him all things hold together" — creation's origin and sustaining in Christ; cosmic Christology
- **Rom 8:19–22** — "the creation waits in eager expectation for the children of God to be revealed... the creation itself will be liberated from its bondage to decay" — the fallen creation and its hope; the new creation as liberation
- **Heb 1:3** — "sustaining all things by his powerful word" — God's continuous sustaining of creation
- **Heb 11:3** — "by faith we understand that the universe was formed at God's command, so that what is seen was not made out of what was visible" — *ex nihilo*; creation from nothing
- **Rev 4:11** — "you are worthy, our Lord and God, to receive glory and honor and power, for you created all things, and by your will they were created and have their being" — creation as worship-ground; the creature's proper response to the Creator
- **Rev 21:1–5** — "then I saw a new heaven and a new earth... he who was seated on the throne said, 'I am making everything new!'" — new creation as telos; renovation not replacement

## Theologian Map

**Irenaeus of Lyon (c. 130–202)**
Against the Gnostics' devaluation of the material world and the material God of the OT, Irenaeus developed the most robustly anti-Gnostic theology of creation in the early church. His contribution: God the Creator and God the Redeemer are the same God; matter is not evil and not beneath God's concern; the incarnation of Christ confirms the goodness of creation — God entered his own good world. His concept of *recapitulatio* (recapitulation): Christ summed up and restored what Adam had destroyed; the new creation is the completion and redemption of the original. Irenaeus established the theological trajectory that culminates in the NT's new creation theology.

**Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)**
*Summa Theologiae* on creation (*ST* I, Q44–46): the first and most philosophically rigorous Catholic treatment of *ex nihilo*. Aquinas demonstrates that *ex nihilo* follows from God's aseity — God's existence from himself — which entails that everything else receives its existence from him. The Creator/creature distinction is absolute and unbridgeable; creation participates in existence but does not *possess* it the way God possesses it. Aquinas also distinguishes creation (origination) from conservation (sustaining) and argues that they are the same divine act. His framework is the philosophical foundation for most subsequent Christian reflection on creation.

**John Walton (1952–)**
*The Lost World of Genesis One* (2009) — the most influential recent evangelical contribution to the origins debate. Walton's argument: ancient cosmogonies were not material origin accounts but functional accounts; Genesis 1 is in this genre; the text describes God setting up his cosmic temple by assigning functions, not a scientific account of material manufacture. The "days" of Genesis 1 are days of a cosmic inauguration ceremony, not days of material creation. This reading, if correct, dissolves the conflict between Genesis 1 and evolutionary biology, since Genesis 1 was never making the claims the conflict presupposes. Walton remains committed to a historical Adam within an ancestral human population.

**Francis Collins (1950–) / BioLogos**
Collins founded BioLogos to articulate and defend evolutionary creationism — the position that evolutionary biology is the correct account of biological diversity and that this is fully compatible with orthodox Christian faith. *The Language of God* (2006) is his personal account of moving from atheism to Christian faith while working as a geneticist and director of the Human Genome Project. His argument: the evidence for common descent is overwhelming; a God who creates through natural processes is not smaller than a God who creates by fiat; the conflict between Christian faith and evolutionary science is unnecessary and harmful to both. His position is contested by YEC and OEC advocates, but his scientific credibility makes him impossible to dismiss.

## Entry Points

- **"How can a Christian believe in evolution?"** — the most common popular entry; the three positions; what the actual theological commitments require; Walton on Genesis genre
- **"Doesn't Genesis say God created in six days?"** — the genre and hermeneutics entry; literal vs. literary readings; the framework hypothesis
- **"Does the age of the earth matter for Christian faith?"** — the question of what is theologically essential vs. secondary; the difference between inerrancy and a specific interpretation of Genesis
- **"Why did God create the world?"** — the theology of creation's purpose; God's freedom; creation as gift; glory as telos
- **"Is it wrong to care about the environment?"** — creation care as theological imperative; the new creation as creation redeemed, not replaced; stewardship of *imago Dei*
- **Scholars:** *Ex nihilo* and its alternatives; Walton on ancient cosmogony; the three positions on origins; the new creation as creation's telos

## Failure Modes

- **Concordism** — assuming Genesis 1 is intended to correspond to modern scientific categories (days = geological ages, the sequence = evolutionary sequence); this misreads the ancient text by modern questions
- **Conflation of inerrancy with YEC** — treating young-earth creationism as a requirement of biblical inerrancy; the Chicago Statement does not require it; many inerrantists hold OEC or EC
- **Scientific deferrism** — treating scientific consensus as the arbiter of theological claims; science and theology operate in different domains; the age of the earth is not the same kind of question as whether God created
- **Gnostic drift** — treating the material world as a problem to be escaped rather than a gift to be stewarded; creation is good; the body is good; the new creation is material
- **Origins obsession** — spending the library of evangelical energy on the origins debate at the cost of the richer theology of creation — its goodness, its dependence, its groaning, its hope
- **Eschatological indifference** — treating creation's fate as destruction rather than renewal; this undermines creation care, cultural engagement, and the proper hope of the resurrection
