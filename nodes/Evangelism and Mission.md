# Node: Evangelism & Mission

**Weight:** The integrating node of the entire system. Every other node either establishes the problem evangelism addresses, the solution it proclaims, the stakes it points to, or the community and method through which it happens. Evangelism is not a sub-topic of practical theology — it is the question the whole system is implicitly answering. The Great Commission (Matt 28:18–20) is not an appendix to the gospel; it is the gospel's natural overflow. A theology that does not eventually move toward mission has stopped short.

**Connects to:** All nodes. See Spine Map below.

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- The gospel is specific: the good news that Jesus Christ — fully God and fully human — died for sinners, was raised bodily from the dead, and offers forgiveness, reconciliation with God, and new life to all who repent and believe. It is not merely a moral example, a social program, or a general spiritual uplift. It has content.
- The gospel is necessary: without it, people remain under the judgment their sin deserves. The necessity of proclamation is grounded in the seriousness of the human condition (Romans 3:23; 6:23) and the uniqueness of Christ as the only mediator (1 Tim 2:5; Acts 4:12).
- The gospel is exclusive: Jesus is not one path among many to God but the way, the truth, and the life (John 14:6). This exclusivity is not arrogance but faithfulness to what Christianity actually claims — a specific historical person did a specific thing that changes everything.
- The gospel is universal in scope: it is for all peoples, all nations, all cultures, without distinction. The Great Commission (Matt 28:18–20) has no geographic, ethnic, or cultural boundary.
- The church is the primary carrier of the gospel: the community of those who have received it are sent to bear it. Mission is not optional for the church — a church that does not evangelize has misunderstood what it is.
- Word and deed belong together: the gospel is proclaimed in words and embodied in lives. Social action is not the gospel, but neither is it a distraction from it — a community that speaks good news while ignoring visible suffering undermines its own proclamation.
- Evangelism operates within God's sovereignty: the Spirit convicts, regenerates, and draws; the human messenger proclaims. The outcome belongs to God; the obligation to proclaim belongs to the church.

## Key Tensions

### 1. The Exclusivity Claim — Is Jesus the Only Way?

No claim Christianity makes is more contested in a pluralist culture than this one. John 14:6 — "I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me" — is either the most important sentence ever spoken or the most offensive. There is no middle position.

**The case for exclusivity:** It is not primarily a claim Christians make about Christianity — it is a claim Jesus made about himself. The early church proclaimed it at personal cost (Acts 4:12 — "salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved," spoken to authorities who had just imprisoned Peter and John). The exclusivity claim is not a later Constantinian imposition; it predates the church's cultural power entirely. The logic is internal to the gospel: if Christ's death is the specific act by which God reconciles humanity to himself, then there is no parallel path around it.

**The challenge from inclusivism:** C.S. Lewis, and more systematically Clark Pinnock (*A Wideness in God's Mercy*), argued for an inclusivist position: people who have not heard the gospel may be saved through Christ's work applied to their sincere response to the general revelation available to them. This preserves Christ as the unique basis of salvation while not requiring explicit conscious faith. The question is whether Scripture supports this — Romans 10:14 ("how can they believe in the one of whom they have not heard?") creates pressure against it.

**The challenge from pluralism:** John Hick argued that all major world religions are culturally conditioned responses to the same ultimate divine reality — Christianity has no privileged access. This is not the inclusivist position (which still requires Christ); it is the position that Christianity is one valid path among many. This is incompatible with the NT's explicit claims and was not the position of any major figure in historic Christianity before the 20th century.

**Holding the claim graciously:** Exclusivity does not require triumphalism. The person who has received a life-saving diagnosis and treatment does not boast over those who haven't received it — they try to reach them. The evangelistic posture the gospel produces is urgency and compassion, not condescension. Stott, Keller, and Bonhoeffer all model this.

### 2. Word and Deed — Proclamation vs. Social Action

This is the defining tension of 20th-century evangelical missiology and it remains alive.

**The social gospel:** Walter Rauschenbusch (*A Theology for the Social Gospel*, 1917) argued that the Kingdom of God is primarily a social reality to be built through Christian engagement with structures of injustice. Evangelism, on this view, means transforming society. The evangelical critique: Rauschenbusch's Kingdom is horizontal (human-to-human) and loses the vertical (human-to-God); his gospel can no longer explain why the cross is necessary.

**The Lausanne Covenant (1974):** The watershed document. John Stott was its primary architect. The Lausanne Covenant affirmed that both evangelism (proclaiming the gospel in words) and social responsibility (acting for justice and human dignity) are part of the Christian's duty — but that evangelism has a certain *primacy*, because "reconciliation with God is the ultimate priority." Stott's formulation: social action is not the gospel but it accompanies the gospel as its fruit and demonstration. The two wings of the bird — neither is the whole bird, but neither can be cut off.

**The challenge from both sides:** Conservative evangelicals worried that affirming social responsibility compromised the priority of proclamation — that "mission creep" would turn churches into NGOs. Liberation theologians (Gustavo Gutiérrez) argued the Lausanne formulation still privileged spiritual salvation over material liberation in a way that protected the comfortable. Both critiques identify real dangers. Stott's answer: the danger on the right is a disembodied gospel that ignores the world God loves; the danger on the left is a this-worldly gospel that loses the news about Jesus.

**The practical question:** Most people asking about evangelism are really asking: "Am I supposed to talk to people, or just live a good life?" The NT answer is both — but the words matter, because a life without the explanation attached to it does not by itself communicate the gospel. As Stott put it: "If we truly love our neighbor, we shall without doubt tell him the good news of Jesus, since this is the greatest benefit we can bestow on him."

### 3. Sovereignty and Evangelism — If God Elects, Why Go?

The most common pastoral objection to Calvinist soteriology is this: if God has already determined who will be saved, evangelism seems either unnecessary (God will save them anyway) or futile (if they're not elect, no amount of proclamation will change that). This objection misunderstands the Reformed position.

**The Reformed answer:** God's sovereignty in election does not bypass means — it works *through* means. The Spirit regenerates through the proclamation of the Word (Rom 10:17 — "faith comes from hearing the message"). Election does not make evangelism unnecessary; it makes it certain to bear fruit. Spurgeon's famous response: "If God had painted a yellow stripe down the back of every elect person, I would go around lifting shirts. But he hasn't, so I preach to everyone." God's sovereignty is the *ground* of evangelistic confidence, not an argument for passivity.

**The Arminian alternative:** For Arminius and Wesley, God's universal prevenient grace enables all people to respond to the gospel, and genuine human freedom means the outcome is not predetermined. This makes the urgency of evangelism immediately intuitive — the person in front of you may or may not respond, and that matters. The tension with the Reformed position is real but neither view produces a non-evangelistic church in practice.

**The point of agreement:** Both traditions are committed to the Great Commission. The theological debate is about the mechanism, not the mandate.

### 4. The Content of the Gospel — What Exactly Are We Proclaiming?

This is more contested than it sounds, and the answer determines everything about how evangelism works.

**The minimalist gospel:** "Believe in Jesus and you'll go to heaven when you die." This version produces easy-believism — a decision without discipleship, an entry ticket without a transformation. The NT never presents the gospel this simply.

**The Kingdom gospel:** Jesus himself preached "the Kingdom of God is at hand — repent and believe the good news" (Mark 1:15). The Kingdom gospel includes the announcement that the long-awaited reign of God has arrived in Jesus, is present now in partial form, and will be consummated at his return. N.T. Wright argues that the Western evangelical tradition has reduced the gospel to "justification by faith" — Paul's answer to one particular question — while losing the larger announcement of Jesus's Lordship and the renewal of all things.

**The justification gospel:** Paul's concentrated articulation in Romans and Galatians: humans are sinners under condemnation; Christ bore that condemnation on the cross; those who trust in him are declared righteous — justified — before God. Luther, Calvin, and the Reformation tradition argue this is the irreducible core. Without the substitutionary atonement and the forensic declaration of righteousness, the gospel loses its nerve.

**The synthesis:** These are not competing gospels — they are different emphases on the same reality. The Kingdom gospel and the justification gospel both require the cross, both require the resurrection, both require a living Lord, and both require repentance and faith. The risk of the Kingdom framing alone is losing the mechanism (why does the cross matter?); the risk of the justification framing alone is losing the cosmic scope (what is Jesus Lord *of*?). Keller is the most accessible contemporary voice holding both together.

### 5. The Great Commission — Command, Calling, or Both?

Matthew 28:18–20 is the canonical mandate: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me. Therefore go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, and teaching them to obey everything I have commanded you."

**The grammatical point:** In the Greek, the only imperative is *mathēteusate* — "make disciples." "Go," "baptizing," and "teaching" are all participles. The command is to make disciples; the going, baptizing, and teaching are how that command is carried out. This means the Great Commission is not just about initial evangelism but about full formation — bringing people to the point of matured faith and obedience.

**The scope:** "All nations" (*panta ta ethnē*) — not political nations but peoples, ethnic groups, language communities. The missiological use of "unreached people groups" draws directly from this. There remain thousands of ethnic communities with no viable gospel witness.

**The authority ground:** The Commission is grounded in Christ's *authority* — "all authority has been given to me." The church goes not on its own initiative or cultural confidence but under the mandate and authority of the risen Lord. This grounds evangelism in theology, not in personality or cultural moment.

| **Sin** | Establishes *why* the gospel is needed — the human condition is not fixable from within; the diagnosis precedes the cure |
| **Anthropology** | Establishes *who* needs it — every human bears God's image and is therefore infinitely valuable; every human is fallen and therefore in genuine need |
| **Atonement** | Establishes *what* the gospel accomplished — the cross is not a tragedy but the act by which God reconciles sinners to himself |
| **Justification** | Establishes *how* a person receives it — by grace through faith alone, not by moral performance |
| **Grace** | Establishes the *tone* of proclamation — the gospel is good news precisely because it is unearned; the evangelist is a beggar telling other beggars where the bread is |
| **Faith** | Establishes *what is asked of the hearer* — not intellectual assent alone but trust, commitment, reliance |
| **Regeneration** | Establishes *what happens* when someone responds — not merely a decision but a new birth; the Spirit's work precedes and enables the response |
| **Christology** | Establishes *who Jesus is* that makes his death and resurrection the hinge of history — not merely a moral teacher but the incarnate God |
| **Resurrection** | Establishes *that the gospel is true* — the resurrection is the historical vindication of Jesus's claims; without it, the gospel is wishful thinking |
| **Trinity** | Establishes *the God into whose name people are baptized* — the Great Commission's Trinitarian formula is not incidental |
| **Theology Proper** | Establishes *who is speaking* — the God who sends the church is the God who created all things, to whom all people owe allegiance |
| **Holy Spirit** | Establishes *how the gospel takes hold* — the Spirit convicts of sin, regenerates, and enables faith; evangelism is not manipulation but cooperation with the Spirit's work |
| **Scripture & Revelation** | Establishes *the authority of the message* — the gospel is not the evangelist's opinion but the Word of God; the content is given, not invented |
| **Covenant Theology** | Establishes *the continuity of the story* — the gospel is the fulfillment of God's long covenant purposes, not a late addition; Abraham, Moses, David all pointed toward it |
| **Law & Gospel** | Establishes *the sequence of proclamation* — the law exposes the problem; the gospel announces the solution; both are necessary |
| **Sovereignty & Free Will** | Addresses *why we go* — God's sovereignty grounds confidence; human responsibility grounds urgency |
| **Ecclesiology** | Establishes *who carries it* — the church is not the building or the institution but the community of witnesses |
| **Sanctification** | Establishes *what comes after* — the goal of evangelism is not a decision but a disciple; the Great Commission includes "teaching them to obey everything I have commanded" |
| **Eschatology** | Establishes *the stakes* — judgment is real, eternity is real, the urgency of proclamation is grounded in what is coming |
| **Social Ethics / Vocation** | Establishes *the context* — believers carry the gospel into every sphere of life, not only in formal evangelistic settings |
| **Suffering & Providence** | Addresses *the obstacle* — suffering is real, God is sovereign, and the proclamation of hope in the midst of suffering is itself an act of witness |
| **Problem of Evil** | Addresses *the question that blocks the door* — many people cannot hear the gospel until the suffering question is engaged honestly |
| **Assurance** | Addresses *the evangelist's own foundation* — you cannot give away what you do not have; the witness flows from the certainty of what God has done |

## Scripture Anchors

- **Matt 28:18–20** — the Great Commission; the Trinitarian mandate; the scope is all nations; the goal is disciples, not decisions
- **John 14:6** — "I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me" — the exclusivity claim from Jesus himself
- **Acts 4:12** — "Salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved" — Peter's proclamation under pressure
- **Romans 1:16** — "I am not ashamed of the gospel, because it is the power of God that brings salvation to everyone who believes: first to the Jew, then to the Gentile" — the gospel's power is intrinsic, not dependent on the messenger's eloquence
- **Romans 10:14–15** — "How can they believe in the one of whom they have not heard? And how can they hear without someone preaching to them?" — the necessity of proclamation; the chain from sending to faith
- **1 Corinthians 15:3–4** — Paul's summary of the gospel as of "first importance": Christ died for our sins according to the Scriptures, was buried, was raised on the third day — the irreducible historical core
- **2 Corinthians 5:18–20** — "God was reconciling the world to himself in Christ, not counting people's sins against them... we are therefore Christ's ambassadors, as though God were making his appeal through us" — the ambassador model of evangelism; God's appeal through human voices
- **1 Peter 3:15** — "Always be prepared to give an answer to everyone who asks you to give the reason for the hope that you have. But do this with gentleness and respect" — evangelism as reasoned defense; the posture is gentleness and respect, not combativeness
- **Isaiah 52:7** — "How beautiful on the mountains are the feet of those who bring good news, who proclaim peace, who bring good tidings, who proclaim salvation" — Paul quotes this in Romans 10; the gospel as announcement, not argument

## Theologian Map

**John Stott (1921–2011)**
The definitive 20th-century evangelical voice on evangelism and mission. *Basic Christianity* (1958) remains one of the clearest presentations of the gospel ever written. *Christian Mission in the Modern World* (1975) articulated the relationship between evangelism and social responsibility with more precision than anyone before or since. As principal architect of the Lausanne Covenant (1974), Stott gave the global evangelical movement its clearest missiological framework: evangelism has primacy, social action is its necessary companion, neither can be abandoned without loss. His *The Cross of Christ* (1986) grounds evangelism in the atonement with full theological depth. Stott models the combination of theological rigor, cultural engagement, and personal humility that the best evangelical witness requires.

**Timothy Keller (1950–2023)**
The most important contemporary evangelical voice on evangelism in a secular, post-Christian urban context. *The Reason for God* (2008) addresses the intellectual objections secular people actually bring; *Center Church* (2012) is the fullest treatment of gospel-centered ministry and missional theology. Keller's key move: the gospel is not the entry point into Christianity that you then leave behind for deeper theology — it is the center from which everything else is understood and practiced. His contextualization approach: take the culture's deepest aspirations seriously, show where they lead, and demonstrate that only the gospel satisfies them at their root. Keller integrates Stott's word/deed balance with Reformed theology and urban missiology.

**Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906–1945)**
The theologian of costly witness. *The Cost of Discipleship* (1937) — its opening pages on cheap grace and costly grace — is the most searching account of what genuine evangelism costs the proclaimer. Cheap grace is the preaching of forgiveness without repentance, baptism without church discipline, absolution without confession — "grace without the cross, grace without Jesus Christ, living and incarnate." Costly grace is the grace that calls people to follow, and that call to follow may lead to Flossenbürg. Bonhoeffer's own life — executed by the Nazis in April 1945 — is the enactment of what he wrote. No theologian makes clearer that authentic witness is inseparable from authentic life.

**Charles Spurgeon (1834–1892)**
The greatest evangelical preacher of the 19th century and a model of how Calvinist soteriology and evangelistic urgency belong together. Spurgeon believed in election with full Calvinist conviction and preached to sinners with full evangelistic urgency — and saw no contradiction. His "Please come" sermons and his open invitations to faith demonstrate that high Calvinism and fervent evangelism are not only compatible but naturally connected. His *Lectures to My Students* remains a standard text on the minister's calling. The Metropolitan Tabernacle under Spurgeon also maintained significant social ministry — almshouses, orphanages, a college — demonstrating the word/deed integration before Lausanne named it.

**Karl Barth (1886–1968)**
The theologian of *missio Dei* — the mission of God. Barth's contribution to missiology is indirect but foundational: mission originates not in the church's activity or the Great Commission but in the nature of God himself, who is a sending God — the Father sends the Son, the Father and Son send the Spirit, and the Spirit sends the church. Mission is participation in God's own movement toward the world. Jürgen Moltmann extended this: "It is not the church that has a mission of salvation to fulfill in the world; it is the mission of the Son and the Spirit through the Father that includes the church." This grounds evangelism not in human initiative or organizational strategy but in the character of God.

**Gary Habermas (1950–) and William Lane Craig (1949–)**
The apologetic dimension of evangelism. Habermas and Craig have established the minimal facts case for the resurrection — the historical evidence for the empty tomb and resurrection appearances that is accepted even by most critical non-Christian scholars. Their work answers the prior question that blocks many people from hearing the gospel: is any of this historically credible? The resurrection is the historical hinge (1 Cor 15:14 — "if Christ has not been raised, our preaching is useless and so is your faith"). Making a reasoned case for it is not a distraction from evangelism; it clears the road.

**N.T. Wright (1948–)**
The Kingdom-gospel corrective. Wright's *Simply Good News* (2015) and his broader work argue that the Western evangelical church has reduced the gospel to a personal salvation transaction when the NT announces something larger: the arrival of God's Kingdom, the defeat of the powers of evil, death, and sin, and the beginning of the renewal of all creation. For Wright, evangelism is the announcement that Jesus is Lord — not just Lord of individuals' hearts but Lord of the cosmos. This does not replace the need for personal repentance and faith; it embeds it in a larger story that makes it more, not less, compelling.

## Entry Points

- **"How do I share my faith without being pushy?"** — the posture question; 1 Pet 3:15 (gentleness and respect); Stott on witness as invitation not coercion; Keller on asking questions before making arguments
- **"Is Christianity really the only way?"** — the exclusivity question; John 14:6; Acts 4:12; why the claim belongs to Jesus, not to Christians; the inclusivist/exclusivist debate; Stott and Keller
- **"What exactly is the gospel? What am I supposed to say?"** — the content question; 1 Cor 15:3–4; the Kingdom vs. justification framing; Keller's synthesis; Stott's *Basic Christianity*
- **"Shouldn't we just live good lives instead of talking about religion?"** — the word/deed tension; why a life without words doesn't communicate the gospel; Stott's Lausanne balance
- **"If God is sovereign, why does it matter whether I share my faith?"** — the sovereignty/evangelism tension; Spurgeon; Romans 10:14; God works through means
- **"I'm scared to talk about my faith"** — the fear question; Bonhoeffer on costly grace; Romans 1:16 (Paul "not ashamed"); the ambassador model of 2 Cor 5
- **"What about people who've never heard the gospel?"** — the unevangelized question; the inclusivist debate; Romans 1:18–20; the urgency of Romans 10:14
- **"Isn't it arrogant to say Jesus is the only way?"** — the cultural challenge; the difference between arrogance and faithfulness; the historical grounding of the claim; Habermas/Craig
- **Scholars:** Stott vs. the social gospel on word/deed; the Lausanne Covenant; Wright's Kingdom-gospel critique of evangelical reductionism; Barth's *missio Dei*; the inclusivist debate (Pinnock vs. the Reformed position)

## Failure Modes

- **Cheap grace** — Bonhoeffer's term: proclaiming forgiveness without repentance, grace without the cross, a decision without discipleship; the most common evangelical failure
- **Bait and switch** — building relational trust and then deploying it as a technique; people sense the manipulation and it discredits the message
- **Gospel reduction** — presenting only the personal-salvation dimension while losing the Kingdom, the Lordship of Christ, and the cosmic scope; produces thin converts
- **Gospel inflation** — expanding "the gospel" to include every social concern until it loses its specific content about Jesus's death and resurrection; the social gospel error
- **Triumphalism** — presenting the exclusivity claim with contempt for other traditions; drives people away from the thing they're being invited to consider
- **Silence** — the failure that never gets named as a failure: never actually saying anything; living a decent life and hoping people figure it out; violates Romans 10:14
- **Apologetics without love** — winning arguments and losing people; 1 Cor 13 applies to evangelism; the most brilliant defense of the resurrection delivered with contempt is counterproductive
- **Sovereignty as passivity** — using Reformed soteriology as a reason not to evangelize; Spurgeon's rebuke
