# Interfaith Entry Points: Recognition and Routing Guide

**Purpose:** Users arrive at TES from many starting points. Some come not from within Christianity but from another religious tradition — or from no tradition at all. This guide prepares TES to recognize those starting points, honor what the person brings, and route them to the theological content that most directly engages their actual question.

**The operating principle:** TES does not argue people into Christianity. It presents what Christians actually believe and why, honestly and without condescension, and trusts the person to engage it. Users from other traditions deserve the same quality of engagement as users already inside the Christian conversation — which means taking their tradition seriously, not caricaturing it, and naming real differences honestly rather than papering over them.

**Format per tradition:** What this person likely brings → Points of genuine contact → Points of sharpest tension → Relevant TES nodes → Tier 1 voices to route to → Register guidance → Flags.

## Islam

### What this person likely brings

A strong monotheism (*tawhid*) — the absolute oneness and incomparability of God; the Quran as the direct, uncorrupted word of God; Muhammad as the final prophet; Jesus (*Isa*) as a prophet, born of a virgin, who performed miracles, but who is not divine, did not die on the cross, and was not resurrected in the Christian sense; a profound commitment to prayer, fasting, charity, and submission (*islam*) to God's will; possibly a view that the Bible has been corrupted (*tahrif*) from its original form; respect for Abraham, Moses, and the prophets; a sense that Christianity's Trinity doctrine is *shirk* — associating partners with God, the one unforgivable sin.

### Points of genuine contact

- **Monotheism:** Islam and Christianity share the conviction that there is one God, Creator of all things, who is holy, sovereign, and morally serious — who will judge humanity. This is not a small overlap.
- **Jesus (*Isa*):** The Quran affirms the virgin birth, Jesus's miracles (including raising the dead), his sinlessness, and his unique role as the Word of God (*Kalimat Allah*) and Spirit from God (*Ruh min Allah* — 4:171). This gives TES a genuine starting point in what Muslims already believe about Jesus.
- **Moral seriousness:** Both traditions take seriously the reality of sin, the obligation to live justly, and the importance of prayer as genuine communication with God.
- **Abraham:** The Abrahamic connection is real. A Muslim coming to TES is already oriented toward a God who speaks, commands, and acts in history.

### Points of sharpest tension

**The Trinity (*shirk*):** The Muslim objection to the Trinity is not a misunderstanding — it is a theological position. Islam reads "God is one" to mean that God cannot be three persons; Trinitarian language sounds to a Muslim like the worship of three gods. The Christian response is not to deny God's oneness but to explain what Christians actually mean: not three gods but one God in three persons, with "person" (Greek *hypostasis*) meaning a distinct mode of subsistence within the one divine being, not a separate divine being. The Cappadocians, Barth, and Moltmann are the relevant voices; the key move is explaining what the Trinity is before defending it.

**The Incarnation:** Islam holds that God cannot become a creature — this would compromise divine transcendence. The Christian claim is that the Incarnation does not compromise God's transcendence but reveals God's freedom: God is so fully himself that he can freely take on human nature without ceasing to be God. Athanasius, Cyril, and Barth are the relevant voices.

**The crucifixion:** The Quran states that Jesus was not crucified — "it was made to appear so to them" (4:157). For Christianity, the crucifixion is not incidental but central: the atonement requires the death of Jesus. The historical question of the crucifixion (attested in Roman, Jewish, and Christian sources within decades of the event) is where Habermas and Craig are relevant. The theological question of why the cross matters is where Anselm, Stott, and Aulén speak.

**Scripture:** If the Bible has been corrupted, the Christian case cannot get off the ground from Scripture. The historical evidence — the manuscript tradition, the Dead Sea Scrolls, the early citations — suggests the text of Scripture has been transmitted with high fidelity. Warfield and Vanhoozer address the theological account of Scripture; the textual-reliability question is more an apologetics point that can be handled factually.

### TES Node Map

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Trinity | The *shirk* objection is the sharpest theological challenge; the Christian doctrine of God's unity-in-three-persons must be explained before it can be discussed |
| Christology | The Incarnation — can God become human? What did Jesus's divine-human nature mean? Athanasius, Cyril, and the Chalcedonian definition |
| Atonement | Why did Jesus need to die? The Muslim view is that God simply forgives; the Christian view is that the cross accomplishes something that mere declaration cannot — this is where Anselm, Stott, and Aulén are most relevant |
| Resurrection | The historical question: did Jesus rise? Habermas and Craig; the crucifixion is the prior question (which Islam denies) |
| Scripture & Revelation | The corruption charge (*tahrif*) and the Christian account of Scripture's reliability and authority; Warfield, Vanhoozer |
| Theology Proper | The nature of God — transcendence, holiness, sovereignty; Charnock and Tozer offer the deepest devotional-theological engagement with the God Muslims also honor |

### Tier 1 Voices

**Primary:** Athanasius (Christology — the divine identity of Jesus); Barth (Trinity, revelation — the God who freely speaks and acts); Habermas and Craig (Resurrection — the historical case); Anselm and Stott (Atonement — why the cross)

**Secondary:** Warfield and Vanhoozer (Scripture); Charnock and Tozer (Theology Proper — common ground on the holiness and majesty of God)

### Register Guidance

- Begin with genuine respect for Islamic monotheism and moral seriousness — these are not nothing, and TES should not treat them as if they were
- Never use *shirk* as an accusation; explain the Trinity from within before defending it
- Be honest that the crucifixion question is the sharpest historical disagreement — don't sidestep it
- The best move when a Muslim asks about Christianity: start with what Islam and Christianity share about God's holiness and human accountability, then ask "what would it take for God to be both just and merciful to sinful people?" — this is the question the atonement answers
- Never suggest Islam is merely a deficient or corrupt version of Christianity; it is a distinct tradition with its own theological integrity, which is precisely why the differences matter

## Judaism

### What this person likely brings

The Hebrew Scriptures (Tanakh — Torah, Prophets, Writings) as the foundation; the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob; the covenant with Israel; the Shema (*Hear O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one*) as the central theological affirmation; the mitzvot (613 commandments) as the framework for a life of covenant faithfulness; possibly the Talmudic tradition; a view that the Messiah has not yet come (because the Messianic promises — peace, the ingathering of exiles, the rebuilding of the Temple, universal knowledge of God — have not been fulfilled); a sense that the Trinity and the deification of Jesus are incompatible with Jewish monotheism; possibly significant historical weight: centuries of Christian antisemitism, persecution, and coerced conversion are part of what "Christianity" means to many Jewish people.

### Points of genuine contact

This is the most theologically proximate tradition to Christianity — the entire Old Testament is shared Scripture, the God of both traditions is the same God, and Jesus and his earliest followers were Jewish. The overlap is deep:

- **The Hebrew Scriptures:** Christianity accepts the Tanakh as authoritative and canonical (as the Old Testament). The covenants, the promises, the prophets, the Psalms are common ground.
- **The God of Israel:** The God Christians worship is the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob — not a different God. The attributes of God — holiness, faithfulness, justice, mercy, sovereignty — are shared theological territory.
- **Messianic expectation:** Both traditions await or have awaited a Messiah. The disagreement is whether Jesus is that Messiah, not whether a Messiah was promised.
- **The moral law:** The Ten Commandments, the prophetic call to justice, the neighbor-love commandment (Lev 19:18) — all are common ground.

### Points of sharpest tension

**Did Jesus fulfill the Messianic prophecies?** The Jewish objection is coherent: the Hebrew prophets described a Messiah who would establish peace among the nations, end exile, rebuild the Temple, and bring universal knowledge of God. Jesus did not visibly accomplish these things. The Christian response — a two-coming framework in which the first coming accomplishes atonement and the second accomplishes the visible kingdom — is not a dodge but a genuine theological claim rooted in the Isaiah 53 servant-suffering tradition and the Psalmist's "my God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" (Ps 22). The dialogue is real.

**The Trinity and Jewish monotheism:** Like Islam, Judaism hears Trinitarian language as the introduction of multiple divine beings into what must be absolute divine unity. The Christian response is the same: explain what *hypostasis* and *ousia* mean before defending the doctrine.

**The Torah and the New Covenant:** Christianity's claim that the Mosaic law has been fulfilled in Christ — that the sacrificial system pointed forward to the cross, that the dietary and purity laws are no longer binding on Gentile believers — sounds to Jewish ears like the abolition of the covenant. The Christian account (the Law is fulfilled, not abolished; the moral law remains; the ceremonial law was typological) requires careful engagement.

**The church and Israel:** This is the most complex internal Christian debate with the highest stakes for Jewish-Christian dialogue. Covenant theology (Robertson, Vos) sees the church as the true Israel, the fulfillment of the Abrahamic covenant through Christ. Dispensationalism (Darby, Ryrie) maintains a distinct future for national Israel and sees the promises to Israel as not yet fulfilled. Jewish readers deserve to know this is a live debate within Christianity, not settled.

**The weight of history:** Before any theology: the Crusades, the Inquisition, forced conversions, pogroms, and the silence of much of the church during the Holocaust — this is the historical context Jewish people bring to any encounter with Christianity. TES must acknowledge this, not defensively, but honestly.

### TES Node Map

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Covenant Theology | The central question: what is the relationship between the Old Covenant and the New? Is the church Israel? What happens to the promises made to Abraham and David? Robertson vs. Ryrie; Vos on biblical theology |
| Christology | The Messianic claim: is Jesus the fulfillment of the Hebrew prophets? Isaiah 53, Psalm 22, Daniel 7, Micah 5:2; Athanasius and the patristic account of Jesus's divine identity |
| Atonement | The sacrificial system of the Torah and its fulfillment; why Christians believe the cross accomplishes what the Temple sacrifices pointed toward; Anselm, Stott, Aulén |
| Trinity | Jewish monotheism and the Trinitarian claim; same as the Muslim question but with Scriptural common ground available |
| Eschatology | The promises to Israel — how are they fulfilled? Already/not-yet framework; the relationship between Ladd's inaugurated eschatology and the Messianic promises |
| Scripture & Revelation | The relationship between the two Testaments; the Christian account of typology and fulfillment vs. the Jewish account of the plain sense of the text |

### Tier 1 Voices

**Primary:** Robertson and Vos (Covenant Theology — the theological relationship between the covenants is the central question); Athanasius (Christology — Jesus's identity); Ladd (Eschatology — the already/not-yet framework that addresses the "why hasn't the Messiah accomplished the visible kingdom?" question); Anselm and Stott (Atonement — the sacrificial fulfillment)

**Secondary:** Darby and Ryrie (for the dispensational account of Israel's distinct future — relevant if the user asks about whether Christianity has simply absorbed and replaced Israel)

### Register Guidance

- Most Jewish users arrive simply curious about what Christians believe — about the Messiah, the Trinity, the relationship between the Testaments. Begin there, not with historical apologetics.
- Never speak of Judaism as simply a "preparation" for Christianity in a dismissive way; it is a living tradition with its own integrity and its own continuing encounter with the God of Israel
- The Messianic question is the most productive entry point: "What would you need to see in a Messiah?" This honors the Jewish framework before making the Christian claim
- The Torah question requires pastoral care: do not give the impression that the Old Testament has been discarded; the Christian claim is fulfillment, not replacement
- The Covenant Theology / Dispensationalism debate is worth noting explicitly when a Jewish user raises the "has Christianity replaced Israel?" question — this is an area of genuine Christian disagreement
- If historical pain surfaces — references to the Crusades, the Inquisition, pogroms, or the church's silence during the Holocaust — acknowledge it honestly and without defensiveness. Don't preemptively lead with historical apology; for a user who just wants to understand what Christians believe about the Messiah, that can feel like a detour or even patronizing.

**Flag:** Historically sensitive, but do not assume every Jewish user carries that weight into the conversation. Respond to what is actually present rather than preemptively managing what might be.

## Buddhism

### What this person likely brings

Depending on tradition (Theravada, Mahayana, Zen, Tibetan — these are genuinely different): the Four Noble Truths (life is marked by *dukkha*/suffering; suffering arises from craving and attachment; suffering can cease; there is a path to its cessation); the Eightfold Path as the practice of liberation; the teaching of *anatta* (no permanent self — the "self" is a constructed aggregation, not a substantial entity); *anicca* (impermanence — everything is in flux); *nirvana* as the cessation of craving and release from *samsara* (the cycle of rebirth); compassion (*karuna*) as a central virtue; a non-theistic framework (the Buddha did not affirm a Creator God and regarded metaphysical speculation as a distraction from practice); possibly a sophisticated philosophical tradition (Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka, Yogacara) that has thought deeply about consciousness, the self, and reality.

### Points of genuine contact

- **Suffering:** Both Buddhism and Christianity begin with the reality and seriousness of suffering. The Four Noble Truths and the cross both take suffering with complete seriousness. This is a genuine point of contact, not a superficial one.
- **The inadequacy of the ego:** Buddhism's diagnosis that the constructed ego/self is the source of suffering has resonance with the Christian account of sin as self-centeredness, the turn inward (*incurvatus in se* — Augustine), the pride that refuses to receive oneself as dependent on God.
- **Compassion:** Buddhist *karuna* and Christian *agape* are not identical, but both traditions center on a disposition of care toward those who suffer. Nouwen's "wounded healer" tradition, in particular, has resonance with Buddhist compassion.
- **The limits of the ego's project:** Both Buddhism and Christianity resist the modern Western project of self-creation and self-sufficiency. Both say: the self cannot save itself.

### Points of sharpest tension

**The self:** Buddhism teaches *anatta* — there is no permanent, substantial self. Christianity teaches that the human person is a genuine individual who bears the image of God, who will be raised bodily, and who exists in eternal relationship with God. The resurrection — the restoration of the individual person — is the sharpest contrast. For Buddhism, the goal is release from individuality into nirvana; for Christianity, the goal is the glorification of the individual person in resurrection communion with God.

**God:** Buddhism is non-theistic in its foundational texts. The Buddha explicitly set aside questions about the Creator God as unhelpful for liberation. Christianity is irreducibly theistic — the God who creates, speaks, acts in history, and raises Jesus from the dead is not an optional addition. The Theology Proper node is directly relevant here.

**Salvation by grace vs. liberation by practice:** Buddhism's soteriological framework is path-based: liberation comes through right practice, meditation, and the gradual dissolution of craving. Christianity's central claim is that salvation is given, not achieved — it is God's act in Christ, received by faith, not earned through practice. The Atonement and Grace nodes speak to this most directly. (Note: Pure Land Buddhism, with its devotion to Amitabha Buddha and the gift of rebirth in the Pure Land, is the form of Buddhist soteriology closest to Christian grace — worth noting if the user comes from that tradition.)

**Karma vs. grace:** The karmic framework (actions produce consequences that extend across lifetimes) and the Christian framework of grace (God forgives and does not give us what our actions deserve) are not merely different accounts of the same thing; they represent different accounts of the moral structure of reality.

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Anthropology | The self — *anatta* vs. the image of God; what a person is and what their final destiny is |
| Suffering & Providence | The Four Noble Truths and the cross; how Christianity addresses suffering differently from Buddhism (not release from attachment but redemption through the suffering one) |
| Problem of Evil | For Buddhist users who are asking the intellectual question about suffering and God's existence |
| Grace | The practice/gift contrast; salvation as something received, not achieved |
| Atonement | Why does liberation require something done *for* us rather than a path *we* walk? |
| Eschatology | Resurrection vs. nirvana as the end; the individual's destiny |
| Theology Proper | The existence and nature of a personal Creator God — the foundational question that Buddhism sets aside and Christianity cannot |

### Tier 1 Voices

**Primary:** Lewis (suffering — *The Problem of Pain*; *A Grief Observed* — speaks to Buddhist users who are serious about suffering); Barth (Anthropology and Theology Proper — the relational account of the person and of God's personal self-disclosure); Keller (Grace — and the cultural apologetics that speaks to a Buddhist's suspicion of Western Christianity's individualism); Nouwen (the contemplative tradition that has genuine resonance with Buddhist meditative practice)

**Secondary:** Wolterstorff (lament — the Christian alternative to the Buddhist release-from-attachment response to grief); Augustine (the *incurvatus in se* account of sin has the deepest resonance with Buddhist ego-analysis); Plantinga (Problem of Evil — for the intellectually-oriented Buddhist asking the theodicy question)

### Register Guidance

- Begin by taking Buddhism seriously as a system: "Buddhism has thought more carefully about suffering and the problem of the self than almost any other tradition." Then name where the paths diverge.
- The self question is the deepest: explore it carefully rather than dismissing *anatta*. The Christian account of the person as the *imago Dei* — a relational identity, not a self-contained ego — is not simply the Western individualism Buddhism rightly critiques.
- Do not present Christianity as simply "Buddhism with a God added" — they are genuinely different responses to the human situation
- The suffering point of contact is the best entry: both traditions begin here; ask "what does suffering mean, and what can be done about it?" — then show how the cross and resurrection answer differently from the Eightfold Path
- For meditatively-oriented users: acknowledge that Christian contemplative tradition (the Desert Fathers, Thomas Merton, Nouwen) has its own deep practice-formation tradition, though with a different theological framework

## Hinduism

### What this person likely brings

Hinduism is enormously diverse — far more so than the other traditions here. A user from a Vaishnavite (devotion to Vishnu/Krishna) background will bring very different sensibilities than one from an Advaita Vedanta (non-dualist) background. The following covers the most common frameworks:

**Theistic Hinduism (Vaishnavism, Shaivism, Shaktism):** A personal God or goddess (*Ishvara*, Vishnu, Shiva, Devi) to whom devotion (*bhakti*) is offered; the reality of divine grace (*prasad*); avatars of the divine who descend into the world (the ten avatars of Vishnu, including Rama and Krishna); the Bhagavad Gita as a central scripture; karma and dharma as the moral-cosmic framework; *moksha* (liberation) as the goal, achieved through *bhakti*, *jnana* (knowledge), or *karma yoga* (action without attachment to results); reincarnation across many lifetimes.

**Non-dualist Hinduism (Advaita Vedanta):** *Brahman* (ultimate reality) is identical with *Atman* (the individual self) — *Aham Brahmasmi* ("I am Brahman"); the individual ego-self is *maya* (illusion); liberation (*moksha*) is the recognition of one's identity with Brahman; Shankara is the great systematic exponent; personal gods are *saguna Brahman* (Brahman with attributes) — pedagogically useful but ultimately provisional.

### Points of genuine contact

- **Theistic Hinduism and Christianity:** The *bhakti* (devotion) tradition — personal love for and devotion to a personal God who is lovable and who responds to human love — has genuine resonance with Christian worship. Vaishnavism in particular offers devotional poetry, music, and an account of divine love that Christians can recognize as pointing toward something real.
- **The avatar and the Incarnation:** Vishnu's avatars — divine descents into the created world for the purpose of restoring *dharma* — are the Hindu concept most proximate to the Incarnation, and Hindu users often make this comparison themselves. The comparison is worth engaging honestly: there are real similarities (the divine enters the human realm) and real differences (the Incarnation is unique, unrepeatable, historical, and accomplishes atonement — it is not one in a series of divine descents).
- **The inadequacy of the material world as final:** Both Hinduism and Christianity resist the view that the material world of transient pleasures and sorrows is the ultimate reality. Both traditions say: there is more.
- **Karma and moral accountability:** The karmic framework reflects a real intuition — that actions have consequences, that the moral universe is not arbitrary. Christianity affirms moral accountability while providing a different account of how it is resolved (atonement vs. karmic working-out across lifetimes).

### Points of sharpest tension

**Monotheism vs. polytheism (or monism):** Christianity is strictly monotheistic; while Hinduism contains monotheistic strands (especially in Vaishnavism), it also encompasses polytheism and monism in ways Christianity cannot affirm. The Creator/creature distinction — which is fundamental to Christian theology — is denied by Advaita Vedanta (*Brahman = Atman*).

**The uniqueness of the Incarnation:** The avatar tradition is the most important comparison point, and also the most important point of divergence: Christian theology claims that the Incarnation of Jesus Christ is unique, not one in a series of divine descents. Jesus is not another avatar. The cross and resurrection accomplish something singular and unrepeatable.

**Reincarnation vs. resurrection:** Christianity teaches that each person lives once, dies once, and faces judgment (Heb 9:27); the hope is resurrection — the restoration of the individual person in a glorified body. This is categorically different from the Hindu cycle of rebirth (*samsara*) across many lifetimes. The Eschatology and Anthropology nodes are directly relevant.

**Many paths vs. one:** Many Hindus hold a generous religious epistemology — "all paths lead to the same summit." Christianity's claim that Jesus is "the way, the truth, and the life" and that "no one comes to the Father except through him" (John 14:6) is experienced as exclusivist and even offensive. This is worth addressing directly rather than softening it: Christianity does make a unique claim, and the question is whether that claim is true.

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Theology Proper | The nature of God — personal, Creator, distinct from creation; monotheism and the Creator/creature distinction vs. Advaita's Brahman = Atman |
| Christology | The Incarnation and the avatar comparison — real similarities, real differences; the uniqueness of the Incarnation |
| Trinity | For users curious about how Christianity maintains monotheism while affirming three persons |
| Creation | *Ex nihilo* creation (God creates out of nothing, distinct from creation) vs. emanation or cyclical cosmology |

| Atonement | Why the cross is not analogous to karmic working-out: God bears the moral consequence, not the individual across future lifetimes |

### Tier 1 Voices

**Primary:** Barth (Theology Proper and Anthropology — the Creator/creature distinction and the relational account of the human person); Athanasius (Christology — the uniqueness of the Incarnation); Aquinas (Creation — *ex nihilo* and the metaphysical Creator/creature distinction); Ladd (Eschatology — the resurrection hope as the alternative to reincarnation)

**Secondary:** Lewis (the common-ground apologetics that a Hindu devotional-tradition user often resonates with; *Mere Christianity*'s moral law argument is culturally available); Keller (for the cultural apologetics that addresses the "many paths" view); Volf (Anthropology — the relational person as image of the Trinity)

### Register Guidance

- Do not collapse Hinduism into a single system; ask what tradition the user comes from before assuming
- The *bhakti* user deserves recognition: their devotion to a personal God is not nothing; it is pointing somewhere real, even if the full picture differs
- The avatar comparison is worth taking seriously before naming the difference: "Hinduism's avatar tradition reflects a genuine intuition that the divine can enter the world — Christianity's claim is that this happened once, definitively, and not as one in a series"
- For Advaita Vedanta users: engage the philosophical seriousness of the tradition before the theological difference; the *Aham Brahmasmi* claim and the Christian claim that the creature is genuinely distinct from and loved by the Creator are not just different metaphysics — they generate different accounts of personhood, relationship, and what love even means
- The "many paths" view is worth engaging: ask "if all paths lead to the same summit, what is that summit?" — and then show how different the destinations actually are

## Secular / Atheist / Agnostic

*This is the most common non-Christian entry point and is already substantially covered by the Bridge Voice system (Lewis, Peterson) and the Faith and Problem of Evil nodes. This entry is brief.*

### What this person likely brings

No religious framework; possibly active skepticism about religious claims; the sense that science has made God unnecessary; the problem of evil as the most serious objection to theism; possibly a moral framework without a transcendent grounding; possibly genuine openness to the questions — they arrived at TES for a reason.

### Points of genuine contact

- The moral sense: virtually everyone has it; the question of its grounding is the aperture
- The questions of meaning, purpose, and death — these do not go away in a secular framework

### TES Node Map

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Faith | The rationality of Christian belief; Plantinga's properly basic belief and sensus divinitatis; Lewis's Moral Law argument |
| Problem of Evil | The most serious intellectual objection; Plantinga's free will defense; Lewis (*The Problem of Pain*); Wolterstorff (lament) |
| Anthropology | The question of what a person is — what grounds human dignity if not the image of God? |
| Resurrection | The historical question: what happened? Habermas and Craig |

### Tier 1 Voices

Lewis (Faith and Problem of Evil — the canonical entry point; *Mere Christianity*, *The Problem of Pain*); Plantinga (Faith and Problem of Evil — the philosophical account); Keller (*The Reason for God* — the contemporary cultural apologetics for a secular audience); Habermas and Craig (Resurrection — the historical question is often the most productive entry for skeptics)

### Bridge Voices

Peterson (pre-threshold — for users who have engaged Peterson's biblical lectures and are warming to the idea that the Christian narrative addresses real questions); Lewis (also functions as a Bridge Voice for intellectually-oriented skeptics)

### Register Guidance

- Do not assume hostility; a secular user who arrives at TES is already curious
- Lead with questions, not answers: "What would it take for a claim like this to be worth taking seriously?"
- The Problem of Evil is the most frequent entry point; take it seriously before responding; don't rush to the free will defense
- The Resurrection is often the most productive specific question: "Did Jesus rise from the dead?" is answerable in historical terms, which is the secular user's preferred mode of inquiry

## Mormonism (The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints)

### What this person likely brings

The most important thing for TES to recognize at the outset: an LDS user arrives using Christian vocabulary — Jesus, salvation, scripture, grace, covenant, prayer — but with substantially different meanings attached to those words. They may think they are very close to traditional Christianity and may not realize how different the foundational commitments actually are. The vocabulary overlap is the source of most confusion in LDS-Christian dialogue; TES should clarify it early.

Specific beliefs a typical LDS user brings:
- God the Father has a physical, perfected body of flesh and bones; he was once a man who progressed to godhood
- The Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three separate, distinct beings — unified in purpose but not in being
- Jesus is the firstborn spirit child of the Father; the atonement was accomplished primarily in Gethsemane, then completed on the cross
- The Bible is accepted "as far as it is translated correctly"; the Book of Mormon, Doctrine and Covenants, and Pearl of Great Price are additional authoritative scripture that supplement and in places correct it
- All humans pre-existed as spirit children of Heavenly Father and Heavenly Mother before earth life
- Exaltation — becoming gods in the Celestial Kingdom — is available to faithful Latter-day Saints who receive temple ordinances and keep their covenants
- The original Christian church fell into apostasy after the apostles died; priesthood authority was lost until restored through Joseph Smith in 1830
- The LDS church is the one true church with exclusive priesthood authority

### Points of genuine contact

- **Jesus:** An LDS user's devotion to Jesus is real, even if their account of who Jesus is differs fundamentally from Chalcedonian Christology
- **Scripture:** Both traditions take written, authoritative scripture seriously as divine communication (though the content of the canon differs fundamentally)
- **Moral seriousness:** The LDS ethical framework — family, community, honesty, service — reflects genuine commitments that Christians also hold
- **Prayer:** Both traditions practice personal prayer as real communication with a personal God who hears and responds

### Points of sharpest tension

**The nature of God** — this is the foundational difference, from which everything else follows. Classical Christian theology holds that God is spirit: infinite, eternal, incorporeal, without beginning, immutable. This is the unanimous teaching of Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant Christianity from the early councils forward. Mormonism holds that God the Father has a physical body of flesh and bones, and that he was once a man who progressed to godhood. These are not two versions of the same claim. They are incompatible accounts of what God is.

**The Trinity:** The Nicene Creed (381 AD) teaches one God in three persons — one divine being, three distinct modes of subsistence. The LDS church explicitly rejects this: Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three separate beings. By any classical definition this is tritheism, not Trinitarianism. An LDS user saying "I believe in Jesus" means something the Nicene tradition would not recognize as belief in the second person of the Trinity.

**Human nature and deification:** The Lorenzo Snow couplet — "As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may become" — is the sharpest single divergence from classical Christian anthropology. Christianity maintains the Creator/creature distinction absolutely: God is Creator, humans are creatures, and this distinction cannot be collapsed in either direction. LDS theology dissolves it in both directions. This is not a minor variation; it changes the entire structure of what God, humanity, salvation, and eternity mean.

**Scripture and canon:** Christianity holds the biblical canon closed and sufficient. The LDS church holds that God continues to speak through a living prophet and through four books of scripture. Both the Protestant principle of sola scriptura and the Catholic/Orthodox Scripture-and-Tradition principle close the canon; LDS theology fundamentally reopens it.

**Salvation and ordinances:** Mormonism teaches a tiered salvation: universal resurrection is Christ's gift to all; exaltation (the highest degree of glory) requires faith, repentance, LDS baptism, the gift of the Holy Ghost by confirmation, temple endowment, eternal marriage sealing, and ongoing covenant-keeping. This is works-plus-ordinances soteriology, not the grace-through-faith-alone account that defines the Reformation tradition — or even the Catholic account that holds grace as prior and primary.

**Apostasy and authority:** The LDS claim that all of historic Christianity fell into apostasy after the apostles — losing priesthood authority until 1830 — is an ecclesiological claim of the highest order. It means the Nicene Creed, the ecumenical councils, and the entire Christian tradition are the product of an apostate church. TES should name this rather than leaving it implicit.

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Theology Proper | The foundational difference: infinite, eternal, incorporeal God vs. embodied, once-human, progressing God; Charnock and Tozer on the divine attributes |

### Tier 1 Voices

**Primary:** Charnock and Tozer (Theology Proper — the infinite, incorporeal God is the foundational claim LDS theology directly contradicts); Barth and the Cappadocians (Trinity); Athanasius (Christology and the Creator/creature distinction); Warfield and Vanhoozer (Scripture); Luther, Calvin, and Sproul (Salvation & Justification — grace through faith, not works-plus-ordinances)

**Secondary:** Aquinas (the Creator/creature metaphysics that LDS anthropology dissolves); Keller (Grace — the accessible account of grace that speaks to LDS users who feel the weight of covenant performance)

### Register Guidance

- Recognize the genuine devotion to Jesus and the moral seriousness an LDS user brings; it is real and should not be dismissed
- **Clarify vocabulary early.** When an LDS user says "God," "Jesus," "salvation," or "scripture," the words are shared but the content may differ substantially. Make the difference visible rather than assuming shared ground that isn't there.
- Start with Theology Proper, not soteriology: "Before we talk about salvation, it helps to be precise about what Christianity teaches about God himself — because this is where the difference is most fundamental."
- The Lorenzo Snow couplet is often the most surprising point for LDS users: "Christianity teaches something closer to the opposite — not that humans can become God, but that God became human, and that humans are brought into communion with God while remaining creatures."
- The apostasy claim is worth naming but not dwelling on: "Christianity's claim is that the church, though always imperfect, preserved the apostolic faith through Scripture and council — the Nicene Creed is not a corruption of the gospel but the church's faithful articulation of what the apostles taught about Jesus."
- Do not be combative. Most LDS users exploring TES are curious, not defending. Explain classical Christian theology clearly and generously.

**Flag:** The most important flag for LDS users is the vocabulary problem — shared words, different content. TES should surface this early rather than letting a conversation proceed on a false assumption of common ground. The differences are substantial, go to the root, and should be stated honestly. This is not hostility toward LDS faith; it is precision about what traditional Christianity actually claims.

## Jehovah's Witnesses

### What this person likely brings

A rigorous monotheism centered on Jehovah (YHWH) as the one true God; a view of Jesus as God's first and greatest creation — the archangel Michael in his pre-human form, a divine being but not God himself; a conviction that the Trinity is a pagan corruption of the original Christian faith, invented at the Council of Nicaea in 325; a high view of Scripture (specifically the New World Translation, the Watchtower's own rendering); complete trust in the Governing Body of the Watchtower Bible and Tract Society as Jehovah's sole channel of spiritual truth on earth; active door-to-door evangelism as a religious obligation; and a life shaped by significant organizational discipline — no blood transfusions, no military service, no voting, no celebration of birthdays or Christmas or Easter. They bring genuine devotion, significant biblical literacy, and a strong community. They also bring a vocabulary that sounds Christian but carries substantially different content: *Jesus*, *salvation*, *resurrection*, *the Kingdom*, and *eternal life* all mean something different inside Watchtower theology than in historic Christianity.

### Points of Genuine Contact

- **Scripture:** JW's have a high regard for the Bible and take it seriously as God's word — they read it constantly, memorize it, and build their theology from it. This is genuine common ground, even if the NWT translation and the Watchtower's authoritative interpretive role complicate what "Scripture alone" means in practice.
- **Monotheism:** The insistence that there is one God, Jehovah, who is holy, sovereign, and the Creator of all things is real. Christianity agrees completely — the dispute is not about whether God is one but about what the oneness of God means and who Jesus is within it.
- **Jesus as central:** JW's believe in Jesus as a real historical figure, born of a virgin, who performed miracles, died (on a stake, in their view), was raised by Jehovah, and is now ruling in heaven. The person of Jesus is not peripheral to their faith. This gives TES a starting point.
- **The Kingdom of God:** JW's have a strong expectation of a coming Kingdom of God on a restored earth — a physical paradise where the resurrected dead will live. Christianity shares the expectation of a renewed creation and a resurrection of the dead, though the details and the identity of who inherits it differ.
- **Moral seriousness and community:** JW's typically live with significant moral discipline — strong families, abstention from many cultural vices, active community — which reflects genuine values. This should be recognized, not dismissed.

### Points of Sharpest Tension

**Who is Jesus?** This is the central question, and everything else follows from it. JW theology is essentially a revival of Arianism — the 4th-century position condemned at the Council of Nicaea (325) and the Council of Constantinople (381). Arius taught that the Son was the first and greatest of God's creations — divine, but not co-equal and co-eternal with the Father. JW theology makes the same move: Jesus is "a god" (John 1:1 in the NWT), the highest created being, but not Jehovah. Against this, Athanasius argued his entire career: if Jesus is a creature, then the Incarnation does not bring us into contact with God himself, and the salvation Jesus offers is not the salvation of God but of a creature — which cannot save. The Nicene formula — *homoousios*, "of the same substance" as the Father — is precisely the answer to Arianism. Athanasius is the primary voice; the Cappadocians (Basil, Gregory of Nazianzus, Gregory of Nyssa) completed the work of articulating what the full Trinity means.

**John 1:1 and the NWT:** The Greek reads *kai theos ēn ho logos* — "and the Word was God." The NWT renders this "and the Word was a god" — inserting the indefinite article to support the view that Jesus is a divine being but not God himself. This translation is rejected by virtually every Greek scholar, including non-Christian ones, because Greek uses the absence of the definite article not to indicate "a god" (indefinite) but to indicate the qualitative nature of the noun — the Word fully participates in what God is. The NWT translation is a theological choice embedded in a translation, not a neutral reading of the Greek. This is a concrete, discussable point that does not require hostility.

**The Holy Spirit as person or force:** JW theology holds that the Holy Spirit is not a person but God's active force — like electricity, impersonal. The NT texts, however, describe the Spirit with personal pronouns, personal actions (teaching, grieving, interceding, speaking, forbidding — John 14:26; Eph 4:30; Rom 8:26; Acts 16:6–7), and a personal relationship with both the Father and the Son. The Spirit's personhood is not a later theological invention but is embedded in the NT's own language.

**The soul and death:** JW theology holds that humans do not have immortal souls — at death, the person simply ceases to exist until the resurrection (soul sleep). The 144,000 elect go to heaven as spirit beings; the remaining faithful ("other sheep") are resurrected to life on a restored paradise earth. Historic Christianity holds that the person continues in some form between death and resurrection (2 Cor 5:8; Phil 1:23; Luke 23:43), and that the final state is resurrection — bodily, not purely spiritual — on a renewed earth and heaven. The Anthropology and Eschatology nodes are the relevant landing points.

**The Watchtower as sole interpreter:** For JW's, Scripture cannot be properly understood apart from the Governing Body's guidance — individual interpretation is explicitly discouraged. This is functionally the opposite of the Reformation's *sola scriptura* and the perspicuity of Scripture — that Scripture is clear enough in its central teachings to be read and understood by ordinary believers with the Spirit's help. This is the deepest structural issue: if the Watchtower's authority is granted, no independent engagement with the biblical text is possible. The Scripture & Revelation node is the entry point here.

**1914 and the invisible return:** JW theology holds that Christ began his invisible heavenly rule in 1914 (derived from Russell's calculations based on Daniel 4). This date has been revised repeatedly and has no exegetical basis that mainstream biblical scholarship — evangelical or otherwise — accepts. George Ladd and Oscar Cullmann on the "already/not yet" of the Kingdom are the relevant voices.

| Node | Why it's relevant |
|---|---|
| Trinity | The Arian-revival Christology of JW theology is the direct target of Nicene Trinitarianism; Athanasius and the Cappadocians |
| Christology | Who is Jesus? The *homoousios* claim; John 1:1; the divine identity question |
| Holy Spirit | Personhood of the Spirit vs. impersonal force; the NT evidence |
| Anthropology | Soul sleep vs. intermediate state; what happens at death; the image of God |
| Eschatology | The 144,000 / other sheep distinction; the earthly resurrection; 1914; the already/not yet Kingdom |
| Scripture & Revelation | NWT translation concerns; Watchtower authority vs. *sola scriptura*; perspicuity |

### Tier 1 Voices

**Primary:** Athanasius of Alexandria — his entire career was a refutation of Arianism, which is the theological framework JW Christology inhabits. *De Incarnatione* and his anti-Arian writings are the most direct response to the claim that Jesus is a created divine being. The Cappadocians — Basil, Gregory of Nazianzus, and Gregory of Nyssa — completed the Trinitarian articulation Athanasius began and addressed the personhood of the Holy Spirit directly.

**Secondary:** Warfield and Vanhoozer (Scripture — the NWT translation issue and the question of interpretive authority); George Ladd (Eschatology — the Kingdom of God and why 1914 is not exegetically grounded); C.S. Lewis (Christology — the Trilemma, which presses the question of whether Jesus can be merely a great created being given his own claims); Charnock (Theology Proper — the eternal, self-existent nature of God that the JW account of Jesus's origins contradicts).

### Register Guidance

- Recognize the genuine devotion immediately. JW's who engage TES are often intellectually serious, biblically literate, and genuinely seeking — their commitment to Scripture is real even if the translation and interpretive framework differ.
- **The vocabulary problem is acute.** More so than almost any other tradition, JW's use Christian words with different content. "Jesus," "salvation," "resurrection," "the Kingdom," and "eternal life" must be unpacked early. Don't assume shared meaning.
- Start with Christology, not the Watchtower's authority. The organizational-authority question tends to produce defensiveness; the Christology question — who Jesus actually is — is where the theological substance lies and where genuine engagement is most possible.
- The NWT translation of John 1:1 is a concrete, specific point that can be discussed factually. Don't make it an attack; frame it as: "Most Greek scholars — including many who are not Christian — read this differently, and here's why."
- The Arian controversy is directly relevant and worth naming: "What you're describing about Jesus sounds very similar to what Arius taught in the 4th century, and the Council of Nicaea was specifically a response to that. Athanasius spent his entire career arguing the opposite — here's why it mattered to him."
- Avoid condescension about the Watchtower. For many JW's, the organization is the framework through which they've experienced God, community, and meaning. Critiquing it requires care, not contempt.

**Flag:** The most significant flag for JW users is the vocabulary problem — shared words, different content — combined with the organizational-authority structure, which means any claim TES makes will be weighed against "what the Watchtower teaches." The Christology question (who is Jesus, really?) is the most productive entry point because it is both the deepest theological difference and the one most directly addressed by Scripture and the church's historic response to Arianism.

## Christian Science (Church of Christ, Scientist)

### What this person likely brings

Like Mormonism and Jehovah's Witnesses, a Christian Science user arrives using deeply familiar Christian vocabulary — God, Christ, sin, healing, salvation, prayer, even the Bible itself — while meaning something categorically different by nearly every one of those words. The tradition traces to Mary Baker Eddy (1821–1910) and her 1875 textbook *Science and Health with Key to the Scriptures*, which Christian Scientists read alongside the Bible as its authoritative interpretive key — the "pastor" of Christian Science churches is, formally, these two books read together, not a person. Central convictions a typical user brings: God is best understood not as a personal being but as divine Principle — Eddy's "seven synonyms" for God are Life, Truth, Love, Mind, Spirit, Soul, and Principle; matter, and the material world generally, is not the ultimate reality it appears to be — it is the product of "mortal mind," a kind of collective error in perception, not what God actually made; sin, sickness, and death are, correspondingly, not realities to be overcome by grace or medicine but errors of belief to be corrected through right spiritual understanding — Eddy's system of healing (prayer-based "Christian Science treatment," historically administered by trained "practitioners") is meant to replace, not supplement, medical care; Jesus is deeply honored as the supreme "Way-shower" who demonstrated humanity's true spiritual nature more perfectly than anyone else, but Eddy draws a sharp distinction between "the man Jesus" and "the Christ" — the Christ being the divine idea or principle Jesus demonstrated, not identical with his particular human person. Users often bring genuine devotion, a real and demanding spiritual discipline (daily study of the Bible and *Science and Health*, weekly testimony meetings), and — not infrequently — a personal or family history involving healing (or a healing that didn't happen), which can carry real emotional weight.

### Points of Genuine Contact

- **Scripture:** Christian Scientists read the Bible daily and take it as authoritative — the Christian Science Quarterly assigns weekly Bible readings paired with passages from *Science and Health*. The overlap is genuine even though the interpretive key applied to the text is not one historic Christianity would recognize.
- **The reality and seriousness of suffering:** Eddy's system arose substantially out of her own long history of illness and her search for healing — Christian Science takes human suffering with real seriousness, even though its diagnosis of what suffering actually is differs sharply from the historic Christian account.
- **Devotion to Jesus:** A Christian Scientist's regard for Jesus as the supreme revealer of spiritual truth is genuine, even though the Christology beneath it differs fundamentally from Chalcedonian orthodoxy.
- **The expectation that faith should be lived, not merely believed:** Christian Science's insistence that genuine spiritual understanding should produce visible, practical results (healing, changed circumstances) has a real — if differently grounded — resonance with the biblical expectation that faith without works is dead (James 2:17) and that the gospel is meant to be more than notional assent.

### Points of Sharpest Tension

**Is matter real, or an error of mortal mind?** This is the foundational claim, and nearly everything else in Christian Science follows from it. Eddy taught that the entire material universe — including the human body, disease, and death — is not what God created and not ultimately real; it is the product of "mortal mind," a mistaken, limited mode of perception to be spiritually overcome. This is, structurally, a direct revival of the 2nd-century Gnostic devaluation of matter that Irenaeus spent his career answering (see the Heresy, False Teachers & the Great Apostasy node, Key Tension 3, which names this exact pattern). Irenaeus's answer is the direct response: the God who created the material world declared it "very good" (Gen 1:31), and the Incarnation — God the Son genuinely, permanently taking on real flesh — is God's own confirmation that matter is worth inhabiting, not an error to be escaped. Christian Science and 2nd-century Gnosticism reach the same conclusion (matter is not the deepest reality) from different starting points, which is exactly why Irenaeus's *Against Heresies* remains the most direct historic answer available.

**Who is Jesus — one person, or a man plus an indwelling idea?** Eddy's distinction between "the man Jesus" and "the Christ" — the eternal divine idea Jesus demonstrated more fully than any other person — separates what Chalcedonian Christology insists must be held together: one person, fully God and fully human, without division. This has real structural similarity to ancient separationist Christologies (Cerinthus's teaching, which Irenaeus and John's own writings are traditionally understood to address) that treated "the Christ" as a divine power that descended on the human Jesus at baptism and departed before the crucifixion. Athanasius's soteriological argument is the direct answer: if the one who died and rose is not, in his own single person, both fully God and fully human, then the healing of humanity's condition never actually happens in a body remotely like the ones humans actually have.

**Sin: real transgression, or mistaken belief?** Historic Christianity holds that sin is a genuine moral offense against a personal, holy God — real guilt, requiring real atonement (see the Sin and Atonement nodes). Christian Science holds that sin, like sickness, is fundamentally an error of mortal thought rather than a moral reality requiring forgiveness — the "cure" is corrected understanding, not grace received by faith. This is not a difference of emphasis; it relocates the entire problem from the will and the relationship with God into perception and belief.

**The atonement: an objective transaction, or "at-one-ment" through right understanding?** Eddy reinterprets "atonement" etymologically as "at-one-ment" — reconciliation with God achieved through overcoming the illusions of matter, sin, and death via spiritual understanding, which Jesus demonstrated supremely. This is a genuinely different claim from the classical Christian doctrine that Christ's death is an objective, substitutionary payment for real sin (see Anselm, Stott, Aulén in the Atonement node) — not a demonstration to be imitated but an accomplishment done *for* sinners who could not do it themselves.

**The resurrection: bodily event, or demonstration that Life has no real opposite?** Historic Christianity's claim (1 Cor 15) is that Jesus was bodily, physically raised — a real event in space and time, the historical anchor of the whole gospel (see Habermas and Craig in the Resurrection node). Christian Science treats the resurrection primarily as a demonstration that Life (one of the divine synonyms) has no genuine opposite in death — closer to a proof of a metaphysical principle than a historical, bodily event. This is a substantially different claim about what actually happened and why it matters.

**Medical care and the practical stakes.** Unlike most of the tensions above, this one is not merely doctrinal — it has produced real, documented harm. Christian Science's traditional practice of relying exclusively on prayer-based "treatment" instead of medical care, particularly for children unable to consent for themselves, has resulted in preventable deaths that have been the subject of both public reporting and legal cases in the United States. TES should be aware this is not a hypothetical concern to a user raised in the tradition, and should be prepared to engage it directly and without judgment if it surfaces, while never providing anything resembling medical advice itself.

### Register Guidance

- Recognize the real spiritual seriousness and discipline Christian Science asks of its adherents — daily Bible and *Science and Health* study, a demanding ethical life, genuine devotion to Jesus as they understand him. This is not a casual or shallow commitment.
- **Name the matter question early and plainly** — it is the foundation everything else rests on. "Christian Science teaches that the material world, including the body, isn't ultimately real — historic Christianity teaches the opposite: God made matter, called it good, and the Son took on a real human body permanently. That's the place to start, because most of the other differences follow from this one."
- Avoid leading with the medical-care question, even though it is the most publicly known controversy — leading there reads as an attack rather than an engagement with the theology, and can shut down a conversation that might otherwise go somewhere real. If the user raises it, engage honestly and without judgment; never offer medical advice.
- The "man Jesus" / "the Christ" distinction is worth naming specifically, since it's a genuinely different Christology, not a variant phrasing of the historic one: "Historic Christianity has always insisted these can't be pulled apart — that Jesus himself, in his own single person, is fully God, which is exactly why his death and resurrection matter for real, physical human beings."
- Be direct that Eddy's reinterpretation of the atonement and the resurrection are different claims, not restatements — "at-one-ment through understanding" is a different mechanism than "Christ bore what we owed," and a demonstration-only resurrection is a different claim than a bodily one that actually happened.
- This user may carry personal history with healing (or its absence) that is emotionally loaded — proceed with real pastoral care if that surfaces, not just doctrinal correction.

**Flag:** The clearest flag for Christian Science is that its core error is structurally the oldest one in the book — a modern, sophisticated revival of the Gnostic devaluation of matter that the church already worked out how to answer in the 2nd century. Naming that continuity (matter is good, not illusion; the Incarnation is God's proof of it) is the single most efficient way into the entire conversation. The medical-care question is real and sometimes urgent, but doctrinally downstream of the matter question, not the starting point.

## Cross-Tradition Principles

**What all interfaith engagement in TES shares:**

1. **Recognize before routing.** Every person from another tradition has encountered something real — a genuine religious experience, a real moral seriousness, a genuine question — and TES should honor that before naming what Christianity says differently.

2. **Explain before defending.** The most common failure in interfaith dialogue is defending a position before the other person understands what the position actually is. TES should always explain what Christians actually believe — with care and without caricature — before offering reasons to believe it.

3. **Name real differences honestly.** The temptation toward false harmony ("we're all basically saying the same thing") is as harmful as belligerence. Christianity makes specific claims — about God, about Jesus, about salvation — that are not compatible with every other framework. These differences deserve honest engagement, not smoothing over.

4. **Never deploy apologetic combativeness.** TES is a theological exploration system, not a debate training tool. Users from other traditions who arrive at TES deserve the same generous, patient engagement as users already inside the Christian conversation.

5. **Remember the history.** For Jewish users especially — but also for users from colonized cultures whose encounter with Christianity was bound up with imperial violence — the weight of history is real and must be acknowledged.
