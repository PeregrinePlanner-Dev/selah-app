# Node: Law & Gospel

**Weight:** Luther called the ability to distinguish Law and Gospel "the highest art in Christianity." This is not an overstatement. Virtually every pastoral error in Christian history — legalism, antinomianism, moralism, cheap grace — is a failure to hold Law and Gospel in their proper relationship. The distinction determines how Scripture is preached, how the sinner comes to faith, how the believer relates to God's commands, and how the church balances grace and obligation. It is invisible as a topic to most ordinary Christians while structuring everything they experience in the Christian life.

**Connects to:** Justification, Sanctification, Grace, Faith, Atonement, Anthropology, Scripture & Revelation, Covenant Theology

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- The Law is God's revealed will for human conduct — it is holy, righteous, and good (Rom 7:12); it is not an error or an Old Testament problem but a permanent expression of God's character
- The Gospel is the announcement of what God has done in Christ for sinners — it is not advice or demand but proclamation; it gives what the Law requires
- The Law and Gospel are both from God — the same God who commands is the God who saves; the distinction is not between the God of the OT (law) and the God of the NT (grace); this is the Marcionite heresy
- The Law reveals sin — it exposes human inability and rebellion; without the Law, sin is not recognized (Rom 7:7); this diagnostic function is essential to understanding the Gospel
- The Law cannot save — justification is not by works of the law (Gal 2:16); the law that diagnoses cannot cure; this is the Reformation's foundational claim against medieval works-righteousness
- The Gospel fulfills the Law — Christ's active obedience (obeying the law perfectly) and passive obedience (bearing the law's penalty) means the law's demands are satisfied in him, not abandoned
- The redeemed are not under the law as a covenant of works — believers are not in a performance relationship with God; grace, not merit, governs the standing of the justified

## Key Tensions

### 1. The Uses of the Law — Two or Three?

The Reformation tradition identified two or three distinct functions (uses) of the law. The number is not merely academic — it determines whether the law has a positive role in the Christian life or only a preparatory one.

**The First Use (Civil/Civic):** The law functions as a restraint on sin in society — it threatens punishment and thereby limits the worst expressions of human depravity. This use applies to all people, not just believers. It is the basis for natural law theory, the legitimacy of civil government, and common moral discourse. Even where the law cannot regenerate, it can restrain — and a society without law devolves toward chaos.

**The Second Use (Theological/Pedagogical):** The law functions as a mirror — it shows the sinner their sin, their guilt, and their need for grace. Paul: "through the law comes knowledge of sin" (Rom 3:20); "the law was our guardian until Christ came, in order that we might be justified by faith" (Gal 3:24). This is the law as preparation for the gospel: not salvation itself, but the exposure of the need for salvation. Lutheran preaching classically moves through law (the crushing word of demand and judgment) to gospel (the liberating word of grace) in every sermon.

**The Third Use (Normative):** The law functions as a guide and rule for the believer's life — not as a means of justification but as a revelation of what pleasing God looks like. Calvin gave the third use priority; it is the law as the shape of gratitude. Reformed theology holds that the believer is not under the law as a covenant of works but is still guided by the law's moral content. The Decalogue as a pattern for Christian living is the third use in action.

**The Lutheran-Reformed divide:** Luther was suspicious of the third use — not because he thought Christians could live without moral guidance, but because he worried that introducing the law back into the life of the justified would collapse the Law/Gospel distinction and produce a new form of works-righteousness. Lutheran theology tends to emphasize that the believer lives in permanent tension between old nature (under law) and new nature (under gospel); the law always functions as accuser, even for the believer. Reformed theology emphasizes the third use more strongly: the law is the grateful Christian's map for living, received from the Father's hand, not the taskmaster's.

### 2. Antinomianism — The Error on the Gospel Side

Antinomianism (from *anti-*, against, + *nomos*, law) is the position that the gospel's freedom from the law means the law has no claim on the Christian at all — that moral commands are not binding, that obedience is legalism, or that genuine grace produces no concern for holiness.

**Historical forms:**
- *Situational antinomianism* — the situational ethics position; love replaces law; each situation is judged by love without reference to fixed commands
- *Hyper-grace teaching* — modern forms that treat any emphasis on Christian obedience as a return to works-righteousness; the believer is so free that the law's moral content is irrelevant
- *Cheap grace (Bonhoeffer)* — grace proclaimed without demand; forgiveness without discipleship; "the grace we bestow on ourselves" that costs nothing and changes nothing

**The Reformation response:** The reformers (especially Calvin) consistently distinguished justification from sanctification while insisting on their inseparability. You are not saved by works; but you are not saved without works following. The tree that bears no fruit is cut down (Matt 7:19). The grace that does not transform is not grace at all. Faith alone justifies, but the faith that justifies is never alone.

**Paul's own question:** "Shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? By no means!" (Rom 6:1–2). The antinomian conclusion ("since we're saved by grace, obedience doesn't matter") is precisely what Paul refutes in Romans 6. The answer is not "try harder" but "know who you are" — united to Christ in death and resurrection, the old self is dead; sin no longer has dominion.

### 3. Legalism — The Error on the Law Side

Legalism is the position that standing before God depends on moral performance — whether through initial justification by works or through maintaining relationship through law-keeping. It is the perennial alternative to grace.

**Forms of legalism:**
- *Justification legalism* — earning initial favor with God by moral achievement; the classic Reformation target; medieval indulgences, works-righteousness
- *Sanctification legalism* — having been justified by grace, maintaining relationship with God through performance; the more common evangelical form; moralism masquerading as discipleship
- *Pharisaism* — external compliance without heart transformation; rule-keeping as the primary spiritual discipline; Jesus's most sustained critique

**The diagnostic question:** Is the Christian's relationship with God primarily characterized by obligation or by adoption? The New Testament consistently grounds obedience in the indicative ("you are" — sons, free, dead to sin, alive to God) before the imperative ("therefore, be/do"). Legalism reverses this: it makes the imperative primary and the indicative dependent on performance.

**The pastoral consequence:** Legalistic Christianity produces either pride (if the rules are met) or despair (if they are not). Neither is the gospel. The alternative is not moral indifference but obedience from love — the obedience of children who know they are loved, not servants who fear they will be cast out.

### 4. The Law in Preaching — Law Before Gospel?

The most practically contested application of the Law/Gospel distinction is in preaching. Should every sermon move through law to gospel (exposing sin before proclaiming grace)? Or is the law/gospel sequence a theological framework, not a homiletical prescription?

**The Lutheran homiletical tradition:** The law must be preached in its full severity before the gospel is proclaimed, because the person who has not felt the weight of condemnation cannot hear the gospel as gospel. The word of grace is wasted on those who do not know they need it. "The whole Bible," Luther said, "is either law or gospel" — and these must be carefully distinguished in preaching if the hearers are to receive them rightly.

**The Reformed alternative:** The law and gospel can be preached as a unity — in Christ, the law is fulfilled and the gospel proclaimed together. Not every text is a move from condemnation to grace; some texts address the believer directly as one who already stands in grace. The third use of the law means preaching the law's guidance can be entirely positive for the believing community.

**The practical question for TES:** How does a theology of the law shape how users engage with Scripture? The user who approaches the Bible as a moral code (law without gospel) will exhaust themselves or despair. The user who approaches it as pure good news (gospel without law) will lose the seriousness of sin and the cost of grace. TES should help users hold both — the word that undoes and the word that rebuilds.

## Scripture Anchors

- **Rom 3:20–22** — "through the law comes knowledge of sin... the righteousness of God through faith in Jesus Christ for all who believe" — the second use (diagnostic) and its resolution in the gospel
- **Rom 6:1–2, 14–15** — "shall we go on sinning so that grace may increase? By no means!... you are not under law but under grace" — the antinomian temptation and its answer
- **Rom 7:7, 12** — "if it had not been for the law, I would not have known sin"; "the law is holy, and the commandment is holy and righteous and good" — the goodness of the law; the diagnostic function
- **Rom 10:4** — "Christ is the end (*telos*) of the law for righteousness to everyone who believes" — the law's goal reached in Christ; the interpretive debate: end as termination or fulfillment?
- **Gal 2:16** — "a person is not justified by works of the law but through faith in Jesus Christ" — the Reformation's core text
- **Gal 3:24** — "the law was our guardian until Christ came, in order that we might be justified by faith" — the second use (pedagogical)
- **Gal 5:13–14** — "do not use your freedom as an opportunity for the flesh, but through love serve one another... for the whole law is fulfilled in one word: 'You shall love your neighbor as yourself'" — the third use; freedom for love, not license
- **Matt 5:17–20** — "do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them" — Jesus and the law; the continuity question
- **John 1:17** — "the law was given through Moses; grace and truth came through Jesus Christ" — law and gospel as two economies; not contradiction but progression
- **2 Cor 3:6** — "the letter kills, but the Spirit gives life" — Paul's law/gospel contrast in the covenant context; the new covenant and the Spirit

## Theologian Map

**Martin Luther (1483–1546)**
Luther's most significant theological contribution is arguably the Law/Gospel distinction itself. Before he could articulate justification by faith, he had to distinguish the law that condemns from the gospel that saves. His conversion experience was precisely this: he had been reading Romans 1:17 ("the righteousness of God") as a demand — God's righteous standard that condemned him — until he realized it referred to the righteousness God gives. Law became gospel for him. His *Against the Antinomians* (1539) shows he also fought against abolishing the law entirely — both errors (legalism and antinomianism) are failures of the Law/Gospel distinction. For Luther, the distinction is the hinge on which all of theology turns.

**John Calvin (1509–1564)**
Calvin gave a more positive account of the law's role in the Christian life than Luther, developing the Third Use as the primary purpose of the law. His exposition of the Decalogue in *Institutes* II.7–8 is a model of law-as-guide: each commandment is expounded in its fullest moral intention (going beyond the letter to the Spirit's application) as the Christian's map for gratitude-shaped living. Calvin's formulation: "the law is not abolished but the curse of the law is removed." He agrees with Luther that the law cannot justify; he disagrees that the law's only remaining role is diagnostic. For believers, the law is the Father's instruction for beloved children.

**Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906–1945)**
*The Cost of Discipleship* — the most important 20th-century treatment of cheap grace (antinomianism in modern dress). Bonhoeffer's distinction: cheap grace is grace preached without the cross, discipleship, or transformation — grace that forgives without requiring anything. Costly grace is the gospel that calls us to follow Christ, which is both utterly free (not earned) and utterly demanding (the whole life). His phrase: "cheap grace is the deadly enemy of our Church." Bonhoeffer wrote from within a German Christianity that had become so comfortable with itself that it could bless the Nazi state. The cheap grace critique is not a law-gospel confusion — it is an insistence that genuine grace always transforms.

**Stephen Westerholm (1948–)**
*Perspectives Old and New on Paul* — the most important evangelical scholarly treatment of Paul and the law. Westerholm carefully distinguishes Paul's "law" in its various uses and argues that the Reformation reading (Paul opposing justification by works of law as moral achievement) is essentially correct against the New Perspective's claim that "works of the law" refers only to Jewish identity markers. His work grounds the Lutheran Luther's Law/Gospel reading in careful NT exegesis, countering the New Perspective's revision.

## Entry Points

- **"If I'm saved by grace, why does it matter how I live?"** — the antinomian temptation; Bonhoeffer on cheap grace; the inseparability of justification and sanctification
- **"I feel like I can never be good enough for God"** — the second use of the law; the distinction between law (which accuses) and gospel (which frees); Luther's conversion story
- **"Is the Old Testament still relevant for Christians?"** — the continuity of the law; the three uses; which OT laws apply and how; the ceremonial/civil/moral distinction
- **"Isn't Christianity just about following rules?"** — the moralism critique; how grace transforms rather than removing moral commitment; the difference between legalism and grateful obedience
- **"How do I know when I'm being legalistic?"** — the diagnostic question; standing before God based on performance vs. adoption; the indicative before the imperative
- **Scholars:** The Lutheran Law/Gospel distinction and its Lutheran-Reformed difference; Bonhoeffer on cheap grace; the New Perspective on Paul debate; Westerholm's response

## Failure Modes

- **Marcionite dualism** — treating the God of the OT (law, wrath) as different from the God of the NT (grace, love); both Testaments reveal the same God; the law in the OT and the grace in the OT are equally present; Jesus did not abolish the law
- **Antinomianism** — using the Law/Gospel distinction to evacuate moral seriousness from the Christian life; Bonhoeffer's cheap grace; the error of treating freedom from the law as freedom for sin
- **Third-Use legalism** — using the third use of the law to re-introduce merit or performance into the believer's standing before God; treating the law as guide but subtly reverting to treating it as means of acceptance
- **Two-use minimalism** — so restricting the law to its diagnostic/civic uses that the believer has no positive moral guidance from Scripture; Christian life becomes entirely Spirit-led with no reference to specific commands
- **Law/Gospel as historical sequence** — treating the law as belonging to the old covenant and the gospel to the new, so that law becomes irrelevant for NT Christians; misreads Paul, who cites the law to guide the gospel communities
- **Preaching without law** — comfortable congregations who are never exposed to the law's searching and exposing work; they cannot hear the gospel as relief because they have not felt the burden
