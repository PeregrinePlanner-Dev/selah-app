# Node: Miracles

**Weight:** The hinge point where Christian theism and philosophical naturalism meet directly. If miracles cannot happen, the Resurrection is impossible and Christian faith collapses at its center. If they do happen, naturalism fails as a closed account of reality. The node operates on two levels simultaneously: the intellectual objection (can miracles happen at all?) and the pastoral question (why does God act miraculously for some and not others?). It also carries the live intra-evangelical debate between cessationists and continuationists — a debate that shapes entire ecclesial cultures, worship styles, and personal expectations of what the Christian life looks like.

**Connects to:** Theology Proper, Sovereignty & Free Will, Holy Spirit, Resurrection, Christology, Scripture & Revelation, Faith, Suffering & Providence

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- God is omnipotent and is not bound by natural law — he created it and can act within, through, or beyond it
- Miracles have occurred — the biblical narrative presents them as historical events, not merely symbolic or spiritual illustrations
- The Resurrection is the central and defining miracle — it is not one miracle among others but the one on which all others depend, and the pattern to which all future miracles point
- Miracles are not violations of natural law in the sense of breaking or contradicting nature's regularities — they are intrusions of a higher order into the lower; natural laws describe what happens when no higher power intervenes
- God is not obligated to perform miracles on demand — the biblical pattern shows miracles as purposeful divine acts, not automatic responses to sufficient faith
- Whether miraculous gifts continue in the church today is a legitimate intra-evangelical debate with serious biblical arguments on both sides

## Key Tensions

### 1. The Humean Challenge — Can Miracles Happen?

David Hume's argument (*An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding*, 1748): A miracle is a violation of natural law. Natural laws are established by "firm and unalterable experience." The probability that a natural law holds always exceeds the probability that any human testimony to a miracle is reliable. Therefore, belief in miracles is never rationally justified — the evidence for natural regularity always outweighs the evidence for the exception.

**C.S. Lewis's response** (*Miracles*, 1947): Hume's argument begs the question. It assumes naturalism — that the natural order is a closed system and that nothing exists beyond it — and then uses that assumption to rule out miracles. But this is circular: if God exists and created nature, there is no a priori reason he cannot act within it. The question of whether miracles occur cannot be settled by probability calculations about testimony; it must first be settled philosophically, by asking whether nature is all there is. Lewis's deeper move: naturalism is self-defeating. If all human thought is the product of mindless natural processes, there is no reason to trust it — including the reasoning that produces naturalism. The very act of following an argument presupposes something that transcends the merely natural. This argument (later developed by Plantinga as the "evolutionary argument against naturalism") establishes that supernaturalism is not intellectually irresponsible before Lewis ever examines a specific miracle claim.

**What a miracle is, positively:** Lewis argues that a miracle is not God breaking the laws of nature but God introducing new material on which the laws then operate normally. At the Resurrection, natural laws are not suspended — they continue to apply to the risen body. But a new event has entered the sequence from outside it. The laws of nature are descriptions of regularities; they are not constraints on the Creator.

**The remaining challenge:** Hume's question about testimony doesn't disappear entirely. How does a person evaluate claims to miraculous events? Craig Keener's two-volume empirical survey (*Miracles*, 2011) argues that the contemporary global church — particularly in the majority world — provides extensive, multiply-attested reports that cannot be explained away as pre-scientific credulity. The question is not whether unusual events are reported (they are, continuously and cross-culturally) but how to interpret them.

### 2. Miracle vs. Extraordinary Providence

Not every unusual answer to prayer is a miracle in the theologically precise sense. There is a spectrum: coincidence → answered prayer → extraordinary providence → miracle proper.

**The traditional distinction:** A miracle requires divine action that *transcends* — not merely orchestrates — the natural order. A cancer going into unexplained remission after prayer may be extraordinary providence; the instantaneous healing of a congenital condition, or a resurrection from death, is a miracle proper. This distinction matters because it clarifies the biblical pattern: miracles in Scripture are concentrated at specific redemptive-historical moments — the Exodus, the ministry of Elijah and Elisha, the incarnation and apostolic age — not distributed uniformly across history.

**The pastoral implication:** Collapsing everything unusual into "miracle" inflates the category and increases disappointment when the unusual event doesn't come. A person who prays for a job and gets one is not experiencing a lesser thing than a miraculous healing — they are experiencing God's ordinary providential care, which is itself a profound reality. Over-miracle-izing ordinary providence can make genuine faith in God's daily provision feel like a second-tier experience.

**The diagnostic question:** Did what happened require divine action beyond the created order, or could it be explained by natural causes directed by providence? The category matters, though discernment is often genuinely difficult at the margins.

### 3. Cessationism vs. Continuationism

Do the miraculous sign gifts — tongues, prophecy, healing, miracles — continue in the church after the apostolic age, or did they cease with the death of the apostles?

**The cessationist case (Warfield, MacArthur):** The sign gifts had a specific authenticating function — they confirmed the apostolic message as divine before the NT canon was complete. Acts shows miraculous gifts clustering around apostolic ministry, not ordinary congregational life. 1 Corinthians 13:10 ("when the perfect comes, the partial will pass away") refers to the completion of the canon. Once the apostolic foundation was laid and the canon closed, the authenticating function was complete and the gifts ceased. Post-apostolic miracle claims — including Catholic, charismatic, and Pentecostal — fail the biblical standard: they are private, unverifiable, therapeutic rather than nature-transforming, and lack the public evidential character of NT miracles.

**The continuationist case (Fee, Grudem, Keener):** Nothing in Scripture indicates a temporal limit on the gifts. 1 Corinthians 13 does not refer to the canon — "when the perfect comes" is contrasted with "seeing in a mirror dimly" (v. 12), which is the condition of the present age as a whole, not the pre-canonical church specifically. The Spirit is the eschatological down-payment (*arrabōn*) of the age to come — his gifts are signs of the new creation breaking into the present, and there is no biblical warrant to expect them to diminish before the end. The experience of the global church, especially in the majority world, provides extensive and documented contemporary testimony.

**The pastoral stakes:** Cessationist churches tend toward word-centered worship, ordered ministry, and a healthy skepticism about claims of direct revelation. Continuationist and charismatic churches tend toward expectant, Spirit-oriented worship and openness to the irregular. Neither tradition can claim the other has no biblical case — this is a genuine tension within a shared commitment to Scripture's authority.

### 4. Miracles and Faith — Signs for Belief or Tests of Belief?

The NT holds miracles and faith in a complex relationship that resists simple resolution:

- **John 20:30–31** — "these signs are written so that you may believe" — miracles are designed to generate faith
- **Acts 2:22** — Jesus was "accredited by God... by miracles, wonders and signs" — miracles authenticate
- **John 4:48** — "Unless you see signs and wonders you will never believe" — Jesus rebukes faith that requires signs
- **Matthew 12:38–39** — the request for a sign is called a mark of wickedness
- **Mark 6:5–6** — Jesus "could not do any miracles there" because of unbelief — faith and miracle are connected
- **John 20:29** — "blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed" — faith without sight is the higher call

**The pattern:** Miracles can be graciously given as faith-supports — especially at redemptive-historical turning points. But demanding miracles as a condition of belief, or treating the absence of miracles as evidence of God's indifference, is a spiritual failure the NT consistently warns against. The Thomas episode is the pivot: the miracle is given, but the direction of movement is toward those who believe without it. Miracles serve faith; they are not meant to replace or guarantee it.

**The faith-healing complication:** The "name it and claim it" tradition argues that miraculous healing is always available to those with sufficient faith, and that failure to receive healing indicates insufficient faith. This directly contradicts the biblical pattern — Paul had a "thorn in the flesh" that was not removed despite prayer (2 Cor 12:7–9); Timothy had chronic stomach problems (1 Tim 5:23); Epaphroditus "almost died" and was not instantaneously healed (Phil 2:27). Miraculous healing is real; it is not guaranteed to every believer who prays with sufficient faith.

## Scripture Anchors

- **John 2:1–11** — the first sign at Cana; "this, the first of his signs, Jesus did at Cana in Galilee, and manifested his glory. And his disciples believed in him" — miracles as revelation and faith-generation; the sign points beyond itself
- **John 20:30–31** — "Now Jesus did many other signs in the presence of the disciples, which are not written in this book; but these are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name" — the purpose of recorded miracles; faith is the goal
- **Acts 2:22** — "Jesus of Nazareth, a man attested to you by God with mighty works and wonders and signs that God did through him in your midst" — miracles as divine accreditation of Jesus
- **Hebrews 2:3–4** — "it was declared at first by the Lord, and it was attested to us by those who heard, while God also bore witness by signs and wonders and various miracles and by gifts of the Holy Spirit" — miracles authenticating the apostolic proclamation
- **John 4:48** — "Unless you see signs and wonders you will not believe" — Jesus's rebuke of sign-dependent faith
- **Matthew 12:38–39** — "An evil and adulterous generation seeks for a sign, but no sign will be given to it except the sign of the prophet Jonah" — the Resurrection as the definitive and sufficient sign
- **Mark 6:5–6** — "he could do no mighty work there, except that he laid his hands on a few sick people and healed them. And he marveled because of their unbelief" — the mysterious relation between faith and miracle
- **2 Corinthians 12:7–9** — "a thorn was given me in the flesh... Three times I pleaded with the Lord about this, that it should leave me. But he said to me, 'My grace is sufficient for you, for my power is made perfect in weakness'" — miraculous healing withheld; grace sufficient without it
- **1 Corinthians 12:9–10** — "to another gifts of healing by the one Spirit, to another the working of miracles, to another prophecy" — miraculous gifts in the body

## Theologian Map

**C.S. Lewis (1898–1963)**
*Miracles: A Preliminary Study* (1947) — the philosophical defense of the possibility of miracles. Lewis's central argument: the question of whether miracles occur cannot be settled by examining individual miracle claims; it must first be settled philosophically, by deciding whether naturalism or supernaturalism is true. Naturalism (nature is a closed system; nothing exists beyond it) makes miracles definitionally impossible — not because of evidence, but because of prior assumption. Lewis argues that naturalism is self-refuting: a worldview that reduces all thought to the product of mindless natural processes cannot trust its own conclusions. If the mind is entirely a natural product, it has no warrant to claim its reasoning is valid — including the reasoning that produces naturalism. This anticipates Plantinga's evolutionary argument against naturalism. Lewis's constructive claim: a miracle is not God breaking the laws of nature but introducing new material from outside the sequence — the laws then operate normally on the new material. The Incarnation and Resurrection are not violations of nature but the arrival of the supernatural into nature, which then reshapes it from within.

**B.B. Warfield (1851–1921)**
*Counterfeit Miracles* (1918) — the most rigorous biblical and historical case for cessationism. Warfield argues that the miraculous gifts of the apostolic age had a specific, temporary purpose: to authenticate the apostolic proclamation before the NT canon was complete. Examining Acts, he shows that the pattern of miraculous gifts follows the apostles themselves — gifts spread through the laying on of apostolic hands, not through ordinary community life. The cessation of the gifts is not a theological deduction but a historical observation: by the late second century, the charismatic gifts had passed from the church's life, and this tracks exactly with the completion of the apostolic canon. His *Counterfeit Miracles* is also a historical critique of post-apostolic miracle claims — he argues that the medieval Catholic miracles, the claims of the Enthusiasts, and the early healing movements all fail the biblical standard for genuine miracles: they are private, unverifiable, and therapeutically rather than creatively miraculous.

**Gordon Fee (1934–2022)**
*God's Empowering Presence: The Holy Spirit in the Letters of Paul* (1994) — the definitive continuationist exegetical case from within the evangelical tradition. Fee argues that Paul's pneumatology does not support a two-age theory of the Spirit's work. The Spirit is the *arrabōn* (down payment, first installment) of the age to come — his empowering presence and gifts are eschatological signs of the new creation already breaking in. There is no Pauline basis for expecting the Spirit's gifts to diminish or cease before the Parousia. On 1 Corinthians 13: "the perfect" (*to teleion*) is contrasted with present partial knowledge and with seeing "in a mirror dimly" — the entire condition of the present age, not the pre-canonical period specifically. Fee's exegesis is careful, detailed, and does not depend on charismatic experience — it is an argument from the text.

**Craig Keener (1960–)**
*Miracles: The Credibility of the New Testament Accounts* (2 vols., 2011) — the empirical response to Hume. Keener's contribution is not theological but evidentiary: he surveys hundreds of contemporary miracle reports from across the global church — particularly Africa, Asia, and Latin America — many with medical documentation and multiple witnesses. His argument: Hume's dismissal of miracle testimony assumed that such testimony was confined to pre-scientific, credulous populations. The contemporary data does not support this. Educated observers, medical professionals, and multiply-attested reports from the majority-world church constitute a body of evidence that cannot be explained away by appealing to pre-scientific credulity. Keener is not arguing that every claim is genuine but that the class of miraculous divine action is empirically credible — and that dismissing it requires the same a priori naturalist assumption Lewis identified as question-begging.

## Entry Points

- **"Do miracles actually happen?"** — the basic question; the Humean challenge and Lewis's response; naturalism vs. supernaturalism as the real issue
- **"Why doesn't God perform miracles today like he did in the Bible?"** — the cessationism question or the pastoral question of absence; distinguishes the two and takes each seriously
- **"Can God heal?"** — the healing prayer entry; the spectrum from answered prayer to miracle; the NT pattern; what to do when healing doesn't come
- **"I asked God for a miracle and nothing happened"** — the pastoral/experiential entry; connects to Suffering & Providence; Paul's thorn; grace sufficient without the miracle
- **"How do I know if something is a miracle or just a coincidence?"** — the miracle vs. providence distinction; what makes a miracle a miracle
- **"Is it wrong to ask for a sign?"** — the biblical tension; the rebuke of sign-dependent faith; asking vs. demanding
- **Scholars:** Hume's argument and Lewis's *Miracles*; cessationism vs. continuationism; Keener's empirical survey; the naturalism question

## Failure Modes

- **Functional deism** — affirming past miracles while denying present divine action; God set up the universe and stepped back; effectively rules out miracles in the present by a prior assumption rather than by evidence
- **Miracle-on-demand theology** — God is obligated to act miraculously for those with sufficient faith; the failure of the miracle is always the petitioner's fault; directly contradicted by Paul's thorn, Timothy's illness, and Epaphroditus's near-death
- **Category inflation** — treating every unusual event as a "miracle" in the technical sense; dilutes the meaning, makes ordinary providence feel like second-tier experience, and increases pastoral disappointment when the unusual doesn't recur
- **Cessationist overconfidence** — treating cessationism as settled orthodoxy rather than a defensible but contested position; dismissing the experience of the majority-world church without engaging the evidence
- **Continuationist credulity** — accepting all miracle claims uncritically; failing to apply discernment; "God told me" and "I was healed" are claims that require evaluation, not automatic affirmation
- **Hume's error (in reverse)** — treating every unusual event as miraculous to avoid the hard work of discernment; probability reasoning still applies to individual claims even after the philosophical case for miracles is established
