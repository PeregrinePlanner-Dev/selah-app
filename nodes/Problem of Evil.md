# Node: Problem of Evil

**Weight:** The philosophical pressure point at which atheism makes its strongest case against Christian theism. Unlike the Suffering & Providence node — which is primarily pastoral — this node is primarily philosophical: the problem of evil as an argument against God's existence. The two nodes are closely related but serve different users: the person in grief needs Suffering & Providence; the person whose faith is intellectually destabilized by the existence of suffering and evil needs Problem of Evil. Both doors lead to the same God, but the path is different.

**Connects to:** Theology Proper, Sovereignty & Free Will, Anthropology, Suffering & Providence, Eschatology, Christology

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- God is omnipotent — he has the power to do anything that is logically possible
- God is omniscient — he knows all things, including the suffering of his creatures
- God is perfectly good — he wills the good of his creatures and hates evil
- Evil and suffering genuinely exist — this is not an illusion to be explained away; the Christian tradition does not deny the reality of evil
- God is not the author of evil — he is not evil's originating cause; the tradition distinguishes between God's ordaining/governing will and the freedom of creatures to sin
- God will ultimately defeat evil — the eschatological defeat of evil is not merely hoped for but guaranteed in the resurrection of Christ; evil does not have the final word
- The existence of evil does not logically disprove God's existence — the Christian tradition has defended this claim philosophically across centuries

## Key Tensions

### 1. The Logical Problem of Evil — Plantinga's Free Will Defense

The classical formulation: It is logically impossible for all three of these to be true simultaneously:
1. God is omnipotent
2. God is omniscient
3. God is perfectly good
4. Evil exists

If God is omnipotent, he could prevent evil. If he is omniscient, he knows about evil. If he is perfectly good, he would want to prevent evil. Therefore, a God with all three attributes could not coexist with evil.

**Plantinga's free will defense:** The argument fails because it assumes God can create free creatures who always choose the good. But this may be logically impossible — it is a contradiction in terms. A creature who is caused to always choose rightly is not free in the morally relevant sense. If God values genuine creaturely freedom (as Scripture suggests), then a world with free creatures necessarily includes the logical possibility of those creatures choosing evil. God's omnipotence does not extend to logical contradictions; he cannot make free creatures who cannot choose wrongly. Therefore, the logical problem of evil fails: God and evil can coexist without logical contradiction.

**The limitation of the free will defense:** It accounts for moral evil (evil that results from human free choices — murder, abuse, war) but faces difficulty with natural evil (earthquakes, cancer, tsunamis — suffering that does not result from human choices). Plantinga extends the defense to natural evil by positing that free supernatural beings (demons) may be responsible for natural evil, but this is speculative.

**The Calvinist response:** The free will defense requires libertarian free will (genuine freedom to choose contrary to all prior causes). Reformed theology holds compatibilist freedom (freedom to act according to one's own desires and nature, which is itself shaped by God's sovereign governance). Plantinga's defense is not available to strict Calvinists, who must locate the solution differently — in God's right to govern all things according to his purposes, even allowing evil, for his own good reasons.

### 2. The Evidential Problem of Evil — Gratuitous Evil

Even granting that the logical problem is solved, there remains a more powerful evidential form: Even if God and evil can coexist logically, the sheer amount and distribution of suffering in the world is evidence against the existence of a perfectly good, omnipotent God. Why would a good God permit the Holocaust? Why do children die of cancer? Why does an earthquake kill 200,000 people? These seem to be gratuitous evils — suffering that serves no greater good.

**The "no-see-um" response (skeptical theism):** We should not expect to be able to see the greater goods that justify God's permission of evil. If God exists, the gap between his knowledge and ours is infinite; we are in no position to judge that any particular evil is truly gratuitous (serving no greater good). The argument from evidential evil assumes we would know if there were sufficient reasons for God to permit the suffering — but this is an unwarranted assumption given the epistemic disproportion between God and creatures.

**The objection to skeptical theism:** If we cannot trust our moral judgments about what God would or would not permit, then our moral perception is globally undermined — we cannot reason about ethics at all. The skeptical theist response seems to require a pervasive moral skepticism that is too costly.

**The soul-making theodicy (Irenaeus, Hick):** Suffering is necessary for human moral and spiritual development. A world without suffering and challenge would not produce virtue, courage, compassion, or faith. This is not that suffering is good in itself but that the conditions of soul-making require real danger, real difficulty, and real loss. The objection: this doesn't account for the scale of suffering that exceeds any plausible developmental benefit; and a God who could make humans already good without the suffering seems to be pulling his punches.

### 3. The Cross as the Center of Theodicy

The distinctively Christian contribution to the problem of evil is not a philosophical argument but the claim that God entered the suffering. Christian theodicy is not primarily a defense of God's goodness in abstract but a pointing to the cross.

**The cross as protest absorbed:** God in Christ entered the world of suffering and evil and took its worst into himself. The cry of dereliction ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?") is the Son of God voicing the experience of evil and abandonment from the inside. This does not explain evil philosophically; it demonstrates that God is not a distant philosophical absolute above the fray but a participant in suffering who has absorbed its depths.

**The resurrection as answer:** The resurrection does not explain why God permitted the crucifixion but demonstrates that God's final word is not suffering but life. What was genuinely evil — the murder of the Son of God — was not negated but redeemed. The eschatological claim: all evil will be similarly redeemed; the New Creation will be a world in which present suffering is not forgotten but transfigured.

**The limitation:** The cross-as-theodicy does not satisfy the philosophical demand for an explanation of why God permits evil. It is not an argument but a narrative; it does not justify evil theoretically but demonstrates that God is not indifferent to it. For the philosopher demanding a logical defense, this may seem like a subject change. For the theologian, it is the only honest answer.

### 4. Moral Evil and Original Sin — The Historical Question

The problem of evil is sharpest when it confronts not the existence of natural evil but the specific distribution and character of moral evil. Why did God create a world in which Adam would fall, introducing the catastrophic consequences of sin and death?

**The Augustinian response:** God created the world good; the fall was a real historical event in which real human beings chose to disobey, introducing sin, death, and natural disorder into a previously good creation. Evil is not part of God's original design but an intrusion — the result of creaturely freedom misused. God's permission of the fall is within his sovereign purpose, but the origin of evil is creaturely, not divine.

**The challenge of evolutionary creationism:** If the fossil record indicates that predation, death, and natural suffering predate humanity by hundreds of millions of years, then natural evil cannot be explained as the consequence of Adam's fall. This requires either: (a) denying or reinterpreting evolutionary history, (b) locating the cause of natural evil in pre-Adamic angelic rebellion, (c) rethinking what "natural evil" means in a world created for soul-making purposes, or (d) accepting that the Augustinian theodicy requires revision. The tension between evolutionary science and classical theodicy is one of the most pressing unresolved questions in contemporary Christian philosophy of religion.

## Scripture Anchors

- **Gen 1–3** — creation good, the fall, the introduction of sin and its consequences; the origin of moral evil in creaturely choice
- **Job 38–42** — God's answer from the whirlwind; the unanswered "why" and the transforming encounter; the limits of human comprehension
- **Ps 73** — Asaph's near-loss of faith at the prosperity of the wicked; "my feet had almost slipped"; the resolution in the sanctuary; the eschatological perspective
- **Isa 45:7** — "I form light and create darkness; I make well-being and create calamity; I am the Lord who does all these things" — divine sovereignty over all events, including calamity; the strong text for meticulous sovereignty
- **Rom 8:28** — "in all things God works for the good of those who love him, who have been called according to his purpose" — the promise that is eschatological, not immediate; in context (vv. 18–27, creation groaning and Spirit interceding)
- **Rom 9:17–18** — "for this purpose I raised you up, that I might display my power in you" — God's use of Pharaoh; the hardening; the purposes of God in apparent evil
- **Rev 21:3–4** — "he will wipe every tear from their eyes. There will be no more death or mourning or crying or pain, for the old order of things has passed away" — the eschatological elimination of evil; the forward resolution
- **1 Cor 15:24–26** — "the last enemy to be destroyed is death" — the eschatological defeat of evil as guarantee in Christ's resurrection

## Theologian Map

**Alvin Plantinga (1932–)**
The most important Christian philosopher on the problem of evil in the 20th century. *God and Other Minds* (1967) and *The Nature of Necessity* (1974) develop the free will defense: he demonstrates that the logical problem of evil fails because it is logically possible that God, being good, created free creatures and that those creatures chose evil. Plantinga's argument does not require that this is likely or even true — only that it is possible, which is sufficient to defeat the logical claim that God and evil are incompatible. His work largely conceded the field to the evidential problem (which he engages in later work) while establishing the logical problem as solved for serious philosophy of religion.

**C.S. Lewis (1898–1963)**
*The Problem of Pain* — the most widely read popular Christian treatment of the problem of evil. Lewis's argument: an omnipotent creator cannot give creatures free will without accepting the possibility that they misuse it; a world of genuine moral development requires real obstacles, real danger, and real consequence; the Christian life is not the pursuit of happiness but the pursuit of holiness, which suffering serves; the question is not whether God will keep us comfortable but whether he will make us good. Lewis's famous formulation: "God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks to us in our conscience, but shouts to us in our pain: it is his megaphone to rouse a deaf world." His later retreat from confidence in this theodicy in *A Grief Observed* (see Suffering & Providence) makes the two books a matched pair.

**Alvin Plantinga and the Reformed Epistemologists**
Reformed epistemology (Plantinga, Nicholas Wolterstorff, William Alston) argues that belief in God can be "properly basic" — rationally held without argument, as basic as belief in the external world or other minds. On this view, the evidential problem of evil should not shake the rational believer's confidence in God's existence: the evidence of suffering does not override the basic warrant for theistic belief unless one already assumes that theism requires an argument to justify it. This is a meta-level response: it does not solve the problem of evil but challenges the assumption that the problem threatens rational theistic belief.

**John Hick (1922–2012)**
*Evil and the God of Love* — the most developed philosophical statement of the soul-making theodicy in the 20th century. Hick distinguishes the Augustinian tradition (the world was created perfect; evil is a fall from that perfection) from the Irenaean tradition (humans were created immature, in the image of God but not yet in the likeness, and are destined to grow into God's likeness through experience, including suffering). On Hick's reading, the world is not a pleasure palace gone wrong but a vale of soul-making — a place where creatures develop toward the divine likeness through real struggle. Hick is not within the evangelical tradition and his conclusions tend toward universalism, but his theodicy is the most serious alternative to the Augustinian framework.

## Entry Points

- **"If God is good and powerful, why does he allow suffering?"** — the classical formulation; distinguishing the logical from the evidential problem; the free will defense
- **"How can you believe in God after the Holocaust?"** — the evidential problem at its sharpest; the scale of moral evil; the question of gratuitous evil
- **"Did God create evil?"** — the Isa 45:7 entry; the distinction between moral evil and natural evil; primary and secondary causation; evil as privation
- **"Why do bad things happen to good people?"** — the distribution problem; retribution theology and its failure (Job); the eschatological framework
- **"Doesn't the existence of evil disprove God?"** — the logical problem; Plantinga's response; the difference between "explains evil" and "is compatible with evil"
- **Scholars:** Plantinga's free will defense; the logical vs. evidential distinction; skeptical theism; the soul-making theodicy; the cross as theodicy

## Failure Modes

- **Cheap theodicy** — offering explanations that sound theological but function to silence the sufferer or defend God against genuine moral protest
- **Denying the reality of evil** — treating suffering as illusion, spiritual lesson, or hidden good without acknowledging that it is genuinely bad; the Christian tradition does not deny evil's reality
- **The rationalist trap** — assuming that a satisfying intellectual theodicy is what the suffering person needs; the philosophical and pastoral dimensions require different responses
- **Over-confidence about gratuitous evil** — too quickly declaring that suffering serves no good purpose, or too quickly claiming that it does; both exceed what we can know
- **Abandoning divine omnipotence** — some process theologians and open theists limit God's power to escape the problem; this solves the philosophical problem by changing the God in question
- **Skipping to eschatology** — moving immediately to "all will be made right in the end" without standing in the present reality; eschatological hope is real but cannot be used to dismiss present suffering
