# Node: Resurrection

**Weight:** The event on which Christianity stands or falls (1 Cor 15:17). The resurrection is not an appendix to the atonement but constitutive of salvation — it is the inaugural event of the new creation, the vindication of Jesus's identity and mission, and the ground of Christian hope. It is also the most historically contested claim in the tradition: the bodily resurrection of Jesus is the point at which Christianity most directly engages secular historiography, and the apologetic arguments for it are some of the most carefully developed in Christian intellectual history.

**Connects to:** Christology, Atonement, Justification, Eschatology, Anthropology, Assurance, Miracles

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- The resurrection of Jesus was bodily and historical — the tomb was empty; the risen Jesus ate, was touched, and bore the marks of the crucifixion
- The resurrection was not resuscitation — Jesus was raised to an imperishable, glorified bodily existence, not returned to mortal life
- The resurrection is the firstfruits of the general resurrection — it is the beginning of the new creation, not a one-off miracle standing alone
- The resurrection vindicates Jesus's identity and mission — it is God's declaration that Jesus's claims were true and his death was accepted
- The resurrection grounds justification — "raised to life for our justification" (Rom 4:25); the resurrection is itself a saving event, not merely a confirmation of the saving cross
- The resurrection guarantees the believer's future resurrection — "Christ has indeed been raised from the dead, the firstfruits of those who have fallen asleep" (1 Cor 15:20)
- The resurrection is the beginning of the new creation — the age to come has invaded the present age in the body of the risen Jesus

## Key Tensions

### 1. Historical vs. Theological Resurrection — What Kind of Event Was It?

**The historical claim:** The resurrection is a publicly investigable event that occurred in space and time. The tomb was empty; people saw the risen Jesus; these are historical facts that can be examined by historical methods. This is the dominant evangelical position, defended most rigorously by N.T. Wright.

**The theological-only claim (Bultmann, some liberal theology):** The resurrection is a theological affirmation about the significance of Jesus — his cause continues, his message triumphed over death, his followers experienced new life. To ask about the empty tomb is to misunderstand the genre of the resurrection claims; they are confessional, not historical. Rudolf Bultmann: "the resurrection is not a historical event that confirmed Jesus's significance; it is the birth of the Easter faith itself."

**The apologetic significance:** If the resurrection is historical, it is accessible to investigation and its truth or falsity can in principle be argued. The evidential arguments (empty tomb, post-resurrection appearances, the disciples' willingness to die for what they claimed to have seen, the explosion of the early church in Jerusalem where the events occurred) are strong. If the resurrection is only theological, it is removed from investigation but also removed from the force it has as a claim about what God actually did in history.

**Wright's contribution:** *The Resurrection of the Son of God* (2003) is the most comprehensive defense of the bodily, historical resurrection in recent scholarship. Wright argues that neither Jewish nor pagan categories of "resurrection" match what the early Christians claimed — they claimed something new, a bodily resurrection into imperishable life, that cannot be explained as the development of Jewish resurrection hope or as spiritual survival. The early Christians claimed exactly what they meant, and what they meant was historically unprecedented.

### 2. The Nature of the Resurrection Body

1 Corinthians 15 is the primary text. Paul uses the analogy of a seed: the body that is sown is not the same as the body that rises, yet there is continuity. The resurrection body is raised:
- *Incorruptible* — no longer subject to decay and death
- *In glory* — the shame of mortality replaced by the dignity of the new age
- *In power* — no longer subject to weakness and limitation
- *As a spiritual body* (*sōma pneumatikon*) — not a non-physical body but a body animated and transformed by the Spirit

**The continuity question:** Is the resurrection body the same body that died, or a new body? The early church debated this. The most defensible position: material continuity is not required (the atoms of the physical body do not need to be reconstituted), but personal continuity is essential — the resurrection body is genuinely the body of the same person. The mark of the nails in the risen Jesus (John 20:27) suggests a continuity that is more than merely personal identity.

**The "spiritual body" translation problem:** *Sōma pneumatikon* is often translated "spiritual body" in a way that suggests non-materiality. But Paul's usage of *pneumatikon* describes things animated by the Spirit, not things that are non-physical. The resurrection body is a Spirit-animated, physical body — not a ghost or a non-material existence. The risen Jesus eats (Luke 24:41–43) and is touched (John 20:27).

### 3. The Resurrection and the New Creation

N.T. Wright and others have recovered the cosmic dimension of the resurrection that evangelical theology has sometimes underemphasized. The resurrection is not primarily about the survival of individual souls; it is about God's renewal of the whole created order.

**The resurrection as new creation:** In raising Jesus from the dead, God has inaugurated the renewal of all things. The risen body of Jesus is the first element of the new creation within the old creation. Creation groans (Rom 8:22) because it awaits the liberation that has already begun in Christ's resurrection and will be completed at his return.

**Implications for present life:** If the resurrection is the beginning of God's renewal of the material world, then embodied, material existence matters now. The body is not a temporary container to be escaped but the site of resurrection life. This has implications for care of the body, stewardship of creation, the importance of physical healing, and the meaning of suffering in the body. Paul's instruction to flee sexual immorality is grounded in bodily resurrection: "the body is... for the Lord, and the Lord for the body. By his power God raised the Lord from the dead, and he will raise us also" (1 Cor 6:13–14).

### 4. The Appearances — What Did People See?

The NT accounts of resurrection appearances create genuine exegetical questions. The risen Jesus appears behind locked doors (John 20:19, 26), is not always immediately recognized (Luke 24:16; John 20:14–15), and then ascends physically (Acts 1:9). These features distinguish the appearances from the pre-resurrection Jesus while maintaining genuine embodiment.

**Paul's list (1 Cor 15:5–8)** enumerates appearances to Peter, the Twelve, five hundred at once, James, all the apostles, and finally Paul himself. Paul's own experience (Acts 9) was apparently visually overwhelming and auditory — he saw "a light" and heard a voice. Whether Paul's experience is the same kind of event as the appearances to the disciples in Jerusalem is debated.

**The apologetic argument from the appearances:** Hallucination hypotheses fail to account for the five hundred simultaneous witnesses (hallucinations are private, not shared); the specificity and diversity of the appearances; and the disciples' transformation from frightened scatterers to bold martyrs. The swoon theory (Jesus survived the cross) fails given Roman expertise in killing. The legend theory fails given the early date of Paul's creed in 1 Cor 15 (within 3–5 years of the events, citing eyewitness testimony).

## Scripture Anchors

- **1 Cor 15:3–8** — Paul's early creed; the appearances; "he appeared to me also, as to one abnormally born" — the earliest documentary evidence of the resurrection tradition, within years of the events
- **1 Cor 15:12–20** — "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile; you are still in your sins... if only for this life we have hope in Christ, we are of all people most to be pitied" — the stakes of the resurrection
- **1 Cor 15:35–44** — the seed analogy; the nature of the resurrection body; *sōma pneumatikon*
- **Rom 4:25** — "raised to life for our justification" — resurrection as itself a saving event
- **Rom 8:11** — "the Spirit of him who raised Jesus from the dead is living in you" — the resurrection Spirit as the power of present spiritual life
- **John 20:24–29** — Thomas's encounter; the mark of the nails; "my Lord and my God" — bodily continuity and divine identity together
- **Luke 24:36–43** — "touch me and see; a ghost does not have flesh and bones, as you see I have"; he eats fish — the insistence on genuine embodiment
- **Acts 1:9–11** — the ascension; bodily departure; the promise of bodily return
- **Phil 3:20–21** — "our citizenship is in heaven... from there we await a Savior, the Lord Jesus Christ, who will transform our lowly body to be like his glorious body" — the resurrection body as the template

## Theologian Map

**N.T. Wright (1948–)**
*The Resurrection of the Son of God* (2003) — the definitive scholarly defense of the bodily, historical resurrection. Wright's method: establish what Jewish and pagan contemporaries meant by "resurrection" (showing that the early Christian claim was genuinely new and unexpected), then examine the Gospel and Pauline evidence for the resurrection claim, demonstrating that the simplest historical explanation for the evidence is that something happened to Jesus that his followers could only describe as bodily resurrection. His key argument: no first-century Jew would have described what the Christians claimed (bodily resurrection into imperishable life within ongoing history) as "resurrection" unless something unprecedented had occurred. The development of the tradition from resurrection hope to the specific Easter claim cannot be explained without a real, historical event.

**William Lane Craig (1949–)**
The most prominent contemporary apologetic defender of the resurrection. His minimal facts argument: starting only with facts that are accepted by the majority of critical NT scholars (the burial, the empty tomb, the post-resurrection appearances, the disciples' conviction), Craig argues that the bodily resurrection is the best historical explanation for the data. His debates with leading atheist philosophers have made the apologetic case accessible to wide audiences. Craig's contribution to TES: for skeptical users who enter the resurrection question from an apologetic angle, Craig provides the most rigorous accessible argument.

**Oscar Cullmann (1902–1999)**
*Immortality of the Soul or Resurrection of the Dead?* (1956) — the most important Protestant challenge to the Platonic notion that death is the liberation of the immortal soul. Cullmann argued that the Greek philosophical view (the soul is naturally immortal and death is its liberation from the body) is fundamentally incompatible with the NT view (death is an enemy; the resurrection is God's defeat of death; the body matters). The NT does not teach the immortality of the soul — it teaches the resurrection of the body, which is a fundamentally different claim. This distinction is essential for a fully biblical anthropology and eschatology.

**Gary Habermas (1950–)**
The most thorough evangelical NT scholar on the historical resurrection. His *The Historical Jesus* and *The Risen Jesus and Future Hope* compile the scholarly evidence for the resurrection's historicity across multiple lines. Habermas developed the minimal facts approach independently of Craig, and his survey of critical scholarly opinion (including skeptical scholars) establishes which facts about the resurrection enjoy broad historical consensus. His contribution: he moves the discussion from faith statement to historical investigation, demonstrating that even methodologically secular historiography cannot dismiss the resurrection without engaging the evidence.

- **"Does it matter that Jesus rose physically, or is it just symbolic?"** — the bodily/spiritual debate; Bultmann and Wright; why the tomb had to be empty
- **"Will we recognize each other in heaven?"** — the personal continuity question; the resurrection body's relationship to the present body
- **"What does the resurrection have to do with my life now?"** — the new creation entry; the resurrection as the firstfruits; implications for how we live in the body now
- **Scholars:** Wright on the historical resurrection; Cullmann on immortality vs. resurrection; Paul's *sōma pneumatikon*; the minimal facts approach

## Failure Modes

- **Spiritualization** — treating the resurrection as a metaphor for new life in the Spirit without bodily referent; losing the material and historical claim
- **Resuscitation confusion** — treating the resurrection as if Jesus merely returned to mortal life; losing the qualitative difference of the glorified body
- **Gnostic instinct** — treating the resurrection as inconvenient materiality that complicates a spiritually satisfying afterlife theology; the body as an afterthought
- **Isolation from new creation** — treating the resurrection as an individual spiritual event (my afterlife hope) without its cosmic and creational dimensions
- **Apologetic reduction** — treating the resurrection primarily as a fact to be defended rather than a reality to be inhabited; losing the theological richness for the sake of the argument
- **Soul-survival substitution** — using "resurrection" as a synonym for the immortality of the soul; Cullmann's distinction applies
