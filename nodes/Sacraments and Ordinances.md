# Node: Sacraments & Ordinances

**Weight:** The most divisive practical node in the library — churches that agree on justification, the Trinity, and Scripture have split over whether baptism regenerates, who should be baptized, and whether Christ is physically present in the Supper. These are not trivial differences: they reflect fundamentally different understandings of how grace works, what the church is, and what it means for the material world to be the bearer of spiritual reality. The vocabulary itself is contested — "sacrament" (carrying connotations of grace-conveying efficacy) vs. "ordinance" (carrying connotations of commanded memorial) signals a theology before a word is defined.

**Connects to:** Ecclesiology, Grace, Regeneration, Justification, Christology, Covenant Theology, Holy Spirit

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- Baptism and the Lord's Supper are given by Christ to the church — not human inventions but dominical commands (Matt 28:19; Luke 22:19)
- They are signs and seals of the covenant of grace — they visibly represent the gospel and are means of grace, not mere performances
- They do not work automatically (*ex opere operato*) — the recipient's faith (or in the case of infant baptism, the faith of the community) is engaged
- They are performed within the church community — they are corporate acts, not private spiritual experiences
- They point to Christ — their meaning is derived from his person and work, not from the rite itself
- They are two in number for Protestants — Rome adds five additional sacraments; the Reformation argued that only baptism and the Supper have dominical institution and visible sign joined to Word of promise

## Key Tensions — Baptism

### 1. Paedobaptism vs. Credo-baptism — Who Receives Baptism?

**Paedobaptism (infant baptism)** — Reformed, Lutheran, Anglican, Catholic, Orthodox: Baptism is administered to infants of believing parents because they are members of the covenant community, as infant circumcision was administered in the old covenant. Covenant theology (the continuity between Israel and the church as the one people of God) is the foundation: just as circumcision was the sign of covenant membership administered to male infants (Gen 17), so baptism as the covenant sign of the new covenant is appropriately administered to covenant children. The NT does not exclude infants from baptism; the household baptisms in Acts (16:15, 33; 18:8; 1 Cor 1:16) likely included infants.

**Credo-baptism (believer's baptism)** — Baptist, Anabaptist, most Pentecostals: Baptism is administered only to those who make a credible profession of personal faith. The new covenant is constituted by personal regeneration, not ethnic/familial membership — "they will all know me, from the least of them to the greatest" (Jer 31:34). There is no NT precedent for infant baptism (it is not mentioned); the pattern in Acts is always belief followed by baptism. The household baptisms do not clearly include infants. Covenant theology's analogy breaks at the key point: circumcision was applied to male infants; baptism applies to both men and women, and to believers specifically.

**What is at stake:** The debate is not ultimately about infants — it is about the nature of the new covenant, the relationship between Israel and the church, and the basis of church membership. Paedobaptism produces parish/national church models; credo-baptism produces gathered-church models. The shape of the church follows from the baptismal theology.

### 2. Baptismal Regeneration — Does Baptism Save?

**Yes (Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, some Lutheran):** Baptism is the ordinary instrument through which God works regeneration. The water and the Word together accomplish new birth — this is what Jesus means in John 3:5 ("born of water and the Spirit") and what Titus 3:5 ("washing of rebirth") describes. The rite is not magic — the Spirit works through it — but the Spirit works through the sacrament, not merely alongside it.

**No, but it's not nothing (Reformed/Anglican):** Baptism is a sign and seal of the covenant of grace — a visible Word that represents and certifies what the gospel promises. It does not work automatically; the Spirit may work regeneration before, during, or after the rite. The sign does not guarantee the thing signified (which is why baptized covenant members may later apostatize). But baptism is more than a mere symbol — it is a genuine means of grace that strengthens faith and certifies covenant status.

**No — it's a public declaration (Baptist):** Baptism is an ordinance — a commanded act of obedience and public witness. It symbolizes death and resurrection with Christ (Rom 6:3–4); it is the believer's public identification with Christ and the church. It accomplishes nothing independent of the faith it expresses; it is a declaration, not an instrument.

## Key Tensions — Lord's Supper

### 3. The Presence of Christ — Real, Spiritual, or Memorial?

The most theologically significant division among the Reformers. Luther and Zwingli could not agree at the Marburg Colloquy (1529); Lutherans and Reformed have remained divided on this ever since.

**Transubstantiation (Catholic):** The bread and wine are converted in their substance into the body and blood of Christ while their accidents (appearance, taste, texture) remain. After consecration, Christ is physically and locally present under the forms of bread and wine. The Mass is a sacrifice — the representation and re-presentation of Christ's sacrifice at Calvary.

**Consubstantiation/Real Presence (Lutheran):** The body and blood of Christ are truly, substantially present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine — not by conversion of substance but by the coexistence of Christ's body with the bread. "This is my body" (Matt 26:26) is to be read literally. Luther's formulation: the bread is the body as iron heated in fire is fire-in-iron; both are genuinely present. The Lutheran concern with Zwingli: if Christ's words are mere metaphor, what else in Scripture is?

**Spiritual presence (Calvin):** Christ is genuinely and really present in the Supper — not physically or locally (his glorified body is at the right hand of the Father), but spiritually. The Spirit lifts the believer's heart to the ascended Christ, who truly feeds the soul with himself. Calvin's formulation is neither bare memorialism (Zwingli) nor local physical presence (Luther) but genuine spiritual communion. The Supper is the most intense moment of communion with Christ available this side of glory.

**Memorial/Symbolic (Zwingli):** "This is my body" means "this signifies/represents my body." The Supper is a memorial and proclamation of Christ's death — a visible pledge and reminder, not a vehicle of special grace. The benefit is spiritual and received by faith, but it is not particularly concentrated in the Supper any more than in prayer or Scripture. Zwingli's concern: a physically present Christ in bread would require Christ to have multiple bodies.

### 4. Who May Come to the Table?

**Closed communion:** Only members of a specific congregation (or denomination) in good standing may receive. This is the most restrictive practice; it takes seriously the connection between the Table and church membership/discipline.

**Close communion:** Only those who have been baptized (or, in some traditions, baptized as believers) and are members of a church in good standing may receive. The most common evangelical practice among confessional churches.

**Open communion:** Any professing Christian may receive. This is the most common practice among contemporary evangelical churches — the Table is a place of welcome for all who trust Christ.

**The theological question:** The Supper is not primarily an individual act of spiritual nourishment but a communal act of covenant renewal and profession. 1 Cor 11:27–29 warns about eating "unworthily" — which Paul explains as failure to discern the body (the community, not just the bread). Admitting anyone who comes without examination eliminates the Supper's connection to church membership and discipline; but restricting it too narrowly can produce a sacramentalism in which the rite gates the grace rather than celebrates it.

## Scripture Anchors — Baptism

- **Matt 28:19** — "baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" — the dominical command; the Trinitarian formula
- **Acts 2:38** — "repent and be baptized... for the forgiveness of your sins and you will receive the gift of the Holy Spirit" — baptism and forgiveness in close connection; the baptismal regeneration text
- **Rom 6:3–4** — "all of us who were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death... so we too may live a new life" — union with Christ in death and resurrection; the meaning of the rite
- **Titus 3:5** — "the washing of rebirth and renewing by the Holy Spirit" — the regeneration connection; the paedobaptist/baptismal regeneration text
- **Col 2:11–12** — circumcision/baptism parallel; "buried with him in baptism, in which you were also raised with him through faith" — the paedobaptist covenant-sign argument

## Scripture Anchors — Lord's Supper

- **Matt 26:26–28** — the words of institution; "this is my body... this is my blood of the covenant" — the literal/symbolic debate turns on "is"
- **1 Cor 10:16** — "the cup of blessing which we bless, is it not a participation (*koinonia*) in the blood of Christ? The bread that we break, is it not a participation in the body of Christ?" — Calvin's spiritual presence; genuine *koinonia* with Christ
- **1 Cor 11:23–29** — Paul's account of the institution; "in remembrance of me"; "you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes"; examination before eating; judgment for eating unworthily
- **John 6:53–56** — "unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you have no life in you... whoever eats my flesh and drinks my blood remains in me, and I in them" — the eucharistic chapter; literal vs. spiritual reading debate

## Theologian Map

**Martin Luther (1483–1546)**
*The Babylonian Captivity of the Church* (1520) was Luther's attack on Rome's sacramental system, reducing the sacraments from seven to two (or three, briefly). But Luther retained a robust sacramental theology — baptism genuinely works regeneration through the word accompanying the water; the Lord's Supper genuinely contains Christ's body and blood. His debate with Zwingli at Marburg (1529) over the Lord's Supper failed to reach agreement; Luther wrote "This is my body" on the table in chalk and refused to budge. His concern: reducing the sacraments to mere symbols undermines the tangible, material character of God's grace and makes salvation an entirely interior, spiritual affair that the physical sign merely illustrates.

**John Calvin (1509–1564)**
The most theologically sophisticated Protestant sacramentology. Calvin holds that the sacraments are genuine means of grace — real seals of the covenant that confirm and strengthen faith — while refusing both Roman transubstantiation and Lutheran local presence. His distinction: the sign and the thing signified are genuinely connected (not identical, but not merely illustrative). The Spirit truly works through the signs to communicate Christ. His Supper theology: the Spirit lifts the believer to the ascended Christ, who genuinely feeds the soul with his glorified humanity. Calvin's sacramental theology is richer than most evangelicals realize — it is neither Catholic nor Zwinglian but genuinely a third way.

**Ulrich Zwingli (1484–1531)**
The Zurich reformer who took the symbolic/memorial view to its most consistent expression. Zwingli's hermeneutic: "is" in "this is my body" means "signifies" — as in "I am the vine" or "this is the Passover." The Supper is a memorial, a pledge of allegiance, a public declaration — not a vehicle of special grace. The spiritual benefit comes from the faithful remembrance and proclamation, not from any presence in or action of the elements. Zwingli represents the stream that has flowed most widely into contemporary evangelical practice — the Supper as meaningful memorial rather than sacramental vehicle.

**G.R. Beasley-Murray (1916–2000)**
*Baptism in the New Testament* — the most comprehensive evangelical NT examination of baptism. Beasley-Murray defends credo-baptism from thorough exegetical engagement with every relevant NT passage. His conclusion: the NT consistently treats baptism as the moment of conversion's visible expression, joining together repentance, faith, forgiveness, the Spirit's reception, and incorporation into the body. Baptism is not merely a symbol but a sacramental act in which what God declares corresponds to what is happening in the person. His careful work established the exegetical basis for the Baptist position at a scholarly level.

## Entry Points

- **"Do I need to be baptized to be saved?"** — the most common question; Acts 2:38 and Eph 2:8–9 in tension; the instrument/sign distinction
- **"Should I have my baby baptized?"** — the paedobaptism entry; covenant theology vs. believer's baptism; what the decision commits the church to
- **"What's really happening at communion?"** — the presence question; the four positions; the Calvin vs. Zwingli spectrum
- **"Does it matter how I'm baptized?"** — mode (immersion, sprinkling, pouring) and meaning; the immersion/mode debate is secondary to the subject/meaning debate
- **"Why do different churches do communion so differently?"** — historical entry; the Reformation divisions; what each practice embeds theologically
- **Scholars:** The Marburg Colloquy; Calvin's sacramental theology; Beasley-Murray on NT baptism; transubstantiation and its critics; the covenant theology/baptism connection

---

## Failure Modes

- **Sacramentalism** — treating the rite as automatically effective regardless of faith; *ex opere operato* without Spirit and Word; baptism as spiritual insurance
- **Bare memorialism** — reducing the rites to human acts of commemoration with no divine action; losing the means-of-grace dimension; the Supper as a reminder without encounter
- **Bibliolatry of the rite** — making the specific mode or subject of baptism the test of genuine Christianity; treating secondary questions as primary
- **Communion-frequency reduction** — so infrequent that the Supper loses its formative function; Calvin expected weekly; most evangelical churches practice monthly at best
- **Private communion** — removing the corporate, ecclesial character of both rites; the Supper as individual spiritual nourishment disconnected from communal body
- **Casual Table** — open communion with no examination, instruction, or preparation; loses the 1 Cor 11 warning about eating unworthily
