# Node: Sanctification

**Weight:** The ongoing transformation side of salvation — what grace does *in* the person after justification has settled what it does *for* them. Sanctification is where theology meets daily life, and where the most practically urgent pastoral questions live. It is also where the system faces its greatest pressure: users who feel they aren't changing, users who are exhausted from trying, and users who have confused moralism with holiness.

**Connects to:** Justification, Grace, Faith, Regeneration, Atonement, Holy Spirit, Assurance, Vocation & Work

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- Sanctification is the work of the Holy Spirit — it is not a human achievement; the Spirit is its author and engine
- Sanctification is distinct from justification — justification is a change in standing (declared righteous); sanctification is a change in condition (being made holy); they must be distinguished even though they cannot be separated
- Sanctification is inseparable from justification — whoever is truly justified is also being sanctified; receiving one without the other beginning is not possible (Calvin's *duplex gratia*)
- Sanctification involves both divine action and human cooperation — "work out your salvation... for it is God who works in you" (Phil 2:12–13); the two are not in competition
- Sanctification is progressive — it is a process that continues throughout the believer's life, not a crisis that completes it
- Sanctification involves mortification (putting to death sinful patterns) and vivification (being made alive to righteousness)
- Sanctification is not complete in this life — glorification (full conformity to Christ at resurrection) is its eschatological endpoint
- The goal of sanctification is conformity to the image of Christ (Rom 8:29)

## Key Tensions

*These have occupied serious theologians for centuries.*

### 1. Definitive vs. Progressive Sanctification

An internal Protestant question, sharpened by John Murray in the 20th century.

**Definitive sanctification:** Murray argued from Romans 6 that sanctification involves a once-for-all event at conversion — a real break with sin's *ruling power*. "We who died to sin" is not merely positional or metaphorical: the believer has been transferred out of sin's domain. The dominion of sin has been broken, not weakened. This definitive break is the ground on which progressive transformation stands.

**Why the distinction matters:** The structure of Romans 6 is indicative → imperative: "you have died to sin" (v. 2) grounds "do not let sin reign in your mortal body" (v. 12). The command presupposes the completed fact. The moralistic pattern inverts this: *become holy so that God will accept you*. The gospel pattern: *you have been accepted; therefore, become what you are*. The imperative draws its force from the indicative — not the other way around.

**Progressive sanctification:** The ongoing, Spirit-enabled process of actually becoming what has been definitively established. The dominion is broken; but sin still indwells and must be actively put to death. These are not contradictory: the slave owner has been legally stripped of authority, but the slave must learn to live in the freedom that has been established.

### 2. Entire Sanctification — Wesleyan Perfectionism

Wesley's most contested and distinctively Wesleyan teaching.

**Wesley's claim:** Through a second crisis work of grace — subsequent to and distinct from conversion — the Holy Spirit can purify the heart entirely of *inbred sin* (the sinful nature itself, not merely its expressions). The result is not absolute sinless perfection (mistakes remain; the glorified state is qualitatively different) but "perfect love": the complete orientation of the whole person toward love for God and neighbor, with the root of self-will expelled. Wesley grounds this in 1 John 3:9 ("no one born of God makes a practice of sinning"), 1 John 4:17–18 (perfect love casting out fear), and 1 Thess 5:23–24 ("sanctify you through and through... he will do it").

**The Reformed critique:** Entire sanctification misreads Romans 7 (which Reformed interpreters read as the ongoing internal conflict of the genuine believer, not a pre-conversion state). It sets up a two-tier Christianity in which some are "perfected" and others are ordinary believers — an elitism foreign to the NT. And in practice, those who claim it tend either to redefine sin narrowly enough to make the claim coherent, or to experience pride about their perfection — which is itself a significant sin.

**Wesley's response:** He is insistent that the doctrine produces hunger and expectation, never pride — the genuinely perfected believer is the most aware of their remaining limits and most eager to grow. The proper pastoral problem is not that people will claim perfection prematurely but that most Christians never aim for it at all. Refusing to pursue what Scripture holds out is the actual failure.

**The honest edge:** The Wesleyan tradition has rarely produced many who claimed entire sanctification without subsequent qualification. The doctrine functions better as a directional goal — perfect love as the eschatological horizon — than as a crisis experience locatable in time.

### 3. Sanctification by Faith vs. Sanctification by Effort

The 19th century Keswick movement ("Higher Life" teaching) drew the logic of justification across to sanctification: just as you receive forgiveness by faith, not effort, so you receive holiness by faith, not effort. "Let go and let God." "Rest, not wrestle." "Trust, not try." The result is a passivity model: straining and trying at holiness only produce failure; what is needed is surrender, yielding, Spirit-filling received by faith.

**The appeal:** It addresses genuine exhaustion. People who have tried harder at moral improvement and failed are ready for a framework that doesn't demand more effort. It also takes seriously the Spirit's role as the actual agent of change.

**The Reformed response:** This is a category error — the logic of justification (entirely passive, entirely received) does not transfer to sanctification. Scripture commands active effort: "train yourself for godliness" (1 Tim 4:7 — athletic discipline imagery); "put to death therefore what is earthly in you" (Col 3:5 — active imperative); "make every effort to add to your faith..." (2 Pet 1:5); "resist the devil" (James 4:7). Spiritual passivity in the name of trusting the Spirit often produces spiritual stagnation. B.B. Warfield (and more recently, Kevin DeYoung) argued that "let go and let God" mistakes the mechanics of sanctification entirely.

**The synthesis:** Phil 2:12–13 is the classic formulation — the divine working does not replace the human effort but grounds and enables it. "Work out your salvation with fear and trembling, *for* it is God who works in you both to will and to work." The *for* is causal: divine working is the reason for, not the replacement of, human working. Spirit-dependent effort, not passive waiting or anxious striving.

### 4. The Role of the Law in Sanctification

Calvin's Third Use of the Law: the law, even for the justified believer, serves as a positive guide — revealing God's character and the shape of transformed living. The law, written on the heart by the Spirit (Jer 31:33), continues to direct the sanctified life. "I delight in your law" (Ps 119:47) is the testimony of the sanctified person, not the person who is still earning.

**The Lutheran concern:** Law is primarily accusatory — it kills self-righteousness and drives to the gospel. For the Christian, the law's primary function remains the same: expose sin, drive to grace. Gospel motivation — being loved and forgiven — generates holy living more effectively and more genuinely than law-commands. The Christian who lives by gospel logic ("I am loved; therefore I love") is more genuinely holy than the one who lives by law logic ("I must obey to remain acceptable").

**The practical question:** Does the Sermon on the Mount function as law (impossibly high standard that exposes sin and drives to grace) or as gospel-description (the natural shape of the transformed life)? Do the NT ethical commands work like law (compliance by effort) or gospel (fruit produced by Spirit)? The answer shapes the entire pastoral posture toward holiness.

## Scripture Anchors

*Key passages with a note on what they carry.*

- **Rom 6:1–14** — the definitive sanctification passage; "we who died to sin"; the indicative grounds the imperative; "count yourselves dead to sin but alive to God in Christ Jesus" — reckoning a fact, not generating a feeling
- **Phil 2:12–13** — the divine/human cooperation at its most compact; "work out your salvation... for it is God who works in you to will and to act" — the *for* is crucial; divine working is the basis of human working, not its replacement
- **Rom 8:13** — "if by the Spirit you put to death the misdeeds of the body, you will live" — both Spirit and active putting-to-death; neither passivity nor self-effort alone
- **2 Cor 3:18** — "we all, who with unveiled faces contemplate the Lord's glory, are being transformed into his image with ever-increasing glory, which comes from the Lord, who is the Spirit" — progressive; Spirit's work; the direction is always more
- **Gal 5:16–25** — flesh/Spirit conflict; the fruit of the Spirit as Spirit-produced character; "those who belong to Christ Jesus have crucified the flesh with its passions and desires" — again: definitive act grounded in conversion
- **1 Thess 5:23–24** — "may God himself, the God of peace, sanctify you through and through... The one who calls you is faithful, and he will do it" — Wesley's proof text; divine initiative and completion
- **Heb 12:14** — "make every effort to live in peace with everyone and to be holy; without holiness no one will see the Lord" — holiness as non-negotiable; effort language
- **1 John 3:2–3** — "what we will be has not yet been made known... when Christ appears, we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is. All who have this hope in him purify themselves" — eschatological motivation for present sanctification
- **2 Pet 1:3–11** — "make every effort to add to your faith goodness; and to goodness, knowledge..." — the virtue list; sanctification as cultivated growth; "if you do these things, you will never stumble"

## Theologian Map

*Substantive positions — enough for serious engagement from the first exchange.*

**John Calvin (1509–1564)**
Sanctification is received alongside justification through union with Christ — this is the *duplex gratia*. They are inseparable in the receiving but must be carefully distinguished in the understanding. The engine of sanctification for Calvin is mortification and vivification: the putting to death of the old nature and the being made alive to the new. These are Spirit-worked and ongoing. Calvin also gives the most developed treatment of the Third Use of the Law — the law as positive guide for the sanctified life. The Christian looks to the law not to earn favor but to know the shape of the holy life God intends. Calvin's practical program: Word, prayer, sacraments, and discipline are the Spirit's ordinary means; holiness is formed through these structures, not around them.

**John Owen (1616–1683)**
The greatest Puritan treatise on the mechanics of actual moral change. *Of the Mortification of Sin in Believers* is Owen's masterwork: sin must be actively and specifically mortified through the Spirit — it does not simply fade with time, and the believer cannot kill it by willpower alone. "Do you mortify; do it by the Spirit." Owen's method is surgical: identify the specific sin; trace it to its root lust (what desire drives it?); bring the cross to bear on that root; do not merely suppress the expression but kill what feeds it. His diagnostic: sin that is habitually indulged and then repented but never actually weakened has not been mortified — it has been managed. Management is not mortification. Indispensable for users who are serious about the mechanics of actual change rather than conceptual agreement about holiness.

**John Wesley (1703–1791)**
Entire sanctification as the theological horizon and pastoral goal. Wesley insists the doctrine is biblical, not a human innovation; the pattern of Romans 6 and 1 John 3 points beyond progressive improvement toward something more decisive. His practical pastoral concern: most believers aim too low. The doctrine of entire sanctification is not meant to produce satisfaction at arrival but hunger for more. Wesley's spiritual direction was intensely practical — his *A Plain Account of Christian Perfection* is one of the most careful self-revisions in theological history; he refined the doctrine for forty years in response to abuse and misunderstanding. His lasting contribution: sanctification must have a goal, not just a direction — and Scripture supplies one.

**Dietrich Bonhoeffer (1906–1945)**
Sanctification has a concrete form — it is not an interior spiritual state but visible discipleship. *The Cost of Discipleship* is Bonhoeffer's theology of sanctified life: "only those who believe obey, and only those who obey believe." The two are inseparable — not because obedience earns belief, but because genuine faith always takes a concrete shape. Sanctification that produces no disruption to one's social, political, or economic arrangements is suspect. Writing in 1937 Germany, Bonhoeffer is not speaking abstractly: the church that has accommodated National Socialism while claiming interior holiness has not understood what sanctification is. His contribution to TES: holiness is not a private achievement; it is tested in public life.

**Dallas Willard (1935–2013)**
The most important 20th century evangelical voice on the *mechanics* of spiritual formation. *The Spirit of the Disciplines* recovers the classical spiritual disciplines tradition for Protestant evangelicalism. Willard's central argument: the NT's commands to be transformed cannot be executed by direct willpower — you cannot will yourself to love your enemies. But you can place yourself in the training conditions under which the Spirit forms that love. The spiritual disciplines (solitude, silence, fasting, Scripture, prayer, worship, confession, service, fellowship) are not ways of earning holiness; they are the indirect means through which character is actually formed. His key analogy: an athlete cannot directly will running speed; they train — and speed is the byproduct of training. Holiness is a trained capacity, not a spontaneous experience or a passively received gift. Willard critiques both Keswick passivity (wait for the Spirit to do it) and moralistic striving (try harder) as missing the means through which transformation actually occurs.

**Martyn Lloyd-Jones (1899–1981)**
Lloyd-Jones addresses the specific failure mode in sanctification that Owen, Willard, and Calvin all assume the believer understands but frequently does not: the confusion of effort with appropriation. The believer defeated by sin is almost never defeated because they lack willpower; they are defeated because they have not understood and reckoned with Romans 6 — the fact that in Christ their old self has already been crucified. The task is not to achieve a death that needs to happen but to "reckon yourselves dead to sin but alive to God" (Rom 6:11) — a faith-act, not an effort-act. Lloyd-Jones's diagnostic: the person who is trying very hard and still losing is applying law-logic to a gospel-shaped problem. The cure is not more discipline but a clearer grasp of who they already are in Christ. This complements Owen's mortification (which addresses the mechanics of killing specific sin) with the prior identity-question that mortification presupposes.

## Entry Points

*How users typically arrive at this node.*

- **"I keep failing at the same sin over and over"** — Owen's mortification is the answer; tracing to roots, not managing symptoms; Spirit-dependent engagement with the specific
- **"Why am I not growing?"** — the means of grace question; Willard on disciplines; passivity masquerading as trust
- **"I became a Christian but nothing changed"** — is conversion genuine (Faith node question) or is sanctification not being cultivated? Both are possible; emotional register check required
- **"How holy do I need to be?"** — the ceiling question; Wesley vs. Reformed on whether an upper limit exists this side of glorification
- **"Do I have to do something, or does the Spirit just do it?"** — Phil 2:12-13; effort and Spirit together; the false choice between striving and passivity
- **Scholars:** Third Use of the Law debate; Keswick vs. Reformed; definitive sanctification (Murray); spiritual disciplines and character formation

## Failure Modes

*Where thinking about sanctification characteristically goes wrong.*

- **Perfectionism** — setting an impossible standard that produces despair rather than aspiration; the goal becomes a weapon rather than a horizon
- **Passivity** — "let go and let God"; waiting for transformation without engaging the means the Spirit works through; misapplied justification logic
- **Legalism** — treating sanctification as performance for God's approval; re-introduces works as the ground of relationship; produces either pride (I'm succeeding) or despair (I'm failing)
- **Moralism** — behavior modification without heart transformation; sin is managed rather than killed; external compliance without the root being addressed
- **Privatization** — treating holiness as purely interior and individual; missing Bonhoeffer's insistence on its concrete social form; "spiritual" that costs nothing publicly
- **Comparison-based sanctification** — measuring progress against other believers rather than Christ; always produces either pride or despair; the standard is wrong
- **Confusing justification and sanctification** — treating each failure in sanctification as a threat to standing before God; re-introduces anxiety about justification by routing it through performance in sanctification
