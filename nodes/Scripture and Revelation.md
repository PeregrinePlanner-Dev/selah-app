# Node: Scripture & Revelation

**Weight:** The epistemological foundation of the entire system. Every other node draws from Scripture; the doctrine of Scripture determines how that drawing is done. Get this node wrong and the rest of the library warps — either toward a rationalism that treats Scripture as raw data to be managed, or toward an experience-ism that treats it as subordinate to what the Spirit is saying today. This node also determines how the TES bot engages Scripture: with what authority, read by what method, and interpreted within what framework.

**Connects to:** Theology Proper, Trinity, Holy Spirit, Christology, Covenant Theology, Anthropology, Miracles, Heresy & False Teachers & the Great Apostasy

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- Scripture is the inspired Word of God — God is its ultimate author, speaking through human authors in their own language, culture, and personality
- Scripture is inerrant — it does not affirm as true what is false; it is without error in all that it affirms
- Scripture is infallible — it is entirely trustworthy for the purposes for which God gave it; it will not mislead
- Scripture is sufficient — it contains everything necessary for faith and godly living; nothing essential to salvation or holiness is omitted
- Scripture is authoritative — it has the right to govern belief and practice; it stands over the church, not the reverse
- Scripture is clear (perspicuous) in its central teachings — the way of salvation and the essentials of Christian living are accessible to careful readers; this does not require that all passages are equally clear
- Scripture is the product of dual authorship — God is the primary author; the human author's personality, style, and circumstances are genuinely engaged; the human is not a passive dictation machine
- General revelation (creation, conscience, providence) gives real but limited knowledge of God; special revelation (Scripture, culminating in Christ) is necessary for saving knowledge

## Key Tensions

### 1. Inerrancy — What It Claims and What It Doesn't

The Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy (1978) is the most precise definition of the evangelical position. Its Article XII: "We deny that Biblical infallibility and inerrancy are limited to spiritual, religious, or redemptive themes, exclusive of assertions in the fields of history and science." Inerrancy is not limited to "spiritual matters" — it extends to all that Scripture affirms in any domain.

**What inerrancy claims:** Scripture does not err in what it *affirms*. This requires attention to genre, intent, and context. Ancient historical narrative does not claim the precision of modern historiography; phenomenological language ("the sun rose") is not scientific error; round numbers are not mistakes; the Gospels' use of OT quotations follows ancient citation practices that differ from modern footnoting. The *phenomena* of Scripture (what Scripture actually does) must govern the *theory* of inerrancy (what inspiration means), not the reverse.

**What inerrancy does not claim:** It does not claim that every English translation is inerrant; that all apparent contradictions are easily resolved; that ancient science and modern science will agree; or that every biblical statement has a simple literal meaning.

**The challenge from within:** Peter Enns (*Inspiration and Incarnation*, 2005) argued that the standard evangelical account of inerrancy does not adequately account for the human, culturally-embedded character of Scripture — parallels with ancient Near Eastern texts, inner-biblical tensions, the NT's use of the OT. Enns was asked to resign from Westminster Theological Seminary. The debate between Enns and his critics clarified what is at stake: can inerrancy account for the Bible's genuine humanity without retreating to a doctrine of Scripture that is less than fully divine?

**The functional inerrancy reality:** Many evangelical Christians hold inerrancy doctrinally but read Scripture in ways that implicitly compromise it — picking and choosing which texts apply, reading proof-texts without context, or treating it as advice rather than authority.

### 2. Infallibility vs. Inerrancy — Is There a Difference?

Some theologians (particularly in the Reformed tradition and some Anglican evangelicals) distinguish:

**Inerrancy** (stronger claim): Scripture contains no errors of any kind in what it affirms — historical, scientific, or theological.

**Infallibility** (narrower claim): Scripture will not fail in its purpose — it is entirely reliable as a guide to faith and practice — but may contain minor factual imprecisions in matters peripheral to that purpose (genealogical numbers, historical details).

**The evangelical debate:** Those who use "infallibility" without "inerrancy" are often accused of opening the door to reducing Scripture's authority in non-theological domains. Those who use "inerrancy" without sufficient nuance are accused of defending a caricature that doesn't match what Scripture actually does. The Chicago Statement attempted to establish "inerrancy" as the standard evangelical term with sufficient nuance to handle the phenomena of Scripture.

### 3. Hermeneutics — How Is Scripture Rightly Read?

Inspiration establishes what Scripture is; hermeneutics determines how it is read. The two questions are distinct and both matter.

**The literal sense and its limits:** The Reformation's *sensus literalis* (the literal/intended meaning) displaced the medieval fourfold sense (literal, allegorical, tropological, anagogical). Luther and Calvin insisted on reading Scripture in its natural, grammatical sense rather than spiritualizing everything. But "literal" does not mean "woodenly literal" — it means reading each passage according to its genre and intent: poetry as poetry, apocalyptic as apocalyptic, law as law, narrative as narrative.

**Redemptive-historical hermeneutics:** Scripture is a unified story with a telos — it moves from creation through fall, covenant, and redemption toward new creation, with Christ at the center. Reading any passage requires locating it in this arc and asking how it relates to Christ. This is the dominant hermeneutical approach in Reformed biblical theology (Geerhardus Vos, D.A. Carson, Graeme Goldsworthy).

**The application question:** The NT writers apply OT texts in ways that seem to violate their original context (Matthew's use of Hosea 11:1 in Matt 2:15; Paul's use of Deut 25:4 in 1 Cor 9:9). Understanding why they do this — and whether modern readers can do the same — is a live hermeneutical question.

**Cultural relativity:** Which biblical commands are timeless and which are culturally specific? How does the reader distinguish? The trajectory from the household codes (Eph 5-6) to contemporary marriage and workplace practice requires a hermeneutic. Egalitarians and complementarians read the same texts through different hermeneutical frameworks and reach opposite conclusions on gender in church and home.

### 4. General vs. Special Revelation — What Can Nature Tell Us About God?

**General revelation** (available to all humanity through creation, conscience, and history) gives real knowledge of God — his power and divine nature are "clearly seen, being understood from what has been made" (Rom 1:20). The moral law is written on the human heart (Rom 2:15). This is why pagan philosophers can arrive at genuine moral insights and why natural theology (arguments for God's existence from reason) has a legitimate role.

**The limit of general revelation:** It is not saving knowledge. It establishes God's existence and moral character sufficient to leave humanity without excuse (Rom 1:20) but insufficient to reveal the gospel. Saving knowledge of God comes through special revelation — Scripture, and supremely through the Word made flesh, Jesus Christ.

**Natural theology's contested status:** Reformed theology (especially post-Barth) is suspicious of natural theology — the attempt to prove God's existence from reason and nature apart from Scripture. Barth's objection: natural theology places human reason in judgment over revelation and produces an idol rather than the living God. The Thomistic and Catholic tradition is more confident in natural theology as a legitimate pathway to God that is then supplemented and clarified by revelation.

### 5. Canon — Which Books Belong in Scripture?

Before Scripture can function with authority, a prior question must be settled: *which writings constitute Scripture?* The word "canon" (Greek *kanon*, measuring rod or rule) refers to the collection of books recognized as authoritative Scripture. The canon question is not abstract — it determines which text Christians read, cite, and submit to.

**How the OT canon was formed:** The Hebrew Bible (Tanakh) consists of 39 books (in Protestant counting) and its core was recognized as authoritative long before the NT era. The traditional claim that the Council of Jamnia (c. AD 90) fixed the Jewish canon is now doubted by most scholars; it was more a confirmation of existing practice than a decision. The criteria operative in the recognition of the OT canon included: prophetic origin, consistency with established Torah, and the testimony of the community. The books were *recognized* as having intrinsic authority, not *granted* authority by a council — this distinction matters theologically.

**The deuterocanonical books (Apocrypha):** Between the Protestant OT (39 books) and the Catholic OT (46 books) lie seven additional writings — Tobit, Judith, 1-2 Maccabees, Wisdom of Solomon, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), and Baruch — included in the Greek Septuagint (LXX) and in the Latin Vulgate. The Catholic Church formally defined these as canonical at the Council of Trent (1546). Protestants follow the shorter Hebrew canon; Luther placed the Apocrypha in a separate section — "useful to read but not to establish doctrine."

**How the NT canon was formed:** The NT canon was not decreed by a single council but emerged through a process of recognition over the 2nd-4th centuries. Criteria included: apostolicity (written by an apostle or someone in their immediate circle), orthodoxy (consistent with the rule of faith), catholicity (received across the broader church), and antiquity (demonstrably early origin). The Councils of Hippo (393) and Carthage (397) listed the 27-book NT as we have it — ratifying a consensus already operative in practice.

**The critical challenge:** Bart Ehrman and others argue that the canon is a product of the winning side of 2nd-3rd century theological controversies — that the "losers" (Gnostics, Marcionites) also had texts that were simply suppressed. This argument is historically overstated: the core of the NT was recognized before the major Gnostic controversies, and the Gnostic gospels were late compositions that failed the apostolicity and antiquity criteria.

**Did the church create the canon or recognize it?** The Catholic answer: the church, guided by the Spirit, determined which books are Scripture — therefore the church's authority underwrites the canon's authority. The Protestant answer (Luther, Calvin, Warfield): the church recognized what was already intrinsically Scripture; the canon's authority is self-attesting and validated by the Spirit's internal witness.

### 6. Historical-Critical Method — The Scholarly Challenge to Traditional Authorship

The historical-critical method applies the same methods used in secular historical study to the biblical text. It arose in the 18th-19th centuries as part of the Enlightenment's application of reason to all claims, including religious ones.

**Higher vs. lower criticism:** *Lower (textual) criticism* analyzes manuscript variants to establish the most reliable original text — theologically uncontroversial and essential to good scholarship (Metzger, Nestle-Aland). *Higher criticism* investigates authorship, dating, sources, and composition — this is where the controversy lies.

**The Documentary Hypothesis (JEDP):** Julius Wellhausen (1878) argued that the Pentateuch was not written by Moses but was a composite of four documents: J (Yahwist, 9th c. BC), E (Elohist, 8th c.), D (Deuteronomist, 7th c.), and P (Priestly, 6th-5th c.), edited together after the Babylonian exile. This hypothesis dominated critical scholarship through the 20th century and still shapes academic OT work, though it has been significantly revised and contested even within critical scholarship.

**The evangelical response:** K.A. Kitchen (*On the Reliability of the Old Testament*, 2003) marshals archaeological and ancient Near Eastern evidence to argue that Pentateuchal legal, narrative, and covenantal forms fit the 2nd millennium BC far better than Wellhausen's late dating. The key critique: the JEDP hypothesis began with the anti-supernaturalist assumption that predictive prophecy is impossible, so any passage predicting future events must have been written after the fact. Once you grant predictive prophecy as possible, the dating evidence shifts significantly.

**The NT equivalent — the Jesus Seminar and Ehrman:** The Jesus Seminar (founded 1985) concluded that about 18% of the sayings in the Gospels plausibly go back to the historical Jesus. Bart Ehrman's popular works (*Misquoting Jesus*, 2005; *Jesus, Interrupted*, 2009) argue that the NT text is unreliable and that Paul invented much of Christian theology. Evangelical responses from Craig Blomberg, D.A. Carson, and Daniel Wallace engage these arguments specifically: the manuscript tradition for the NT is exceptionally strong compared to any ancient document, and the alleged discrepancies are well within the range of normal ancient historical writing.

**The hermeneutical fork:** The historical-critical method as a *tool* — asking questions about genre, historical context, authorship — is legitimate and valuable; evangelical scholars use it. As an *ideology* — beginning with the assumption that miracles don't happen — it produces different conclusions because it begins with different presuppositions. The evangelical claim is not that critical questions are illegitimate, but that the anti-supernaturalist assumptions built into much critical scholarship foreclose conclusions before the evidence is examined.

## Scripture Anchors

- **2 Tim 3:16-17** — "all Scripture is God-breathed and is useful for teaching, rebuking, correcting and training in righteousness" — the inspiration text; *theopneustos* is a passive participle (breathed out by God); the sufficiency claim
- **2 Pet 1:20-21** — "prophets, though human, spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit" — dual authorship; the human speaks, the Spirit carries
- **Ps 119:105** — "your word is a lamp for my feet, a light on my path" — the functional sufficiency; Scripture as guidance for life
- **Isa 40:8** — "the word of our God endures forever" — the permanence and reliability of the Word
- **John 10:35** — "Scripture cannot be set aside" — Jesus's own view of Scripture's authority; he treats it as binding even in its details
- **John 17:17** — "your word is truth" — Scripture as truth, not merely containing truth
- **Matt 5:17-18** — "not the smallest letter, not the least stroke of a pen, will by any means disappear from the Law" — the detail-level authority of Scripture
- **Rom 1:18-20** — general revelation; God's eternal power and divine nature "have been clearly seen" in creation
- **Rom 15:4** — "everything that was written in the past was written to teach us" — the purposiveness of Scripture; it was written *for* us

## Theologian Map

**B.B. Warfield (1851-1921)**
Princeton Seminary's most influential theologian and the architect of the modern evangelical doctrine of inerrancy. *The Inspiration and Authority of the Bible* is the foundational text. Warfield's key move: full inspiration does not suppress or override the human authors but works through them so completely that every word they freely write is also exactly what God intends. The concursive theory of inspiration: God's sovereign providence worked in, through, and by means of the human authors' personalities, experiences, and abilities so that the result is simultaneously the human's own words and God's own words. Warfield's precision: *verbal* inspiration (the words, not just the ideas) and *plenary* inspiration (all of Scripture, not just the theologically important parts).

**Karl Barth (1886-1968)**
The most significant 20th century challenge to the Warfieldian position from within Protestant theology. Barth's threefold Word of God: the Word revealed (Jesus Christ), the Word written (Scripture), and the Word proclaimed (preaching). Scripture is not itself the Word of God — it *becomes* the Word of God in the event of encounter, when the Spirit uses it to bring the reader into contact with the living Christ. His rejection of inerrancy and his event-theology of revelation place him outside the evangelical consensus. His contribution: the living, dynamic character of Scripture as encounter, not merely information.

**Kevin Vanhoozer (1957-)**
The most sophisticated contemporary evangelical theologian of Scripture. *Is There a Meaning in This Text?* (1998) defends the possibility of authorial intent against deconstructionism. His model: Scripture as God's communicative action — God is the author who performs speech acts through the human authors, and the reader's task is to understand those intended acts. The Spirit's role in illumination is to enable the reader to understand what the divine author communicates, not to generate new meaning beyond it.

**Graeme Goldsworthy (1934-)**
The clearest expositor of the redemptive-historical hermeneutic in accessible form. *According to Plan* and *Preaching the Whole Bible as Christian Scripture* insist that every part of Scripture must be read in relation to its place in the redemptive-historical arc — creation, fall, Israel, Christ, church, new creation — and that Christ is the interpretive key to the whole. This prevents both moralistic readings of OT narrative and the abandonment of the OT as irrelevant to Christians.

**F.F. Bruce (1910-1990)**
The standard evangelical treatment of the canon. *The Canon of Scripture* (1988) traces the historical process by which both OT and NT books came to be recognized as authoritative, arguing that the church's role was *recognition* rather than *creation* — the books carried intrinsic authority that the community came to acknowledge. Bruce remains the first reference for canon questions at a serious level.

**Bruce Metzger (1914-2007)**
Princeton NT textual critic and the authority on both the NT canon (*The Canon of the New Testament*, 1987) and NT manuscript tradition (*The Text of the New Testament*, 1964). His textual work establishes the reliability of the NT transmission process — the manuscript evidence is vastly superior to any comparable ancient text. Significantly, Ehrman was Metzger's student, yet the two reached entirely different conclusions from the same evidence — Metzger found the textual tradition remarkably reliable; Ehrman found it undermined by variation.

**Julius Wellhausen (1844-1918)**
The architect of the Documentary Hypothesis. His *Prolegomena to the History of Israel* (1878) argued that the Pentateuch is a composite of four late sources (JEDP), with monotheism and elaborate Mosaic law emerging late in Israelite history — not at Sinai. Wellhausen must be understood to engage critical OT scholarship; his framework still shapes the presuppositions of much academic work.

**Bart Ehrman (1955-)**
The most widely-read popular-level critic of the NT's reliability. Former evangelical, now agnostic. *Misquoting Jesus* (2005) argues that the NT text has been significantly altered in transmission; *Jesus, Interrupted* (2009) argues the Gospels contradict one another. His work reaches enormous general audiences. The key counter-point: Ehrman's textual variants are real but overwhelmingly minor; no doctrine hangs on a disputed reading. Craig Blomberg, D.A. Carson, and Daniel Wallace have engaged his arguments specifically.

**K.A. Kitchen (1932-)**
Egyptologist and OT scholar whose *On the Reliability of the Old Testament* (2003) provides the most rigorous archaeological and ancient Near Eastern rebuttal of late-dating theories. Kitchen shows that Pentateuchal legal and covenantal forms fit 2nd millennium BC patterns precisely — not the patterns of the 1st millennium that Wellhausen's late dates require. His challenge to the Documentary Hypothesis is empirical, not confessional.

## Entry Points

- **"How do we know the Bible is true?"** — the authority question; the circular but not viciously circular nature of Scripture's self-attestation; the internal witness of the Spirit
- **"Doesn't the Bible have errors and contradictions?"** — the inerrancy challenge; specific alleged contradictions require specific engagement; the genre and intent framework
- **"The Bible was written by men — why should I trust it?"** — the human authorship question; 2 Pet 1:20-21; dual authorship as the answer
- **"How do I know how to interpret the Bible?"** — the hermeneutics entry; genre, context, redemptive-historical location
- **"Are all the Bible's commands still binding today?"** — the cultural-relativity question; how to distinguish timeless from time-bound
- **"Why are these 66 books in the Bible and not others?"** — the canon entry; criteria for recognition (apostolicity, orthodoxy, catholicity); the church recognized vs. created the canon
- **"What about the Gospel of Thomas / the books the church suppressed?"** — the Gnostic gospels and the Ehrman-style challenge; apostolicity and dating criteria; Bruce and Metzger
- **"Why do Catholics have more books in their Bible?"** — deuterocanonical/Apocrypha question; Council of Trent vs. Reformation
- **"Hasn't the Bible been changed over centuries?"** — textual transmission; Metzger vs. Ehrman; what textual variants actually affect
- **"Scholars say Moses didn't write the Pentateuch — is that true?"** — the JEDP Documentary Hypothesis; Wellhausen's presuppositions; Kitchen's archaeological counter-case
- **Scholars:** Warfield vs. Barth on inspiration; the Enns controversy; Vanhoozer's speech-act theory; natural theology debates; Metzger vs. Ehrman on textual reliability; Kitchen vs. Wellhausen on Pentateuchal authorship; F.F. Bruce on canon formation

## Failure Modes

- **Bibliolatry** — treating the text as an end in itself rather than as the vehicle through which the living God speaks; "the Bible says" as a conversation-stopper rather than a conversation-opener
- **Wooden literalism** — ignoring genre and treating all Scripture as flat, context-free instruction; leads to arbitrary application and missed meaning
- **Proof-texting** — extracting individual verses without context to support a predetermined conclusion; the most common abuse of Scripture in popular Christianity
- **Inspiration without illumination** — affirming that Scripture is inspired while ignoring the Spirit's necessary role in enabling understanding
- **Relevance-driven interpretation** — starting with what is culturally acceptable and working backward to find Scripture's support; the reader's preferences govern the text
- **Inerrancy as idol** — defending the doctrine of inerrancy more energetically than actually reading and obeying Scripture; winning the argument while losing the encounter
