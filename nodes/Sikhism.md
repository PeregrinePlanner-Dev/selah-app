# Sikhism (Interfaith Entry Point)

A smaller door, added 2026-07-13, flagged as a likely future need in the original interfaith file's own build notes. **Format:** What this person likely brings → Points of genuine contact → Points of sharpest tension → Relevant TES nodes → Tier 1 voices to route to → Register guidance.

### What this person likely brings

Founded by Guru Nanak (1469–1539) in Punjab; strict monotheism -- one formless, eternal God (*Ik Onkar*), beyond gender and form, known through devotion and meditation on the Divine Name (*Naam*); ten human Gurus, followed by the Guru Granth Sahib (the scripture itself) as the eternal, living Guru -- authority now rests entirely in the text, no further human gurus; strong emphasis on equality (rejection of caste), service (*seva* -- including *langar*, the free community kitchen open to all regardless of religion or caste), honest living, and constant remembrance of God; belief in karma and reincarnation, with liberation (*mukti*) achieved through divine grace together with disciplined meditation on the Name, ending the cycle of rebirth; the Khalsa identity (the Five Ks) for initiated Sikhs, marking a strong, visible communal identity; firm rejection of idol worship and empty ritualism in favor of inner devotion.

### Points of genuine contact

- **Strict monotheism** -- one, eternal, transcendent God -- has real resonance with Christian theism, more so than several traditions covered here.
- **A personal God who can be known through devotion**, not merely a philosophical abstraction, is closer to Christian theism than, for instance, Advaita Vedanta's impersonal Brahman.
- **Ethical seriousness** (service, honesty, generosity, rejection of caste) resonates with Christian neighbor-love and the *imago Dei*'s affirmation of equal human dignity.

### Points of sharpest tension

**No Incarnation.** God remains always formless and transcendent in Sikh theology -- there is no concept of God becoming human. This is the sharpest single divergence from Christianity's central claim that God has definitively entered creation, without ceasing to be God, in Jesus.

**The Guru Granth Sahib as living Guru vs. Scripture testifying to the living Word.** Sikh authority rests in the scripture itself as the final, complete, living Guru; Christian Scripture instead testifies to and points toward a person -- Christ himself as God's definitive self-revelation (Heb 1:1–3).

**Karma and reincarnation vs. atonement and resurrection.** One life, one death, one judgment (Heb 9:27) stands against the cycle of rebirth -- the same tension covered in the Hinduism and Buddhism doors.

**Synergistic liberation vs. monergistic grace.** Sikh *mukti* comes through grace together with one's own disciplined spiritual effort; Christian salvation is God's decisive, accomplished act in Christ, received by faith.

### Tier 1 Voices

Barth (Theology Proper and Christology -- the particularity and necessity of the Incarnation as God's specific self-disclosure, not one path among several); Athanasius (Christology -- why the Incarnation, not merely moral teaching or scripture, is necessary for salvation); Ladd (Eschatology -- resurrection vs. reincarnation, same routing logic as the Hinduism door)

### Register Guidance

- Recognize Sikhism's genuine ethical seriousness and strict monotheism -- this is not paganism or polytheism, and is closer common ground on the doctrine of God than several other traditions covered here
- Name the absence of an Incarnation concept clearly and respectfully as the central point of engagement: "Sikhism holds God as always beyond form; Christianity's most startling claim is that God, while remaining fully God, entered the world in a particular human life" -- frame as the entry point, not as a deficiency to correct combatively
