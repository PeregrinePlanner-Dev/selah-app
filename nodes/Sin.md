# Node: Sin (Hamartiology)

**Weight:** Foundation node — Grace only makes sense against it, and Atonement, Justification, and Sanctification all presuppose it. The depth of a user's understanding of sin directly determines the depth of their understanding of salvation.

**Connects to:** Grace, Atonement, Anthropology, Justification, Sanctification, Sovereignty & Free Will, Suffering & Providence

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- Sin is both a condition (inherited fallen nature) and acts (personal transgressions) — the condition precedes and produces the acts
- Sin is universal — no human being is exempt; "all have sinned" is not hyperbole (Rom 3:23)
- Sin is fundamentally relational — it is first an offense against God, not merely a moral or behavioral failure
- Sin has consequences: spiritual death, separation from God, disordered relationships, distorted conscience
- Sin is not merely weakness, ignorance, or immaturity — it carries genuine moral accountability
- Original sin corrupts the whole person — will, intellect, and affections — not only behavior
- Sin does not erase the image of God in humanity, but it distorts it at every level

## Key Tensions

*These have occupied serious theologians for centuries. None resolves cleanly.*

### 1. Original Sin vs. Personal Responsibility

If sin is inherited (Augustine's reading of Rom 5:12 — "through one man sin entered the world"), in what sense am I responsible for a condition I was born into? Justice seems to require that guilt attach only to what I freely choose. Scripture seems to say I am already implicated in Adam's choice before I make any choice of my own.

**Western (Augustinian/Reformed) position:** Both the corruption of nature *and* the guilt of Adam's sin are inherited. I am not merely born into a broken environment — I am born guilty. The solidarity between Adam and his descendants is real and juridical, not merely biological or environmental. Paul's Adam/Christ parallel in Romans 5 depends on this: just as condemnation comes through Adam's one act to all who did not personally commit it, so justification comes through Christ's one act to all who did not personally earn it.

**Eastern Orthodox position:** Inherited mortality and corruption, yes — but not inherited guilt. I suffer the consequences of Adam's fall (mortality, disordered passions, vulnerability to sin) without being personally guilty for his act. Sin's transmission is about damaged nature, not imputed guilt.

**Pelagian error (rejected at Carthage, 418):** Adam's sin is a bad example, not a transmitted condition. Each person sins freely and by imitation. This was condemned because it makes grace merely instructional — helpful but not necessary — and evacuates the depth of human need.

**The unresolved edge:** How guilt is transmitted through generation is never fully explained in Scripture. The juridical reality (condemnation comes through Adam) is clearer than the mechanism.

### 2. Total Depravity vs. Human Dignity

Reformed theology's "total depravity" is consistently misread as "humans are as evil as possible." The actual claim is structural: every faculty — will, intellect, affections — is corrupted by the fall. No part of the person escapes. The mind suppresses truth (Rom 1:18). The will is enslaved to self and sin and cannot turn toward God unaided. The affections are disordered toward created things over the Creator.

Yet humans retain the image of God (Gen 1:26–27). They produce genuine beauty, exercise real moral restraint, love their children, seek justice. Common grace explains this — God's sustaining and restraining work prevents total depravity from producing total destruction. But the tension remains: if every faculty is corrupted, how reliable is the conscience? How trustworthy is human moral intuition? How much weight can natural theology carry?

**The pastoral version of this tension:** Users who feel they are "basically good people" are not wrong that something good remains. But they are wrong that it constitutes a standing before God. The image persists; the relationship is broken.

### 3. Sin as Condition vs. Sin as Act

The distinction between original sin (the inherited condition) and actual sin (personal transgressions) raises the question of their relationship: does the condition cause the acts inevitably, or does it create a bent toward sin that humans then freely follow?

**The Augustinian/Reformed answer leans toward inevitability:** the will cannot choose good in relation to God without grace; sinful acts flow necessarily from sinful nature. This preserves the depth of the human problem but raises questions about culpability — can I be held responsible for what my nature inevitably produces?

**The Wesleyan answer:** prevenient grace restores sufficient capacity so that the bent toward sin does not become an iron necessity. The sinner is genuinely accountable because the response to grace is genuinely possible. Total depravity is the condition prevenient grace addresses — not a permanent ceiling on human responsiveness.

**Romans 7 as a case study:** "I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate" (v.15). Whether Paul is describing pre- or post-conversion experience is one of the most debated questions in Pauline scholarship. Either way, the text describes sin as a *power* operating in the person — not merely a series of bad decisions, but something that has its own momentum in the human person.

### 4. Individual Sin vs. Corporate Sin

Western Christianity — especially in its Protestant expressions — has tended to individualize sin: my transgressions, my guilt, my need for forgiveness. Scripture is considerably broader. The Hebrew prophets address the sin of nations (Amos 1–2), economic exploitation as collective wickedness (Amos 4:1–3; Isa 5:8–23), and structural injustice as genuinely sinful, not merely unfortunate. The New Testament adds "the world" (kosmos in John's usage) as a system organized in opposition to God — not merely a collection of individual sinners, but a social order with its own logic and momentum.

The tension: can a person be personally righteous within a sinful system? Can individual repentance address corporate sin? Does salvation address structural evil, or only personal guilt?

This tension is often avoided by theologically conservative traditions (focusing on personal sin) and by progressive traditions (focusing on structural sin while softening personal accountability). The more honest position is that both are real and neither exhausts what Scripture means by sin.

## Scripture Anchors

*Key passages with a note on what they carry in this debate.*

- **Gen 3** — the fall narrative; sin as relational rupture before it is behavioral violation; "you will be like God" (v.5) as the root — the desire for autonomy from God; note that the consequences are relational (shame, hiding, blame-shifting) before they are punitive
- **Gen 1:26–27** — image of God; what sin distorts but cannot erase; the basis for human dignity even within a doctrine of radical corruption
- **Ps 51:5** — "in sin did my mother conceive me"; the condition from origin; David's confession reaches behind his specific sin to its source
- **Isa 53:6** — "each of us has turned to our own way"; the directional nature of sin as a self-ward turn, not merely a series of infractions
- **Jer 17:9** — "the heart is deceitful above all things"; the internal corruption of affection and self-knowledge; the problem of self-assessment
- **Rom 1:18–32** — sin as suppression of known truth; the progressive darkening of the mind; sin as an epistemological problem, not only a moral one
- **Rom 3:23** — universality stated flatly; the "falling short of the glory of God" language points to sin as a failure of purpose and direction, not only transgression
- **Rom 5:12–19** — the Adam/Christ parallel; original sin's juridical framework; condemnation through one, justification through One
- **Rom 7:14–25** — sin as power and inner conflict; the war within the person; Paul's "wretched man" passage
- **James 1:14–15** — desire → sin → death; the mechanics of individual temptation and culpability; sin's interior origin

## Theologian Map

*Substantive positions — enough to engage seriously from the first exchange.*

**Augustine (354–430)**
The architect of Western hamartiology. Two key moves: (1) Sin as *privatio boni* — not a substance but a privation, a corruption of something good. Evil has no independent existence; sin is the distortion of what God made good. (2) Sin as disordered love (*amor sui* over *amor Dei*) — the will that has turned from God toward self as its final end. This disordered love is inherited through generation; Augustine reads Romans 5:12 as teaching that all humanity sinned *in* Adam, not merely *like* Adam. The result: the will is captive after the fall, unable to turn toward God without grace — which makes grace not an aid but a liberation. Everything in Western soteriology is a response to this framework.

**John Calvin (1509–1564)**
Inherits and systematizes Augustine. Total depravity as a claim about comprehensiveness, not extremity — every faculty is affected, but this does not produce the worst possible humans, because common grace restrains the full expression of fallen nature. Calvin's precision matters: he distinguishes between the ability to do civic good (which fallen humans retain) and the ability to do spiritual good in relation to God (which they entirely lack). The unregenerate mind can produce genuine philosophy, art, and moral restraint — and Calvin calls these genuine gifts — but cannot produce genuine faith or turning toward God. The image of God persists in its structural form; its directional orientation is reversed.

**John Wesley (1703–1791)**
Affirms original sin as strongly as Calvin — this is often missed by those who read Wesley as soft on human nature. Wesley's Articles of Religion (Article VII) are unambiguous: original sin is the corruption of the nature of every man, and this corruption inclines toward evil. What Wesley adds is prevenient grace as the universal response to this corruption: God does not leave fallen humanity in bare total depravity but immediately extends restoring grace to all, making genuine response possible. Sin's depth requires grace's priority; but prevenient grace means total depravity does not result in a population incapable of responding to God.

**C.S. Lewis (1898–1963)**
Not a systematic theologian but one of the most penetrating analysts of sin's phenomenology. In *Mere Christianity*, Lewis identifies pride as the Great Sin — the root from which all others grow — and the one we are least able to see in ourselves because it is the very faculty by which we assess ourselves. "It is the comparison that makes you proud: the pleasure of being above the rest." In *The Screwtape Letters*, Lewis dramatizes how sin operates not through dramatic temptation but through the slow habituation of small self-indulgences that gradually reorient the self. His contribution to TES: sin is not primarily about the big obvious transgressions; it is about the fundamental direction of the self. Useful for users who do not feel particularly sinful.

**Reinhold Niebuhr (1892–1971)**
Brings the corporate and structural dimension that classical Reformed hamartiology often underemphasizes. Sin for Niebuhr is the attempt to escape human finitude — to secure the self against anxiety and vulnerability. This plays out individually as pride and sensuality; it plays out collectively as nations, ideologies, and economic systems that absolutize themselves and claim ultimate loyalty. *Moral Man and Immoral Society* (1932): individuals can achieve moral behavior that groups systematically cannot, because groups have interests that override the moral intuitions of their members. This is essential for users who engage questions of structural evil, social justice, and the sinfulness of systems — not only persons.

## Entry Points

*How users typically arrive at this node. Start from where they actually are.*

- **"I don't feel that sinful"** — the self-assessment problem; Jeremiah 17:9 and Lewis on pride as the lens we see through, not the thing we see
- **"Why do I keep doing what I know is wrong?"** — Romans 7 experience; sin as power not only poor choice; needs the condition/act distinction
- **Suffering and evil** — "if God is good, why is there so much wrong in the world?"; sin is the theological answer to evil, but don't move there too fast if the person is in personal suffering
- **Guilt and shame** — the felt weight of specific wrongs; emotional register check required; this is personal before it is doctrinal
- **Social justice concerns** — structural and systemic evil; often the entry point for younger or culturally engaged users; Niebuhr is the bridge
- **"What even makes something a sin?"** — the authority question; who defines it and why; epistemological before soteriological

## Failure Modes

*Where thinking about sin characteristically goes wrong. Useful for Bias & Failure Interruption.*

- **Minimization** — "I'm not that bad"; comparison to worse sinners; the standard being used is other humans, not the glory of God
- **Moralism** — reducing sin to a list of prohibited behaviors; misses the condition of the heart behind the acts; produces rule-keeping without transformation
- **Shame spiral** — sin as permanent identity; "I am what I've done"; collapses under the weight of grace rather than meeting it
- **Dualism** — locating sin in the body or material world; gnostic instinct; Scripture locates sin in the will, not in flesh per se
- **Fatalism** — "I can't help it, I was born this way"; misreads total depravity as total determinism; removes accountability while retaining its consequences
- **Individualism** — seeing only personal sin; unable to account for structural evil, corporate guilt, or the systemic dimensions Scripture addresses
- **Therapeutic displacement** — reframing sin as woundedness, dysfunction, or illness; not wrong that these are real — but they are not the same thing, and conflating them evacuates moral accountability
