# Node: Theology Proper (The Nature & Attributes of God)

**Weight:** Who God is determines everything else. If God's nature is misunderstood, every downstream doctrine distorts — the atonement depends on what divine justice is; prayer depends on what divine knowledge is; providence depends on what divine power is. This node is also where classical Christian theism (the dominant tradition from the early church through the Reformers) is most directly challenged by contemporary evangelical alternatives — open theism, relational theology, and process theology. The God of classical theism and the God of popular evangelical devotion are not always the same.

**Connects to:** Trinity, Christology, Creation, Sovereignty & Free Will, Prayer, Suffering & Providence, Problem of Evil, Miracles

---

## Doctrinal Frame

*What must hold within orthodox, conservative theology.*

- God is infinite — unlimited in all his perfections; he is not subject to the constraints that bind finite creatures
- God is self-existent (*aseity*) — he depends on nothing outside himself for his existence; he is the uncaused cause
- God is eternal — without beginning or end; he does not experience time as a succession of moments in the same way creatures do
- God is omniscient — he knows all things, including future free actions; his knowledge is not derived from observation but is constitutive of reality
- God is omnipotent — he is able to do all things that are consistent with his nature; he cannot do what is logically contradictory (create a square circle) or morally incompatible with his holiness
- God is omnipresent — he is fully present everywhere; this is not spatial extension but the immediacy of his relationship to all reality
- God is holy — absolutely pure and set apart from all moral corruption; his holiness is not merely an ethical quality but an ontological one
- God is love — his very nature is self-giving, other-directed; love is not merely what God does but what God is (1 John 4:8, 16)
- God is immutable — he does not change in his essential nature, character, or purposes; he is not subject to internal development or external alteration

## Key Tensions

### 1. Classical Theism vs. Open Theism — Does God Know the Future?

**Classical theism:** God's knowledge is eternal, complete, and simple — he knows all events, including all future free human choices, from eternity. This knowledge is not "foreknowledge" in the sequential sense (God waiting to see what will happen) but simple knowledge — God knows all of time from the standpoint of eternity. His knowledge does not determine human choices; it comprehends them.

**Open theism** (Clark Pinnock, Greg Boyd, John Sanders): God voluntarily limits his foreknowledge of free human choices in order to make creaturely freedom genuine. God's "foreknowledge" of free acts is an incoherent concept — if God knows what you will freely choose, your choice is not genuinely free. Open theism resolves this by having God know everything that is logically knowable, but holding that future free choices are not yet determinate and therefore not knowable even by God. God is maximally competent within the constraints of a genuinely open future.

**The biblical data:** Open theists cite texts where God appears to change his mind (Gen 6:6 — "the Lord regretted making humans"), ask questions ("Where are you?" — Gen 3:9), or respond to prayer in a way that suggests the future is not fixed. Classical theists read these as *anthropopathisms* — God accommodating himself to human ways of speaking — and cite texts that explicitly assert God's complete foreknowledge (Isa 46:10 — "I make known the end from the beginning"; Acts 2:23 — the cross was according to God's "deliberate plan and foreknowledge").

**Why the stakes are high:** Open theism was condemned by the Evangelical Theological Society in 2001 after a formal inquiry. The concern: if God does not know the future, the reliability of prophecy is undermined, providence becomes reactive rather than sovereign, and the assurance of salvation (which depends on God's certain completion of his purposes) is qualified.

### 2. Divine Impassibility — Can God Suffer?

**Classical impassibility:** God does not experience passions or emotional reactions in the way creatures do. He is not "moved" by external events in a way that affects his being. This is not coldness or indifference but a claim about divine transcendence: God's blessedness is not dependent on the created order; he is not subject to involuntary emotional states.

**The relational challenge:** Does a God who cannot suffer genuinely love? Does impassibility produce a God who is philosophically tidy but pastorally remote — unable to be moved by human suffering and therefore unable to be a genuine partner in relationship? The text that most directly challenges impassibility: Isaiah 63:9 — "in all their distress he too was distressed." And the cross: can the eternal Son genuinely suffer if impassibility is true?

**The qualified position:** Most contemporary theologians who hold to classical theism have moved to a qualified impassibility — God is not impassible in the sense of being emotionally indifferent or unmoved, but he is not passible in the sense of having involuntary emotional states caused by creaturely actions. His love, grief, and anger are real, but they are expressions of his free, perfect nature rather than responses that alter his being. The incarnation is the decisive moment: the eternal Son, in taking on human nature, genuinely entered into suffering and grief — but this suffering belongs to the incarnate one, not to the divine nature as such.

### 3. Divine Simplicity — Is God Composed of Parts?

**Divine simplicity** is the classical doctrine that God is not composed of parts — not of essence + existence, not of substance + attributes. God's power is not a property God has; it is what God is. God's love is not one attribute among others; it is identical with his being. This means that all of God's attributes are identical with each other and with his essence — God's love is his justice is his power is his knowledge, all viewed from different angles.

**Why it matters:** Simplicity prevents reducing God to a set of competing attributes that must be balanced against each other — as if God's love and justice pull in different directions and the atonement is about resolving the tension. There is no tension in God; his justice and love are the same reality. It also grounds God's immutability and aseity — a composed being could be disassembled or altered.

**The challenge:** Simplicity is difficult to state without seeming to collapse all distinctions within God. If God's love is identical with his wrath, how are they genuinely different? If the three persons of the Trinity each are identical with the one divine essence, how are they genuinely distinct? The tradition has answers — but they require care.

### 4. The Wrath of God — Attribute or Attitude?

In contemporary preaching and worship culture, divine wrath has largely disappeared — God is presented as loving, patient, and forgiving, with wrath appearing rarely and awkwardly. The biblical witness is considerably less tidy.

**Wrath as divine attribute:** The OT and NT both attribute wrath to God as a consistent, settled response to sin — not a loss of temper but a holy recoil from what is morally corrupt. Romans 1:18 — "the wrath of God is being revealed from heaven against all the godlessness and wickedness of people." This is present tense and ongoing, not merely eschatological. Paul's logic in Romans 1–3 requires that divine wrath is real and present before the atonement can be understood as a genuine solution.

**The Marcionite temptation:** The instinct to confine wrath to the OT "God of wrath" and grace to the NT "God of love" is ancient and recurring. Marcion (2nd century) resolved the tension by positing two different gods. Orthodox theology insists that the one God is both perfectly holy (therefore wrathful toward sin) and perfectly loving (therefore atoning for sin) — and that neither attribute cancels the other. The atonement is precisely where both are fully expressed simultaneously.

**Wrath as impersonal process vs. personal response:** Some theologians (C.H. Dodd, early 20th century) argued that Paul's "wrath" is not a personal attitude of God but an impersonal process — the built-in consequences of sin playing out in the moral order. This removes the personal and relational dimension of divine response to sin. Leon Morris's careful exegesis in *The Apostolic Preaching of the Cross* demonstrated that the biblical language is consistently personal.

## Scripture Anchors

- **Exod 3:14** — "I AM WHO I AM" — divine aseity; the name that defines God as the self-existent one
- **Ps 90:2** — "before the mountains were born... from everlasting to everlasting you are God" — divine eternity
- **Isa 40:28** — "the Lord is the everlasting God, the Creator of the ends of the earth. He will not grow tired or weary, and his understanding no one can fathom" — immutability, omniscience, infinite power
- **Isa 46:9–10** — "I am God, and there is no other... I make known the end from the beginning, from ancient times, what is still to come" — exhaustive foreknowledge
- **Mal 3:6** — "I the Lord do not change" — divine immutability as the ground of covenant faithfulness
- **1 John 4:8, 16** — "God is love" — not merely that God loves but that love is his nature; the being of God as self-giving
- **Rom 1:18** — "the wrath of God is being revealed from heaven" — wrath as present, active, and real
- **Ps 139:7–10** — divine omnipresence; "where can I go from your Spirit?"
- **Heb 4:13** — "nothing in all creation is hidden from God's sight" — omniscience comprehensive
- **Jas 1:17** — "the Father of the heavenly lights, who does not change like shifting shadows" — immutability without rigidity

## Theologian Map

**Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)**
The most systematic treatment of the divine attributes in the Western tradition. *Summa Theologiae* Part I, Questions 2–26 work through God's existence, simplicity, perfection, goodness, infinity, immutability, eternity, unity, and knowledge with extraordinary care. Aquinas's method: via negativa (we know God primarily by what he is not — not finite, not composite, not temporal) supplemented by analogical language (when we say God is "good," we use the term analogically — God is good in a way that surpasses and differs from creaturely goodness). His God is the God of classical theism: simple, impassible, eternal, and entirely self-sufficient. The God of Aquinas and the God of popular evangelical devotion are not always recognizably the same.

**John Calvin (1509–1564)**
The Reformation's most careful theologian of the divine attributes. The *Institutes* open with the observation that knowledge of God and knowledge of self are intertwined — you cannot know God truly without seeing yourself truly, and vice versa. Calvin's God is majestic, sovereign, and holy — the emphasis is on divine transcendence and sovereignty that contemporary evangelical culture sometimes mutes. Calvin's treatment of accommodation: God speaks in Scripture in ways that are accommodated to human capacity (baby talk, in Calvin's striking metaphor) — anthropomorphic language about God changing his mind or experiencing emotions is accommodation to human understanding, not literal description of divine psychology.

**Stephen Charnock (1628–1680)**
*The Existence and Attributes of God* — the most thorough Puritan treatment of the divine attributes, still widely read. Charnock's method: each attribute is treated systematically with extensive Scripture, theological precision, and pastoral application. His treatment of divine eternity, immutability, omniscience, and holiness are classics. His distinctive contribution is the devotional weight he gives to each attribute — knowing that God is omniscient is not merely a theological datum but a claim that transforms how one lives before God.

**Clark Pinnock (1937–2010)**
The most significant evangelical open theist and the target of the ETS investigation. *Most Moved Mover* (2001) argued that classical theism's God (simple, impassible, eternal, omniscient of the future) is more the product of Greek philosophy than of biblical revelation. The God of Scripture is dynamic, relational, responsive — genuinely moved by creaturely love and suffering. Pinnock's contribution: he forced evangelical theology to engage the question of whether the classical synthesis is genuinely biblical or a philosophical imposition. His critics argue he overcorrected — preserving the relational God at the cost of divine sovereignty, omniscience, and the reliability of promise.

## Entry Points

- **"Does God actually care what happens to me?"** — the relational entry; impassibility and divine love in tension; the incarnation as the answer
- **"If God knows everything, why do things go so wrong?"** — omniscience and providence; not every event is God's will; divine permission vs. divine intention
- **"Is God angry with me?"** — the wrath entry; wrath and love as two expressions of the same holy nature; not the same question as personal condemnation
- **"Does God change his mind when I pray?"** — divine immutability and intercessory prayer; Mal 3:6 and Gen 6:6 in tension
- **"What is God actually like — not what I was told, but really?"** — the attributes as a doorway; usually leads to discovering the God is bigger, stranger, and more beautiful than assumed
- **Scholars:** Open theism debate; divine simplicity and its problems; impassibility and the cross; Aquinas vs. relational theology; the wrath of God in Pauline theology

## Failure Modes

- **Therapeutic theism** — God as supportive life-coach; his primary attribute is niceness; wrath, holiness, and judgment disappear; the result is a God who cannot save because he has nothing to save from
- **Deism** — God as original cause who has since withdrawn; prayer is futile; providence is impersonal; the universe runs on its own
- **Anthropomorphism run wild** — taking all accommodation language literally; a God who "changes his mind" in the same sense a human does; a God who "learns" and "discovers"
- **Attribute competition** — framing God's love and justice as competing priorities that the atonement "balances"; divine simplicity rules this out
- **Philosophical God** — the God of the philosophers (pure actuality, unmoved mover) with no personal character; abstractly perfect but unrelatable and unsaving
- **Sentimental love** — "God is love" meaning God approves of everyone and everything; evacuates the holiness and the atonement simultaneously
