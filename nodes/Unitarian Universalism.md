# Unitarian Universalism (Interfaith Entry Point)

A smaller door, added 2026-07-13. **Format:** What this person likely brings → Points of genuine contact → Points of sharpest tension → Relevant TES nodes → Tier 1 voices to route to → Register guidance.

### What this person likely brings

Roots in two distinct 18th/19th century American movements -- Unitarianism (explicit denial of the Trinity: God is one person, not three) and Universalism (universal salvation: all will ultimately be saved, no eternal punishment) -- merged in 1961 into the Unitarian Universalist Association. Contemporary UU has moved further still: many congregations today are explicitly non-creedal and pluralistic, welcoming humanists, atheists, pagans, Buddhists, and Christians alike under a shared covenant of ethical principles rather than shared doctrinal belief; where theistic, Jesus is typically viewed as a great moral teacher and exemplar, not as God incarnate; strong emphasis on individual conscience, reason, and the "free and responsible search for truth and meaning" rather than any fixed creed; deep communal commitment to social justice; worship often draws on readings from many religious and secular sources rather than centering Scripture.

### Points of genuine contact

- **Genuine moral seriousness** around justice, human dignity, and compassion -- values Christianity holds too, even though grounded differently.
- **Respect for Jesus as a moral teacher** is a real starting point, even though the Christian claim goes much further.
- **A commitment to honest, reasoned inquiry** rather than blind assent resonates with a Christian intellectual tradition that also takes reason seriously, even if not as the final authority.

### Points of sharpest tension

**The Trinity and the deity of Christ.** The foundational Unitarian denial is the sharpest, most direct point of difference -- and in some ways goes further than Mormonism or Jehovah's Witnesses, since there is often no claim that Jesus was any kind of preexistent divine being at all, simply an exceptional human teacher.

**No fixed authoritative revelation.** Individual conscience and pluralistic sourcing stand close to the opposite of how Christianity understands revelation as something *given*, not merely constructed by the community's ongoing search.

**Universal salvation vs. final judgment.** UU's inherited universalism contrasts with the historic Christian teaching that takes final judgment seriously, even while affirming God's mercy and desire that none perish.

### Tier 1 Voices

Athanasius (Christology -- the case against Arianism applies directly, and more so, against a purely human-teacher Christology); Barth (Trinity and revelation -- God's self-disclosure as given, not humanly constructed); Lewis (the Trilemma -- *Mere Christianity*'s "Lord, liar, or lunatic" argument directly engages "great teacher but not God," which is often the UU's own explicit starting position)

### Register Guidance

- This is a different starting point than the Secular/Atheist door -- many UU members retain real spiritual and religious practice and language, just without traditional theistic content; don't collapse the two
- The Lewis Trilemma is unusually well-suited here, since "Jesus was a great teacher" is often exactly this person's own stated position, making the argument's force direct rather than needing translation
